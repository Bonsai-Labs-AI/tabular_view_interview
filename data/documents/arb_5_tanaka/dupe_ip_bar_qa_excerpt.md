# "I Try Not to Be the Most Expensive Person in the Room": A Q&A with Dr. Yuki Tanaka

*From* The Licensing Executive *(the quarterly magazine of the Licensing Executives Society International).*

*Volume 58, Issue 1, Spring 2024.*

*Q&A conducted by Aarti Subramaniam, LES-USA & Canada Trustee, in Tokyo on January 26, 2024.*

*Edited for length.*

---

## On a bicoastal practice

**LES: Your practice is more or less evenly divided between the United States and Japan. How did you end up with that footprint?**

Tanaka: Partly by design, mostly by accident.

I grew up in Yokohama.

I did my LL.B. at the University of Tokyo.

I assumed I was going to do corporate work in Marunouchi for the rest of my career.

Then I went to Stanford on an LL.M. and I sort of fell into the patent world and never really climbed out.

I went back to Tokyo after the doctorate to work at a U.S. firm, and I ended up doing a great deal of licensing for Japanese electronics manufacturers who were selling into the American market.

The licenses generated the disputes.

So eventually I was on a plane to San Francisco every six weeks.

That has continued for fifteen years.

## On Japan vs. the U.S.

**LES: You hold panel appointments at the JCAA, the ICDR, and SIAC. Have you observed real differences in how parties from the two sides of the Pacific approach IP arbitration?**

Tanaka: Yes — but the gap is closing.

Japanese parties were, historically, very hesitant to put a technical expert on the stand.

There was a cultural preference for written submissions and for tribunal-appointed neutrals over party-appointed ones.

American parties, of course, want full Daubert-style cross.

What I see now is a real convergence.

The Japanese in-house community has become much more comfortable with party-appointed experts.

And American counsel have learned, sometimes the hard way, that a four-day cross-examination of a Japanese inventor through a court interpreter is rarely the tactic that wins them the case.

## On caseload

**LES: How many matters do you carry at a typical moment?**

Tanaka: It varies.

But on a typical day I have somewhere around eight active matters where I am the sole or co-arbitrator, plus a couple of expert determinations.

Eight is about the upper bound for me if I want to remain the kind of arbitrator who actually reads the exhibits.

I have colleagues who carry twenty.

I don't know how they do it, and I don't think the parties get my best work if I try.

## On current matters

**LES: Can you discuss any current matters?**

Tanaka: Not specifically — confidentiality forbids it.

I can say in general that the matter that has taught me the most in the past year was a FRAND rate-setting in the 4G/5G area.

Three weeks of hearings.

Eleven testifying experts.

Two working languages.

By the end I knew more about chipset bill-of-materials accounting than I had ever expected to.

There was also a JCAA cross-license matter in the semiconductor wafer fabrication space last year that turned on a quite interesting question about whether system-on-chip products fell within the agreed definition of "licensed products."

I issued a partial award on that issue and I think it's a question we'll see again.

## On fees

**LES: Are your fees a barrier for smaller parties?**

Tanaka: I try not to be the most expensive person in the room.

My standard hourly is U.S.$1,250, which I think places me in the middle of the market for arbitrators with my profile.

For JCAA-administered matters the institution sets a fee schedule that parties find predictable.

Which I think is one of JCAA's underrated virtues.

I will sometimes cap my fees on smaller matters — under, say, ¥500 million in dispute — because I think access to qualified neutrals is a matter of basic legitimacy for the system.

The system stops working if only the very large parties can afford it.

## On teaching

**LES: You teach at Keio Law School. Given your docket, why?**

Tanaka: Because the patent litigators of twenty years from now will be the people writing the awards then.

And at the moment there is essentially no Japanese law school course that takes semiconductor licensing seriously as a body of substantive law.

Also, frankly, the students keep me honest.

A bright 2L will ask you why an SEP holder is entitled to any royalty at all, and you have to be able to give a real answer.

If you can't, that is a useful diagnostic.

## On career advice

**LES: What advice would you give a young IP lawyer who wants to do international arbitration?**

Tanaka: Three things.

### First: learn the technology

Learn the technology.

Not at the level of a Ph.D. — you can't, and you don't need to — but well enough that you can read a claim chart without flinching.

And well enough that when an expert is being evasive you can tell.

The lawyers in this practice who are the most effective are the ones whom the engineers respect.

### Second: learn a language

Learn one foreign language well.

It doesn't have to be Japanese — Mandarin, Korean, German all work — but pick one and become genuinely fluent.

The market for fully bilingual IP arbitration counsel is structurally underserved.

### Third: build a reputation for fairness

Build a real reputation for fairness early.

The IP arbitration bar is small.

People remember.

The associate who two-faced a tribunal at year three will not be appointed as an arbitrator at year fifteen.

## On writing

**LES: You write extensively. What's on the desk now?**

Tanaka: I am working on a second edition of the patent disputes book.

The first edition came out in 2019 and it is well out of date, particularly on the Chinese and UPC developments.

I also have a slow-moving article on the question of whether a tribunal can or should set a global rate for a portfolio of standard-essential patents when the parties have agreed only to arbitrate a single national dispute.

The English approach is one answer.

The European institutional approach is another.

I am not sure either is fully satisfying.

Eventually I will figure out what I think and write it down.

## On the future

**LES: Where do you see the field in ten years?**

Tanaka: I think there will be fewer pure-play patent litigations and substantially more arbitrations.

I think the institutional market is going to consolidate around three or four serious venues — Singapore, Geneva, London, and either Tokyo or Hong Kong, depending on geopolitics.

I think the AI cases that are starting to arrive now are going to absorb more of the practice's bandwidth than anyone is currently planning for.

And I think there is going to be a generational turnover in the neutrals pool that the institutions are not yet adequately preparing for.

The senior people are getting older.

There are not enough mid-career arbitrators with the right combination of technical fluency and procedural experience.

## On travel

**LES: Less travel?**

Tanaka: Less travel, ideally.

More writing.

The docket — the docket finds you anyway.

*He is a member of the JCAA, ICDR, SIAC, and HKIAC panels and lectures part-time at Keio University Law School.*
