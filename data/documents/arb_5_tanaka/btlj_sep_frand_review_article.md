# From *Microsoft v. Motorola* to the UPC: A Decade and a Half of FRAND Adjudication

## Caroline E. Whitford & Anjali R. Krishnaswamy

*Berkeley Technology Law Journal, Volume 41, Issue 2 (Spring 2026), pp. 411–502.*

*Lead article. Cite as: 41 Berkeley Tech. L.J. 411 (2026).*

---

## Abstract

Fifteen years after Judge James Robart's decision in *Microsoft Corp. v. Motorola, Inc.*, the global jurisprudence of fair, reasonable, and non-discriminatory (FRAND) licensing has matured into a recognizable body of law — but a fractured one.

United States courts have largely ceded the rate-setting space to private arbitration.

The English Patents Court, after *Unwired Planet*, has positioned itself as a willing global rate-setter, a posture that has drawn parties and provoked others.

The new Unified Patent Court, since its operational launch in June 2023, has issued its first SEP-relevant decisions and signaled an approach that is neither wholly *Huawei v. ZTE* nor wholly *Unwired Planet*.

Chinese courts, particularly the Supreme People's Court and the Wuhan Intermediate People's Court, have moved aggressively to assert global rate-setting jurisdiction of their own, leading to a now-familiar pattern of anti-suit and anti-anti-suit injunctions.

This Article surveys the doctrinal evolution of FRAND adjudication across these jurisdictions from 2010 to early 2026, identifies three persistent doctrinal fault-lines, and proposes a framework for evaluating the legitimacy of cross-border SEP rate-setting that we call the "comity-plus-competence" model.

We argue that the existing institutional landscape is unstable, that the instability is structural rather than merely jurisdictional, and that a partial harmonization through soft-law instruments at the IEEE, ETSI, and ITU level is more feasible than the treaty-based solutions sometimes proposed in the literature.

## I. Introduction

In April 2013, Judge James Robart of the United States District Court for the Western District of Washington issued a 207-page opinion in *Microsoft Corp. v. Motorola, Inc.* setting a FRAND royalty rate for Motorola's standard-essential patents covering the H.264 video codec and the 802.11 wireless standard.

The decision was, at the time, widely understood as a watershed.

An Article III judge had, for the first time, sat as a private rate-setter and produced a multi-hundred-page methodology for valuing a portfolio of SEPs.

It was also, in retrospect, an outlier.

The American judicial appetite for FRAND rate-setting peaked with *Motorola* and declined steadily thereafter.

As of January 2026, exactly two reported U.S. district-court decisions have set FRAND rates on the merits since 2018.

The space has been ceded to private arbitration, ITC Section 337 proceedings (with their own peculiar doctrines about whether an SEP holder can pursue an exclusion order), and the occasional preliminary skirmish over anti-suit relief.

Europe, by contrast, has become the center of gravity.

The Court of Justice of the European Union's decision in *Huawei Technologies Co. Ltd. v. ZTE Corp.* established a procedural framework — the so-called "Huawei dance" — that, while criticized for its formalism, has nonetheless become the operative test for whether an SEP holder may seek injunctive relief.

The English High Court and Court of Appeal, beginning with Mr. Justice Birss's 2017 judgment in *Unwired Planet International Ltd. v. Huawei Technologies Co. Ltd.* and continuing through the Supreme Court's affirmance in 2020, have positioned the English courts as willing global rate-setters.

The Unified Patent Court, operational only since June 2023, has issued perhaps a dozen SEP-relevant decisions, the most consequential of which — the Local Division Munich's January 2025 decision in *Panasonic v. Xiaomi* — is now on appeal to the Court of Appeal in Luxembourg.

This Article does three things.

Part II provides a brief account of the pre-*Motorola* landscape, situating the FRAND debate in the longer history of SEP commitments at the standards bodies.

Parts III through VII trace the doctrinal evolution of the major jurisdictions.

Part VIII identifies three persistent doctrinal fault-lines.

(a) The proper baseline for the "non-discrimination" prong of FRAND.

(b) The legitimacy of a single national court setting a global rate.

(c) The relationship between FRAND determinations in court and in arbitration.

Part IX proposes our comity-plus-competence framework, and Part X concludes.

## II. The Pre-*Motorola* Landscape

The phrase "fair, reasonable, and non-discriminatory" appears in the patent-licensing policies of the major standards-development organizations — IEEE, ETSI, ITU, IETF — in slightly different formulations dating to the 1990s.

The IEEE-SA Standards Board adopted its first patent policy in 1995, with significant revisions in 2007, 2015, and 2022.

The ETSI IPR Policy, finalized in 1994 and amended substantially in 2013 and 2022, contains the most-litigated FRAND commitment in the world.

The ITU-T's Common Patent Policy, jointly maintained with the ITU-R, ISO, and IEC, dates to 2007.

What none of these documents do, and what their drafters consciously chose not to do, is define "fair," "reasonable," or "non-discriminatory."

The policy choice was to leave the meaning of the commitment to bilateral negotiation between SEP holders and implementers, with the implicit assumption that disputes would be resolved either privately or in national courts under contract or competition law.

For the first fifteen years of this regime — roughly 1995 to 2010 — the assumption held.

Litigation over the meaning of FRAND commitments was scarce.

The major SEP holders (Nokia, Ericsson, Qualcomm, Motorola, InterDigital, Philips) negotiated bilateral cross-licenses with the major implementers (the same companies, plus the consumer electronics OEMs), and disputes were resolved in the shadow of a credible threat of injunctive relief.

That equilibrium broke down in the late 2000s for reasons that have been extensively chronicled.

The rise of smartphone competition.

The emergence of significant Chinese implementers.

The growth of patent assertion entities.

And — most importantly — the recognition by several SEP holders that injunctive relief against major implementers could extract significantly above-FRAND rates.

The result was a wave of litigation across multiple jurisdictions beginning roughly in 2010 and continuing through the present.

## III. *Microsoft v. Motorola* and the American Decline

Judge Robart's *Motorola* opinion is, in 2026, principally of historical interest.

The opinion's substantive methodology — a modified Georgia-Pacific analysis combined with a comparable-license benchmarking exercise — has been repeatedly invoked in U.S. arbitration awards but rarely applied by another U.S. court.

A LexisNexis search conducted by the authors on March 10, 2026 returned 47 U.S. district-court opinions citing *Microsoft v. Motorola* substantively.

Of these, only four engaged with the methodology in any depth.

None applied it to set a rate on the merits.

The reasons for the American decline are, in our view, threefold.

### Reason one: regulatory retreat

The Federal Trade Commission and the Department of Justice Antitrust Division, after a period of aggressive enforcement under the Obama administration, walked back the most assertive readings of the SEP-holder's antitrust exposure during the first Trump administration and have not fully restored them.

### Reason two: IPR alternatives

The Patent Trial and Appeal Board's *inter partes* review proceedings, established by the America Invents Act in 2011, have provided implementers with an efficient mechanism to challenge SEP validity, reducing the strategic value of FRAND counterclaims.

### Reason three: arbitration

Most importantly, parties have voted with their feet for arbitration.

Major SEP licensing programs — Avanci's automotive 4G program (launched 2017) and 5G program (launched 2022), the Sisvel 5G multimode pool, the Via Licensing 4G/5G pool — now include arbitration provisions or have produced arbitration-driven dispute resolution patterns.

## IV. *Huawei v. ZTE* and the European Procedural Turn

The CJEU's decision in *Huawei v. ZTE* (Case C-170/13, July 16, 2015) was, in some respects, anti-climactic.

The Court declined to articulate substantive standards for FRAND rates.

It instead set forth a procedural framework: the SEP holder must, before seeking injunctive relief, notify the implementer of the alleged infringement, make a written FRAND offer, and consider in good faith any counter-offer.

The implementer, correspondingly, must not delay and must, if it rejects the offer, post security.

The framework has been criticized for its formalism.

It has been applied unevenly across the EU national courts.

The German courts in particular have developed a substantial body of case law interpreting each step.

The Mannheim, Munich, and Düsseldorf district courts have produced what is now hundreds of pages of doctrine on what a sufficient *Huawei v. ZTE* notice looks like.

The German Federal Court of Justice's decisions in *Sisvel v. Haier I* (KZR 36/17, May 5, 2020) and *Sisvel v. Haier II* (KZR 35/17, November 24, 2020) substantially clarified the German understanding.

The framework remains the dominant European procedural test.

## V. *Unwired Planet*: The English Court as Global Rate-Setter

[Subsequent sections continue with detailed doctrinal analysis. Cases discussed include *Unwired Planet v. Huawei*, *Conversant v. Huawei*, *Apple v. Optis* (2022 EWCA Civ 1411), *InterDigital v. Lenovo*, *Nokia v. OPPO* (German FCJ KZR 35/21), the Wuhan *Xiaomi v. InterDigital* anti-suit proceedings, the Munich Higher Regional Court's anti-anti-suit doctrine, and the UPC Local Division Munich's *Panasonic v. Xiaomi* and *Huawei v. Netgear* decisions.]

The English approach has, over the past decade, become known shorthand as "the global FRAND posture."

It was articulated definitively by the UK Supreme Court in *Unwired Planet International Ltd. v. Huawei Technologies Co. Ltd.* [2020] UKSC 37.

The Court held that an English court could, consistent with the ETSI IPR Policy and the parties' contractual undertakings, set a global FRAND rate for a portfolio of SEPs.

And that the implementer's refusal to accept that rate could properly result in a UK injunction.

The decision drew criticism from the Chinese Ministry of Commerce.

And cautious admiration from the German bar.

It has been the single most-cited FRAND decision worldwide in the years since.

## VI. Chinese Courts and the Race to Jurisdiction

The Chinese courts' assertion of global rate-setting jurisdiction, beginning with the Wuhan Intermediate People's Court's anti-suit injunction in *Xiaomi v. InterDigital* (2020) and continuing through several subsequent decisions of the Supreme People's Court, has substantially altered the landscape.

Combined with the German courts' development of anti-anti-suit doctrine, the result has been a series of jurisdictional contests that increase the costs and risks of multi-forum FRAND litigation for both SEP holders and implementers.

## VII. The UPC's First SEP Decisions

The Unified Patent Court became operational on June 1, 2023.

Its first SEP-relevant decision of consequence was the Local Division Munich's January 17, 2025 judgment in *Panasonic v. Xiaomi* (UPC_CFI_223/2023).

The decision adopted a *Huawei v. ZTE*-derived framework but added several distinctive features.

Including a willingness to consider rate questions on a portfolio rather than individual-patent basis.

And an openness to confidentiality protections that German national courts have historically resisted.

The decision is on appeal to the UPC Court of Appeal in Luxembourg.

A decision is expected in late 2026 or early 2027.

## VIII. Three Doctrinal Fault-Lines

We identify three persistent doctrinal fault-lines that continue to generate inconsistent outcomes across jurisdictions.

### A. The Non-Discrimination Baseline

The "ND" in FRAND has been the subject of recurring confusion.

Three approaches exist in the case law.

(1) The "hard ND" view, which requires that similarly situated licensees receive identical rates.

(2) The "soft ND" view, which permits differentiation based on legitimate commercial considerations (volume, product category, vertical integration).

(3) The "no-ND" view, exemplified by the English approach in *Unwired Planet*, which treats non-discrimination as effectively subsumed by the FR prongs.

The German Federal Court of Justice's 2020 decision in *Sisvel v. Haier II* (KZR 36/17) endorsed something close to a soft-ND view.

The English Supreme Court in *Unwired Planet* endorsed the no-ND view explicitly.

Chinese courts have not yet produced an authoritative articulation.

The UPC has not yet addressed the question.

### B. Single-Forum Global Rate-Setting

The English courts' willingness to set global rates — and to condition the avoidance of a UK injunction on the implementer's acceptance of those rates — is the single most contested feature of contemporary FRAND practice.

Critics, including the Chinese Ministry of Commerce in a 2021 public statement, have argued that this is an extra-territorial assertion of jurisdiction that violates principles of comity.

We take no position on the legitimacy of the English approach as a matter of public international law.

We do argue, in Part IX, that the global rate-setting practice creates instability that is unlikely to be cured by anti-suit and anti-anti-suit injunctions alone.

### C. Court vs. Arbitration

The relationship between judicially set rates and arbitrally set rates is undertheorized.

American practice has effectively delegated rate-setting to arbitration.

European practice has not.

Asian practice is mixed.

The result is that the same SEP holder can face, simultaneously, an English court rate determination, a Chinese court rate determination, and a private arbitration in Singapore or Hong Kong, with no clear hierarchy of precedence.

## IX. The Comity-Plus-Competence Framework

Section IX develops the authors' proposed framework over approximately 22 pages, with reference to the literature on transnational commercial law, the *Hague Choice of Court Convention*, and the doctrine of forum non conveniens.

The core of the framework is two propositions.

First, that the legitimacy of a forum's FRAND determination should depend in part on whether that forum has the institutional competence (technical expertise, procedural tools, neutrality vis-a-vis the parties) to produce a reliable result.

Second, that comity considerations — the recognition that other forums may have stronger competence or stronger ties to the dispute — should constrain a forum's willingness to make global determinations even when its formal jurisdiction permits.

## X. Conclusion

The FRAND adjudication landscape in 2026 is the product of fifteen years of doctrinal evolution that has been, in the main, ad hoc, jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction, and reactive.

The institutional incentives now point in different directions in different forums.

The result is not chaos — sophisticated parties have learned to navigate the multi-forum landscape — but it is also not stability.

We suspect that the next decade will bring further consolidation around the European and Chinese forums.

Continued American withdrawal from judicial rate-setting.

An ongoing tension between the UPC and the English courts as the principal European venues.

The role of arbitration will grow.

The role of standards-body soft law may, finally, grow with it.
