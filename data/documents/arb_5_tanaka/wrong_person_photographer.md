# The Cranes That Stayed: Yuki Tanaka and the Photographic Witness of Hokkaido's Vanishing Winter

*A monograph essay by Helena Brandt.*

*Originally published in* Aperture *No. 254 (Winter 2024), pp. 88–117.*

*Reproduced here with permission. Copyright 2024 Helena Brandt and the Aperture Foundation.*

---

## I. The thing that matters

Yuki Tanaka does not photograph the red-crowned crane the way other photographers photograph the red-crowned crane.

This is the first thing to understand.

It is the only thing that matters.

For more than two decades, the wetlands of eastern Hokkaido — Kushiro, Tsurui, Akan — have been one of the most heavily photographed wildlife sites on earth.

The cranes (tancho, in Japanese) are spectacular.

A meter and a half tall.

White bodies.

Black necks.

A crimson crown that in flight is almost unreasonably beautiful against the blue-shadowed snow.

They dance.

They are monogamous, theatrical, and large.

There are now perhaps 1,900 of them in Hokkaido, up from a population reduced by 1924 to fewer than two dozen birds.

Photographers from across the world arrive in January and February to stand behind tripods at the well-known feeding stations and produce images that, taken together, would fill a hangar.

Tanaka's photographs are not those images.

## II. What he does not shoot

He does not shoot at the feeding stations.

He does not shoot the dance.

He does not shoot the famous mid-air territorial display that any tour group can witness from the wooden viewing platform at Tsurui Ito Sanctuary on any clear morning between 8 and 10 a.m.

## III. What he does shoot

He shoots, instead, what happens at the edges.

A single crane standing alone in a fog so thick the bird is more rumor than presence.

The black silhouette of a parent and chick crossing a frozen river in late afternoon, the ice the same color as the sky, the birds the only thing in the frame that is not a horizontal line.

A pair asleep, necks tucked, on a snowbank where the wind has carved a row of small parallel ridges that look like the strokes of a brush.

And — most famously — the photograph that appeared on the cover of *National Geographic*'s January 2022 issue.

A single crane in flight at dusk.

Low over the pack ice off the Notsuke Peninsula.

The bird almost entirely in silhouette except for a single point of red at the crown that catches the last light.

That photograph took him eleven winters to make.

## IV. Biography

Tanaka was born in Abashiri, on the northeast coast of Hokkaido, in 1976.

His father was a commercial fisherman who worked the Sea of Okhotsk in spring and summer and repaired boats in winter.

His mother taught primary-school art.

Tanaka has said, in the few interviews he has given, that he learned to photograph by sitting on the rocks at the edge of the harbor with a Pentax K1000 his uncle had given him for his twelfth birthday, "waiting for the light to do something interesting, which in Abashiri it does roughly four hours per day, six months per year."

He studied at Tokyo University of the Arts.

Graphic design, not photography — he never had formal training in photography.

He worked for ten years as an art director at a Tokyo advertising agency, photographing only on weekends and during his annual two-week home visit to Hokkaido.

He sold his first wildlife photograph, of a Steller's sea eagle on pack ice, to a Japanese conservation magazine in 2004 for the equivalent of about $80.

He left advertising in 2009 and has worked full-time as a photographer since.

## V. The household

He lives now in a small rented house outside Tsurui, in the central Kushiro wetlands.

With his wife Mari, a textile artist.

And a fifteen-year-old shiba inu named Kuro.

He spends roughly nine months of the year photographing.

Six of those nine in Hokkaido.

The other three on extended assignments that have, over the years, taken him to the Arctic National Wildlife Refuge, the Antarctic Peninsula, the South Georgia archipelago, Wrangel Island, and — for a single difficult expedition in 2018 — to the eastern edge of the Patagonian ice field.

## VI. What he does not do

He does not have a website.

He does not have an Instagram.

His agent, the small Tokyo-based agency Kogetsu, fields all inquiries.

He has refused, politely, every invitation to a workshop, a residency, or a judging panel that has come his way since approximately 2017.

He gives, on average, one interview per year.

## VII. Method

His method is, by his own description, almost embarrassingly simple.

He scouts a location on foot, often for weeks before he attempts to photograph there.

He carries a single camera body.

A Nikon D850, which he has used since 2017 and refuses to replace.

Two lenses.

A 70-200mm f/2.8 and a 500mm f/4.

A small carbon tripod.

A thermos of barley tea.

He does not use camera traps.

He does not use drones.

He does not use blinds, except occasionally a piece of white cloth he drapes over himself for one or two hours at a time.

He does not bait.

He shoots, by his own count, between 200 and 400 frames per day.

## VIII. The famous frame

The famous *National Geographic* cover image was the 91st frame of a session that began at 3:47 p.m. and ended at 4:18 p.m.

On the evening of February 19, 2021.

In temperatures of approximately minus 18 Celsius.

With a wind off the Sea of Okhotsk that he later described as "the only condition under which a person can keep a 500mm lens absolutely still — when you cannot feel your hands at all, you also cannot tremble."

## IX. The work

Tanaka's published work to date consists of three monographs, all from the small Tokyo publisher Iwanami Shashin.

*Tancho* (2013), 64 plates.

*Ice and Wing* (2018), 88 plates, with text by the ornithologist Saito Kenji.

*The Long Wait* (2023), 112 plates, with an introduction by the writer Pico Iyer that has been widely anthologized.

He has been the subject of two *National Geographic* features (March 2019; January 2022).

A *New York Times* Sunday magazine profile (December 2020).

A 47-minute NHK documentary (2022) that follows him across a single winter and contains, in its first 12 minutes, almost no dialogue.

His prints, when they are made available, sell through a small Kyoto gallery called Kissa for between ¥800,000 and ¥3.2 million.

He releases editions of seven.

## X. On the question of identity

Because Tanaka shares his name with several other public figures, he is occasionally misidentified.

Most prominently with an arbitrator who works on patent disputes between American and Japanese companies.

And with a chef whose Berkeley restaurant has recently received considerable press.

He has been asked, in the past five years, whether he is a lawyer.

He is not.

Whether he has a doctorate.

He does not — he holds only a Bachelor of Fine Arts.

Whether he has any opinion at all about the international framework for standard-essential patents.

He laughed, when an interviewer asked him this in 2022, and said: "I have an opinion about the framework for standing in deep snow for nine hours, which is what I have spent the last twenty years learning. The other things are not my business."

## XI. The meeting

His agent has politely asked, on several occasions, that the photographer not be confused with the arbitrator.

The two have, in fact, met.

Once.

At a fundraiser at the Japan Society in San Francisco in 2019, where they were seated at adjoining tables.

The photographer reported that they spent five minutes establishing that they were unrelated.

And then ten minutes discussing red-crowned cranes, about which the arbitrator turned out to know a surprising amount.

## XII. On the future of the work

Tanaka has said, recently, that he is approaching the end of the crane project.

The book *The Long Wait* was conceived, he has said, as a kind of farewell.

Not to the birds, whose population is now relatively stable.

But to a particular Hokkaido winter that, as anyone who has photographed there for two decades will tell you, no longer exists.

The sea ice off Notsuke now arrives later and departs earlier than it did when he began.

The Kushiro wetlands, which froze reliably by mid-December in the early 2000s, sometimes do not freeze fully until late January now.

The cranes, which evolved for a specific kind of winter, are encountering a different one.

## XIII. The record

What this means for the photographs, he says, is that the photographs are no longer about the birds.

"They are about a season that is being lost, and a place that is changing under the birds' feet," he told NHK in 2022.

"The birds will adapt or they will not. The photographs are the record of what was."

## XIV. What is next

He has hinted at a next project.

He will not say what it is.

His agent has confirmed that a fourth monograph is "in early development" and is "not about cranes."
