# Stanford Law School Announces New Center for IP Innovation and Policy, Names Inaugural Faculty Directors

*Stanford Law School, Office of Communications.*

*Press release. For immediate release: October 22, 2025.*

*Reference: SLS-PR-2025-0094.*

---

## Lead

Stanford, California — Stanford Law School today announced the launch of the Stanford Center for IP Innovation and Policy (CIPIP).

The Center is a new research center that will serve as the school's principal hub for interdisciplinary scholarship and policy engagement on intellectual property law in an era of rapid technological change.

The Center will be co-directed by Professor Mark Lemley, the William H. Neukom Professor of Law, and Professor Lisa Larrimore Ouellette, the Deane F. Johnson Professor of Law.

An initial gift of $42 million from the Hoffman-Yee Family Foundation funds the launch.

---

## Mission

The Center for IP Innovation and Policy will conduct empirical and doctrinal research on intellectual property law.

It will host visiting scholars and practitioners from around the world.

And engage directly with policymakers in the United States and internationally.

Its initial research agenda will focus on five priority areas.

### Priority area 1: AI and IP

The interaction between artificial intelligence and the existing IP framework.

Including questions of training-data licensing, output attribution, model patenting, and the application of fair use doctrine to model training.

### Priority area 2: Empirical patent enforcement

The empirical study of patent enforcement and licensing.

Building on the work of the Stanford NPE Litigation Database.

### Priority area 3: Standards and FRAND

The economic and legal evaluation of standard-setting and the FRAND regime.

With particular attention to telecommunications and automotive standards.

### Priority area 4: Pharma and biotech

Pharmaceutical and biotechnology patent policy.

Including the operation of the Hatch-Waxman framework and the Biologics Price Competition and Innovation Act.

And the interaction between patent term and FDA exclusivities.

### Priority area 5: Trade secrets

Trade secret law in the era of remote work and global supply chains.

## From the Dean

"This is a moment of unusual ferment in IP law," said Dean Jenny S. Martinez.

"Generative AI alone has produced a set of legal questions that the existing doctrine was not designed to answer."

"The new Center will give Stanford a permanent institutional home for the kind of rigorous, interdisciplinary work that this moment demands."

## Faculty leadership

The Center's two co-directors are well known within the IP scholarly community.

### Professor Mark A. Lemley

Lemley has been a member of the Stanford Law faculty since 2004.

Having previously taught at Berkeley Law and the University of Texas.

He is the author or co-author of seven books and more than 200 articles.

He is among the most-cited IP scholars in the world.

His recent work has focused on patent reform, antitrust treatment of platforms, and the legal status of generative-AI training.

He is the founding director of the Stanford Program in Law, Science and Technology.

He has served on the advisory committees of multiple federal agencies.

### Professor Lisa Larrimore Ouellette

Ouellette joined the Stanford Law faculty in 2014, having previously been a research fellow at Yale.

Her work, frequently empirical, has addressed the role of patent disclosure, the design of innovation prizes and other non-patent incentives, and the empirical assessment of patent quality.

She holds a Ph.D. in physics from Cornell as well as a J.D. from Yale.

An unusual combination that has informed her work on patent disclosure and enablement doctrine.

She co-edits the *Written Description* blog with Professors Camilla Hrdy, Jason Rantanen, and Sarah Burstein.

### Fellows and visiting scholars

The Center will also be supported by approximately 24 fellows, post-doctoral researchers, and visiting scholars in any given year.

Selected through an open application process administered by the Center's Executive Director (a search for whom is currently under way).

## The Hoffman-Yee gift

The lead gift of $42 million from the Hoffman-Yee Family Foundation will fund the Center's operations for an initial period of fifteen years.

The Hoffman-Yee Family Foundation, established by Reid Hoffman and Michelle Yee in 2014, supports research and education at the intersection of technology, law, and policy.

"We are at a hinge moment," said Reid Hoffman, in remarks at the Center's launch event on October 21.

"The IP framework that emerged from the twentieth century is being asked to do work it was not designed for."

"The Center will help us think carefully about which parts of that framework still serve us and which parts need to change."

## Additional support

## Programming

The Center will inaugurate its programming with a public symposium on November 13–14, 2025, on "Generative AI and the Foundations of IP."

Speakers will include faculty from Stanford, Berkeley, Harvard, Chicago, NYU, Oxford, the Max Planck Institute for Innovation and Competition, and Hitotsubashi University.

Current and former officials from the USPTO and the European Patent Office.

And counsel from a number of companies engaged in current AI-IP litigation.

Registration is open at https://law.stanford.edu/cipip/symposium-2025.

## Recurring programming

### Monthly IP Policy Workshop

A monthly workshop, modeled on the Stanford Law and Economics Workshop, at which faculty and visitors will present works-in-progress.

### Annual CIPIP Conference

An annual conference each spring, bringing together approximately 80 scholars, practitioners, and policymakers for two days of presentations and discussion.

### Practitioner Fellows program

A program that will bring two to four senior practitioners per year to Stanford for one-quarter residencies focused on policy-relevant writing.

### Visiting International Scholars program

A program that will host four to six scholars from outside the United States per year.

## Engagement with policymakers

A distinguishing feature of the Center will be its formal engagement with policymakers.

The Center will produce, on an ongoing basis, technical reports and policy briefs aimed at congressional staff, executive-branch agencies, and foreign counterparts.

Lemley and Ouellette have committed to producing not less than four substantial policy products per year during the Center's first five years.

The Center will not engage in litigation or amicus practice in its institutional capacity.

Faculty are, of course, free to engage individually as they have always done.

## Location and facilities

The Center will be housed on the fourth floor of the William H. Neukom Building.

In space previously occupied by the Stanford Program in Law, Science and Technology (which is being reorganized into the Center).

The space includes ten faculty offices.

Sixteen workstations for fellows and visiting scholars.

A 48-person seminar room.

And a small library focused on IP scholarship.

Construction modifications to the space, funded by a portion of the Hoffman-Yee gift, are expected to be completed by January 2026.

In the interim, the Center will operate out of temporary space in the Crown Quadrangle.

## Governance

The Center will be governed by a Faculty Steering Committee.

Chaired by the co-directors and including six other Stanford Law faculty and two ex officio members (the Dean and the Vice Dean for Research).

An External Advisory Board will be appointed in early 2026.

It will include scholars, practitioners, and policymakers from outside Stanford.

The Center will follow standard Stanford policies on conflicts of interest, donor relationships, and academic independence.

Donors do not direct research and have no role in faculty selection.

## A note on confused identities

The Communications Office has received several inquiries asking whether the Center will be led by, or will affiliate with, Dr. Yuki Tanaka.

Dr. Tanaka is an arbitrator who completed his Ph.D. at Stanford Law in 2009 and whose work in international IP dispute resolution is well-known.

Dr. Tanaka is not affiliated with the Center.

He has been informed of the launch as a courtesy and has, we understand, sent his congratulations.

The Center has not extended an invitation to him to join.

He has not requested one.

We regret any confusion.
