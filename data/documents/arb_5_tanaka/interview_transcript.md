# "The Pacific is Narrower Than It Used to Be": A Conversation with Dr. Yuki Tanaka

*Published in the* Global Arbitration Review *Spotlight Series, 6 February 2024. Interview conducted by Priya Raman in Tokyo, 19 January 2024. Lightly edited for length and clarity.*

---

**GAR: You've built a practice that is almost evenly split between the U.S. and Japan. How did that happen?**

Tanaka: A little bit by design and a lot by accident. I grew up in Yokohama, did my LL.B. at Todai, and I always thought I would do corporate work in Tokyo. Then I went to Stanford for the LL.M. and I just — I never quite left the patent world. After the PhD I went back to Tokyo for a U.S. firm and ended up doing IP licensing for Japanese electronics companies selling into the U.S. market. The disputes followed the licenses. So now I am on a plane to SFO basically every six weeks.

**GAR: You sit on the JCAA, ICDR, and SIAC panels. Do you see real differences in how parties from the two sides of the Pacific approach IP arbitration?**

Tanaka: Yes, but the gap is narrowing. Japanese parties used to be very reluctant to put technical experts on the stand — there was a cultural preference for written submissions and tribunal-appointed neutrals. U.S. parties of course want full Daubert-style cross. What I see now is convergence: Japanese in-house teams are much more comfortable with party-appointed experts, and U.S. counsel have learned that a four-day cross-examination of a Japanese inventor through interpretation is not always their best tactic.

**GAR: Roughly how many matters are you handling at any one time?**

Tanaka: It varies. On a typical day I have around eight active matters where I'm sole or co-arbitrator, plus a couple of expert determinations. Eight is about my ceiling if I want to be the kind of arbitrator who actually reads the exhibits.

**GAR: Can you talk about any of them?**

Tanaka: Not specifically. I can say the recent matter that has taught me the most was a FRAND rate-setting in the 4G/5G space — three weeks of hearings, eleven experts, two languages. By the end I knew more about chipset bill-of-materials accounting than I ever wanted to. There was also a JCAA cross-license dispute in the semiconductor wafer space last year that involved a very interesting question about whether system-on-chip products fell within the licensed-products definition. I issued a partial award on that.

**GAR: Are your fees a barrier for smaller parties?**

Tanaka: I try not to be the most expensive person in the room. My standard hourly rate is U.S.$1,250, which I think is in the middle of the market for arbitrators with my profile. For JCAA-administered cases the institution sets a schedule that the parties find predictable. I will sometimes cap fees on smaller matters — under, say, ¥500 million in dispute — because I think access to high-quality neutrals matters.

**GAR: You teach at Keio. Why bother, given your docket?**

Tanaka: Because patent litigators in twenty years will be the ones writing these awards, and right now there is essentially no Japanese law school course that takes semiconductor licensing seriously. Also, the students keep me honest. A bright 2L will ask you why a SEP holder gets a royalty at all, and you have to be able to answer.

**GAR: What's next?**

Tanaka: Less travel, ideally. More writing. I'm working on a second edition of the patent disputes book. And the docket — the docket always finds you.

---

*Dr. Tanaka is an independent arbitrator based in Palo Alto and Tokyo.*
