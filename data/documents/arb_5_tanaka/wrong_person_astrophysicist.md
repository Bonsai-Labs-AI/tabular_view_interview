# Professor Yuki Tanaka Awarded 2025 Nishina Memorial Prize for Work on Early Galaxy Formation

*Press release, University of Tokyo Department of Physics.*

*March 14, 2025. Embargoed until 11:00 JST.*

*Reference: UTokyo-Phys-Press-2025-014.*

---

## Announcement

The University of Tokyo Department of Physics is pleased to announce that Professor Yuki Tanaka has been awarded the 2025 Nishina Memorial Prize.

Tanaka is a member of the Department of Astronomy and the Kavli Institute for the Physics and Mathematics of the Universe (Kavli IPMU).

The prize is awarded for his contributions to the study of galaxy formation in the early universe.

In particular, his use of the James Webb Space Telescope to characterize the rest-frame ultraviolet spectra of galaxies at redshifts greater than ten.

## About the prize

The Nishina Memorial Prize was established in 1955.

It honors Yoshio Nishina (1890–1951).

It is one of the most prestigious awards in Japanese physics.

Past recipients include twelve scientists who subsequently received the Nobel Prize in Physics.

## Biographical sketch

Professor Tanaka, 54, was born in Sendai.

He received his B.Sc. (1993) and Ph.D. (1998) from the University of Tokyo.

His doctoral dissertation, supervised by the late Professor Sadanori Okamura, characterized the morphology of galaxies in the Hubble Deep Field North using ground-based imaging and the then-new HST data.

He held postdoctoral positions at the Max Planck Institute for Astrophysics in Garching (1998–2001).

And the Space Telescope Science Institute in Baltimore (2001–2004).

Before joining the Subaru Telescope as a staff astronomer in Hawai'i (2004–2009).

He returned to Japan in 2009 as an associate professor at the National Astronomical Observatory of Japan (NAOJ).

He led the Hyper Suprime-Cam strategic survey program from 2014 to 2019.

He was appointed full professor at the University of Tokyo in 2018.

He joined Kavli IPMU as a principal investigator in 2020.

## Productivity

He has authored or co-authored more than 340 refereed publications.

His h-index is 72 (as of January 2025).

He has supervised eleven Ph.D. students to completion.

## Cited work

The Nishina Memorial Foundation cited three contributions in particular.

### Contribution 1: The HSC quasar survey

The Subaru Hyper Suprime-Cam survey of z > 6 quasars.

Tanaka led the team that, between 2014 and 2021, discovered approximately 230 quasars at redshifts greater than six in a 1,400 square-degree imaging survey.

Including the second-most-distant known quasar, HSC J2331+0034, at z = 7.31.

This work has constrained the bright end of the high-redshift quasar luminosity function.

And, by extension, the timing of the epoch of reionization.

### Contribution 2: The JADES Origins Field

The JADES Origins Field deep spectroscopic survey.

Tanaka is a co-principal investigator (with Daniel Eisenstein of the Center for Astrophysics) on the JWST Advanced Deep Extragalactic Survey.

Which between 2022 and 2024 obtained NIRSpec spectra of more than 5,000 galaxies at z > 5.

He led the team that confirmed the spectroscopic redshift of JADES-GS-z13-1 at z = 13.2 in October 2023.

Which at the time was the most distant spectroscopically confirmed galaxy.

### Contribution 3: Population III stars

Theoretical work on Population III star formation.

In collaboration with Naoki Yoshida (University of Tokyo) and Volker Bromm (UT Austin).

Tanaka has produced a series of papers between 2016 and 2024 modeling the formation, lifetime, and chemical signatures of the first generation of stars.

The most-cited of these, "On the Initial Mass Function of Population III Stars in Lyman-Werner-Suppressed Halos" (Tanaka, Yoshida, & Bromm 2019, ApJ 885, 142), has been cited more than 410 times.

## Public engagement

Tanaka has been an active public communicator of science, particularly in Japan.

He has appeared on NHK's *Cosmic Front* program seven times since 2015.

He hosted the NHK Educational documentary *The First Light* (2023, 90 minutes).

He authored two trade books.

*Looking Back in Time* (Iwanami Shinsho, 2020, 248 pages, sold approximately 92,000 copies).

*The Universe in the First Billion Years* (Kodansha Bluebacks, 2024, 312 pages).

He is a frequent contributor to the magazine *Newton*.

He writes a monthly column on cosmology for the *Asahi Shimbun* Sunday science page.

## On confusion with other public figures

Tanaka has commented, in interviews and on his personal website (https://tanaka-cosmology.phys.s.u-tokyo.ac.jp), that he is occasionally confused with several other public figures who share his name.

Including a U.S.-based arbitrator who practices in intellectual property disputes.

A wildlife photographer based in Hokkaido.

And most recently a chef whose Berkeley restaurant has received favorable press.

He notes drily on his FAQ page: "I am none of these people."

"The arbitrator and I were briefly mistakenly invoiced for each other's hotel rooms at a Tokyo conference in 2021."

"We resolved this amicably over coffee."

## A further clarification

He has further clarified, in response to a 2023 email from a journalism student who was researching the wrong Tanaka entirely.

"I have no expertise in standard-essential patents, FRAND licensing, or any other matter of intellectual property law."

"I have not eaten at the Berkeley restaurant."

"I do photograph the sky, but not professionally and not very well."

## Award details

The Nishina Memorial Prize carries a cash award of ¥1,000,000 and a commemorative medal.

The award ceremony will be held at the Nishina Memorial Foundation in Bunkyo-ku, Tokyo, on December 6, 2025.

The prize lecture, "Reaching for the First Light: A Personal Account of JWST's First Three Years," will be delivered on the same date.

It is open to the public.

Registration opens November 1, 2025.

## Comments from colleagues

### Yoshida

"Yuki has been the central figure in Japanese extragalactic astronomy for the past fifteen years," said Professor Naoki Yoshida, director of Kavli IPMU.

"His combination of observational leadership and theoretical insight is unusual."

"The JADES-GS-z13-1 result, in particular, was a genuinely transformative measurement, and he was the right person to lead the team that produced it."

### Ouchi

"Yuki's HSC quasar survey work is what every survey wants to be," said Professor Masami Ouchi (NAOJ).

"It is patient, it is systematic, it is faithfully reported, and it just keeps producing results year after year."

### Tanaka himself

"I am embarrassed by this prize because I share it with the entire team," said Tanaka, when reached by phone for comment.

"JWST data analysis is not done alone."

"I would like to thank my co-investigators, my students, my long-suffering wife, and the engineers at STScI who actually fly the telescope."

## About the Nishina Memorial Foundation

The Nishina Memorial Foundation was established in 1955 to honor the memory of Yoshio Nishina.

Nishina founded modern physics research in Japan.

He was a mentor to the Nobel laureates Hideki Yukawa and Sin-Itiro Tomonaga.

The foundation awards the Nishina Memorial Prize annually to Japanese researchers under the age of 56 (with limited exceptions) for outstanding work in fundamental physics.

The prize has been awarded annually since 1955 with the exception of 1958.

For more information, visit https://www.nishina-mf.or.jp.
