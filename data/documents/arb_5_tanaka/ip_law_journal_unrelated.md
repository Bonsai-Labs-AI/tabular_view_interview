# Standard-Essential Patents and the Smartphone Wars: A Decade in Review

**Berkeley Technology Law Journal** | Vol. 38, Issue 2 | Spring 2023

## Introduction

This article surveys a decade of litigation and licensing disputes involving standard-essential patents (SEPs) in the wireless communications space, beginning with the *Microsoft v. Motorola* injunction-and-RAND-rate proceedings in the Western District of Washington and ending with the more recent string of FRAND determinations in the English courts and the Unified Patent Court. We argue that despite the proliferation of judgments, the substantive standard for what constitutes a "fair, reasonable, and non-discriminatory" licensing rate remains underspecified, and that the divergence between U.S. and European approaches has widened rather than narrowed.

## Part I. The American Trajectory

Part I traces the U.S. case law from *Microsoft v. Motorola* (W.D. Wash. 2013) through the Federal Circuit's decisions in *Ericsson v. D-Link* (2014) and *CSIRO v. Cisco* (2015). We focus particularly on the modified Georgia-Pacific framework as adapted to SEP contexts and on the methodological choices courts have made when constructing comparable-license baselines.
