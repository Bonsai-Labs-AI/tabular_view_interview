# The Quiet Tribunals: How Asia-Pacific Became the Center of Tech-Licensing Dispute Resolution

*By Hiroshi Maeda, Nikkei Asia.*

*Long-form feature, published April 8, 2024.*

*Reading time: 28 minutes.*

---

## Opening scene

**SINGAPORE / TOKYO / SEOUL / SHENZHEN —** On the 39th floor of a glass tower in Singapore's Maxwell Chambers Suites, in a windowless conference room reserved months in advance, three arbitrators in dark suits spent the second week of February quietly setting a global royalty rate for one of the most-licensed patent portfolios in the wireless industry.

The hearing ran for nine working days.

The total amount in dispute, according to two people familiar with the matter who spoke on condition of anonymity because of confidentiality obligations, was approximately US$2.4 billion.

The award, when it issues later this year, will probably not be reported in the press.

The parties' lawyers will not comment.

The arbitrators will not speak about it.

And yet, by the time this article is published, the case will already have shaped the licensing terms that hundreds of millions of mobile devices will be sold under in the next half decade.

## The new geography

This is the new geography of intellectual property dispute resolution.

Opaque.

Private.

Expensive.

And increasingly Asian.

Across the Asia-Pacific region — in Singapore, Hong Kong, Tokyo, Seoul, Shenzhen, and Sydney — a network of arbitral institutions, specialist counsel, and a few dozen trusted arbitrators has, over the past fifteen years, displaced public courts as the principal venue for resolving the most consequential disputes in the global technology economy.

The rise of these tribunals has been gradual and largely unremarked.

There is no press conference when a major SEP dispute is settled by arbitration.

There is no docket to read.

The awards, when they issue, are not published.

Even the existence of a dispute is often confidential.

The result is that the largest single-asset commercial determinations made anywhere in the world are now made, routinely, in proceedings that the public never sees and rarely hears about.

This feature is an attempt to describe the geography of those proceedings.

Who is involved.

Where they happen.

Why parties choose them.

And what the rise of private dispute resolution means for the broader trajectory of innovation policy in the region.

## I. The institutions

The four most-used institutional arbitration providers for technology licensing disputes in the Asia-Pacific region, by case volume, are:

The Singapore International Arbitration Centre (SIAC).

The Hong Kong International Arbitration Centre (HKIAC).

The Japan Commercial Arbitration Association (JCAA).

And — increasingly — the China International Economic and Trade Arbitration Commission (CIETAC).

### SIAC

SIAC, founded in 1991 and modernized aggressively under the leadership of its long-time CEO Lim Seok Hui, administered 1,142 cases in 2023.

Of these, approximately 18 percent (an estimate based on SIAC's own caseload report) involved intellectual property or technology-licensing subject matter.

A share that has grown steadily from approximately 9 percent in 2015.

The institution does not break out SEP-related cases separately.

But parties' counsel describe SIAC as "where the SEP work goes when the parties want a fast, modern process and an arbitrator pool that travels well across jurisdictions."

### HKIAC

HKIAC, somewhat older and historically more conservative, administered 503 cases in 2023.

Hong Kong's particular advantage is the New Arrangement on Reciprocal Recognition and Enforcement of Arbitral Awards with the People's Republic of China.

Which gives HKIAC awards a uniquely smooth path into enforcement in the Chinese mainland.

An advantage that matters greatly when one of the parties is Chinese and assets are located there.

### JCAA

JCAA, the Japanese institution, administered 76 cases in 2023.

Which is small by international comparison but represents a meaningful share of the cross-border patent licensing disputes involving Japanese parties.

JCAA has, under its recently appointed Secretary-General Takashi Hattori, positioned itself as a specialist venue for IP disputes.

With a curated roster of arbitrators including former judges of the IP High Court and a handful of well-known international neutrals.

### CIETAC

CIETAC, the largest of the four by total caseload (5,237 cases in 2023, of which only a small fraction were IP-related), has historically been used principally for Chinese-counterparty commercial disputes.

Its growth in technology cases has been recent and is concentrated in disputes where one or both parties are Chinese.

## II. The arbitrators

The number of people who can credibly chair a complex SEP arbitration involving telecommunications standards is small.

Perhaps two or three dozen, globally.

Of whom roughly half practice principally in or from the Asia-Pacific region.

The names are familiar to specialist practitioners.

They are unfamiliar to almost anyone else.

### The most-appointed

Among the most-appointed arbitrators in Asia-Pacific IP disputes, based on publicly available appointment records and interviews with eleven sitting practitioners conducted for this feature, are:

**Professor Anselmo Reyes**, the former Hong Kong High Court judge who served as the inaugural International Judge of the Singapore International Commercial Court and who is now one of the most-appointed arbitrators in the region for complex commercial and IP matters.

**Andrew Wong SC**, a senior counsel in Hong Kong whose practice has shifted heavily into arbitration in the past decade and who is particularly known for SEP rate-setting work.

**Datuk Sundra Rajoo**, the former Director of the Asian International Arbitration Centre in Kuala Lumpur, who continues to sit as an arbitrator across the region.

**Toshiro Mochizuki**, a retired judge of the Tokyo IP High Court whose name appears on a significant share of JCAA-administered patent cases.

**Beatrice Onica Jarka**, a Singapore-based arbitrator originally from Romania, who has built a substantial practice in technology cases.

**Karen Mills**, a long-time Indonesian arbitrator who serves on multiple regional panels.

**Yoshimi Ohara**, the Tokyo-based partner who appears as advocate in many of these disputes and is increasingly appointed as arbitrator in others.

### Occasionally cited

In a longer list of arbitrators who are "occasionally cited in commentary" as available for specialized SEP work, practitioners mentioned:

Lucy Reed (formerly Freshfields).

Cavinder Bull SC (Drew & Napier).

Professor Doug Jones AO (the Australian arbitrator with the long-running practice in infrastructure disputes who has more recently taken on IP work).

Yuki Tanaka (the Stanford-trained patent specialist who divides time between Palo Alto and Tokyo).

And Hi-Taek Shin (the former President of the Korean Arbitration Association).

### A problem

Notable in this list is the relative paucity of women.

A problem the institutions have publicly acknowledged and committed to addressing.

With limited progress.

## III. The substance

Across roughly thirty interviews conducted for this feature with arbitrators, counsel, in-house lawyers at major Asian and European technology companies, and academic observers, several recurring substantive themes emerged.

### Comparable licenses

Comparable licenses are the dominant valuation methodology.

Notwithstanding the academic literature's interest in bottom-up valuation approaches based on patent counts and incremental value, practitioners report that the substantial majority of FRAND arbitral determinations in the region rely principally on comparable license benchmarking.

One London-based counsel who has appeared in seven such arbitrations described the methodology as "find the most truly comparable license, argue about why it is or is not comparable, and produce a rate."

### A small expert pool

The pool of competent technical experts is small.

Several practitioners mentioned the same handful of names.

Three or four economists.

Perhaps six telecommunications engineers.

Whose testimony appears in the substantial majority of cases.

The repetition of experts across cases raises uncomfortable questions about cross-examination dynamics that practitioners declined to discuss on the record.

### Awards take longer than advertised

Awards take longer than the institutional timelines suggest.

SIAC's expedited procedure target of six months from constitution to award is, in practice, met in only a minority of complex SEP cases.

Most cases run twelve to twenty-four months.

Several practitioners attributed this to the complexity of the underlying material.

And the difficulty of scheduling hearings with arbitrators who routinely have eight or more concurrent matters.

### Confidentiality is uneven

Although parties and arbitrators uniformly support confidentiality, the practical reality is that information about cases circulates through the small specialist bar.

Counsel who appear in one case for an SEP holder typically know, in general terms, the rates being argued in another case for the same SEP holder.

Whether this is a feature (efficient information aggregation) or a problem (informal collusion on rates) is debated.

## IV. The public-court alternative

The principal public-court alternatives to private arbitration in the region are:

The Tokyo IP High Court.

The Beijing IP Court and the Supreme People's Court of China.

The Korean IP High Court.

And the High Court of Singapore.

Of these, the Chinese courts have most aggressively asserted global rate-setting jurisdiction.

With mixed reception elsewhere.

Several practitioners interviewed for this feature predicted that the Chinese courts' jurisdictional assertions would push more disputes into arbitration.

On the theory that sophisticated parties prefer the perceived neutrality and confidentiality of arbitration to the risks of a Chinese court rate-setting on a global portfolio.

This prediction has, so far, been at least partially borne out by the caseload trends.

## V. The future

The region's IP arbitration practice is at an inflection point.

Three trends, each independent, may shape its evolution over the next decade.

### Trend 1: AI

First, the rise of AI-related licensing disputes.

Between model developers and training-data rightsholders.

Between model owners and downstream API customers.

Between owners of patented neural-network architectures and implementers.

Is producing a new generation of cases whose technical and legal questions are substantially different from the SEP disputes of the past fifteen years.

### Trend 2: Patent pools

Second, the growth of patent pools as a licensing mechanism (Avanci, Sisvel, Via Licensing) is changing the bilateral negotiation dynamics that have historically driven into arbitration.

Pool-administered licenses produce a different shape of dispute when they produce one at all.

### Trend 3: Geopolitical fragmentation

Third, the broader geopolitical fragmentation of the technology economy is generating disputes whose jurisdictional and substantive complexity exceed anything the existing institutional framework was designed for.

Whether the institutions can adapt is, in the view of several senior practitioners interviewed for this feature, an open question.

## VI. Coda: A view from the inside

I spoke, finally, with a senior in-house IP counsel at a major Japanese consumer electronics company, who agreed to speak on the condition that the company not be named.

He said something that has stayed with me.

"The thing the public doesn't understand," he said, "is that almost none of this is adversarial in the way litigation is adversarial."

"The companies are in continuous, multi-decade, multi-product relationships."

"We are in arbitration with the same companies we are jointly developing chipsets with."

"The arbitration is one move in a relationship that has thirty other moves going on at the same time."

"The arbitrator who understands that — the one who sees the relationship and not just the case — that is the one we want."

He paused.

"There are only a few of them. We are not going to tell you who."
