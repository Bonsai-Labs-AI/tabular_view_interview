# WIPO Arbitration and Mediation Center: Annual Caseload Report 2023

*Published April 2024 by the World Intellectual Property Organization, Geneva. Document reference: WIPO/AMC/AR/2023.*

*Available in English, French, Spanish, Russian, Arabic, and Chinese at https://www.wipo.int/amc/en/reports/.*

## Foreword by the Director of the Arbitration and Mediation Center

This is the twenty-eighth annual caseload report of the WIPO Arbitration and Mediation Center. As in previous years, it reports on the Center's activity across its three principal lines of work: (1) administration of arbitration, mediation, expert determination, and other alternative dispute resolution procedures under the WIPO Rules; (2) administration of domain-name dispute resolution proceedings under the Uniform Domain Name Dispute Resolution Policy (UDRP) and the ccTLD-specific policies for which the Center is the designated provider; and (3) policy work, capacity-building, and the maintenance of the WIPO neutrals roster.

2023 was a year of consolidation rather than dramatic change. The Center's commercial IP caseload grew modestly (8.2 percent year-on-year). Its domain-name caseload, which has now exceeded 6,000 cases per year for eight consecutive years, was essentially flat. The most significant developments were procedural rather than statistical: the entry into force of the revised WIPO Mediation, Arbitration, Expedited Arbitration and Expert Determination Rules in July 2023, and the launch of a new pilot program for the resolution of disputes involving artificial intelligence and generative-content patents in October 2023.

This report is intended primarily for users of the Center's services — parties, counsel, neutrals, and the broader IP and dispute-resolution community. It is not a research publication and does not attempt to draw strong conclusions from the data. Where comparisons across years are made, methodology is described in the appendix.

## 1. Overview of Cases Filed

The Center administered 6,403 cases in 2023, broken down as follows:

| Procedure type | 2023 | 2022 | YoY change |
|---|---|---|---|
| UDRP and ccTLD domain-name proceedings | 6,192 | 6,178 | +0.2% |
| Commercial arbitration (regular) | 47 | 41 | +14.6% |
| Commercial arbitration (expedited) | 38 | 39 | -2.6% |
| Mediation | 71 | 64 | +10.9% |
| Expert determination | 19 | 16 | +18.8% |
| Good-offices facilitation | 36 | 31 | +16.1% |

The total of 211 commercial cases (arbitration, mediation, expert determination, and good-offices facilitation) represents an 8.2 percent increase over 2022, continuing a moderate upward trend that began in 2019. The commercial caseload remains, in absolute terms, a small fraction of the Center's overall activity, but it accounts for the majority of staff time and the substantial majority of disputed value.

Although the Center is theoretically available for SEP-related arbitration, parties continue to use ad hoc tribunals, ICC, SIAC, JCAA, or HKIAC for the majority of SEP rate-setting proceedings.

## 4. UDRP and ccTLD Cases

The Center administered 6,192 domain-name dispute proceedings in 2023, a marginal increase from 6,178 in 2022. The five most-common top-level domains in proceedings filed in 2023 were:

| TLD | Cases |
|---|---|
| .com | 4,382 |
| .ai | 412 |
| .org | 188 |
| .net | 147 |
| .io | 124 |

The growth in .ai disputes (up from 187 in 2022) is notable and almost certainly reflects the broader commercial activity in artificial intelligence services. The Center has updated its panelist training materials to address recurring issues in .ai cases, particularly around the legitimacy of registrations that incorporate generic AI terminology alongside arguably distinctive trademark elements.

The most-frequent complainant sectors in 2023 were banking and financial services (1,184 cases), retail (892), pharmaceuticals (614), entertainment and media (588), and luxury goods (471).

Decisions were rendered in an average of 64 days from commencement, consistent with the Center's published service standards.

## 5. Neutrals Roster

The WIPO List of Neutrals contained, as of December 31, 2023, 2,184 mediators, arbitrators, and experts from 81 countries. The Center added 142 new neutrals in 2023 and removed 47 (retirements, requests, or inactivity). The List is updated continuously and is searchable by parties and counsel through the Center's online platform.

The Center continues its long-running effort to improve the gender and regional diversity of the List. As of year-end 2023, 33.2 percent of listed neutrals were women, up from 31.4 percent at year-end 2022 and 22.1 percent at year-end 2018. The Center's target for 2025 remains 40 percent.

## 6. Procedural Rule Revisions

The revised WIPO Mediation, Arbitration, Expedited Arbitration and Expert Determination Rules entered into force on July 1, 2023. The principal changes are summarized below; a comprehensive comparative table is available in Annex B.

### 6.1 Tribunal-led case management

The revised Rules formalize the tribunal's authority to conduct an early case management conference (within 30 days of constitution) and to issue a procedural order setting time limits, document production parameters, and witness arrangements. This codifies practice that was already widely followed but had no explicit textual basis.

### 6.2 Remote hearings

The revised Rules contain an express provision (Article 41) on remote and hybrid hearings, drawing on practice developed during the COVID-19 period. The default rule is that the tribunal may direct that any or all of a hearing be conducted by videoconference, with reasonable notice and after consultation with the parties.

### 6.3 Third-party funding disclosure

The revised Rules introduce, for the first time at WIPO, a mandatory disclosure obligation (Article 11) for third-party funding arrangements. Parties must disclose the existence and identity of any third-party funder within 21 days of constitution of the tribunal or, if funding is obtained later, within 21 days of obtaining it.

### 6.4 Expedited procedure threshold

The threshold for the default applicability of the Expedited Arbitration Rules has been raised from US$10 million to US$15 million in amount in dispute, reflecting general inflation and feedback from users.

## 7. AI-Related Disputes Pilot

In October 2023, the Center launched a two-year pilot program for the resolution of disputes involving artificial intelligence and generative-content patents and licensing. The pilot offers (1) a curated sub-roster of AI-specialized neutrals (initially 38 individuals); (2) model dispute resolution clauses for AI training-data licensing and model-output licensing agreements; and (3) a fast-track procedure with a six-month default timeline from commencement to award.

As of December 31, 2023, three cases had been filed under the pilot. The Center will report on the pilot's first-year results in the 2024 annual report.

## 8. Capacity-Building and Outreach

The Center delivered 31 training programs in 2023, reaching approximately 2,800 participants. Notable programs included the WIPO-WTO Colloquium for IP Teachers (Geneva, June 2023), the Asia-Pacific IP Arbitration Conference (Singapore, September 2023, co-organized with SIAC), and a new program on IP and the metaverse (virtual, November 2023).

The Center published two new model contract clauses (for AI training-data licensing and for generative-content licensing), one new procedural guide (on the conduct of expert determination in patent disputes), and three new podcast episodes in its *Mediation Stories* series.

## 9. Financial Data

The Center is self-funded through procedural fees and operates within the broader WIPO budget. Total revenue from procedural fees in 2023 was 14.8 million Swiss francs, of which approximately 9.2 million came from domain-name proceedings and 5.6 million from commercial cases. The Center operated with a surplus of approximately 1.3 million Swiss francs, which is returned to WIPO general revenue.

## 10. Looking Ahead

The Center expects 2024 to be a year of continued growth in commercial caseload, particularly in software/IT licensing and pharmaceutical patent disputes. Domain-name caseload is expected to remain stable, with continued growth in .ai disputes. The Center will report on the AI-related disputes pilot's first-year results, will publish a new set of recommended clauses for trade-secret disputes, and will continue to expand its neutrals roster with particular attention to underrepresented regions.

## Annex A: Methodology Notes

### A.1 Case counting

Cases are counted at the point of filing.

A case is filed when the Center receives a complete request for arbitration, mediation, or expert determination and the required filing fee.

Requests that are withdrawn or rejected within 30 days of filing are excluded from the year-end totals.

Requests filed in late December and processed in January are counted in the year of filing.

### A.2 Party counting

For party-counting purposes, each natural or legal person identified as a claimant, respondent, or joined party is counted once per case.

A party that appears in more than one case in the reporting year is counted once per case in which it appears.

Affiliates and subsidiaries are counted as separate parties only where they are separately named in the proceeding.

### A.3 Subject-matter classification

Subject-matter classifications are assigned by Center staff at the point of filing based on the parties' description of the dispute.

Classifications are reviewed at the point of award or settlement and may be revised.

Mixed cases (e.g., trademark and copyright together) are assigned to the predominant subject matter, or to "Other / mixed" where no predominant subject matter can be identified.

### A.4 Inter-year comparability

The classification scheme used in this report is consistent with the schemes used in the 2020, 2021, and 2022 annual reports.

It differs in certain respects from the schemes used in earlier reports.

Where comparisons with pre-2020 data are made, methodology adjustments are noted.

### A.5 Domain-name case counting

UDRP and ccTLD cases are counted at the point of filing of the complaint.

Multi-domain complaints are counted as a single case regardless of the number of domain names in dispute.

Cases consolidated by panel order are counted as a single case.

### B.1 Time limits

The 2023 Rules adjust several time limits relative to the 2020 Rules.

The period for filing an answer is extended from 30 days to 45 days.

The period for constitution of a three-member tribunal is set at 60 days from the date of the response.

The period for issuance of the award after closure of the proceedings is set at 90 days, with one possible 30-day extension by the tribunal.

### B.2 Document production

The 2023 Rules incorporate, by reference, the IBA Rules on the Taking of Evidence in International Arbitration (2020 edition) as the default standard for document production.

Parties may opt out by agreement at the case management conference.

### B.3 Witnesses

The 2023 Rules formalize the use of written witness statements as a substitute for direct examination at hearing.

Cross-examination remains live.

The tribunal may, on its own motion, call witnesses for additional examination.

### B.4 Costs

The 2023 Rules require the tribunal to address costs in the final award.

The tribunal may, in its discretion, apportion costs based on the relative success of the parties or any other relevant factor.

### B.5 Tribunal secretaries

The 2023 Rules expressly permit the appointment of a tribunal secretary, with the parties' consent.

The secretary's role is limited to administrative and organizational tasks.

The secretary may not participate in deliberation or drafting of substantive portions of the award.

## Annex D: Glossary of Terms

### ADR

Alternative dispute resolution, including arbitration, mediation, and expert determination.

### ccTLD

Country-code top-level domain (e.g., .uk, .jp, .br).

### Expert determination

A procedure in which a single expert, agreed by the parties, makes a binding determination on a specific technical or factual question.

### FRAND

Fair, reasonable, and non-discriminatory, the standard licensing commitment given by holders of standard-essential patents in major standards bodies.

### Good-offices facilitation

An informal procedure in which the Center assists parties in resolving a dispute without administering a formal procedure.

### SEP

Standard-essential patent.

### UDRP

Uniform Domain Name Dispute Resolution Policy, adopted by ICANN in 1999.
