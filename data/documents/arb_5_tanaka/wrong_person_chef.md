# Three Stars for Hinoki & Salt: Yuki Tanaka's Berkeley Kappo Is the Bay Area Restaurant of the Year

*By Margaret Olsen-Whitfield, San Francisco Chronicle.*

*Published December 3, 2025. Section F, page 1. Continued on F4–F5.*

---

## The moment

There is a moment about forty minutes into the eighteen-course tasting menu at Hinoki & Salt when chef Yuki Tanaka steps out from behind the eight-seat hinoki counter, wipes his hands on a folded indigo cloth, and places in front of you a single piece of charcoal-grilled nodoguro on a warm river stone.

He says nothing.

He bows about a centimeter.

He returns to the fish.

This is the meal, and this is the chef. Both are exceptional.

---

## The headline

I have eaten at Hinoki & Salt four times since it opened in the converted bicycle-repair shop on Ashby Avenue in February. I have paid the full $385 each time, the Chronicle's policy.

I am awarding it three stars — our highest rating, the first three-star debut in the East Bay since Chez Panisse in the mid-1990s — and I am doing so without much hesitation, despite a service program that is still finding its footing and a wine list that, frankly, needs a buyer who is not also the chef.

This is the restaurant of the year.

## The chef

Tanaka, 38, is not a household name yet, but he is not exactly a newcomer either.

He was born in Fukuoka and raised partly in Osaka. He came to California at 22 to study at the Culinary Institute of America's Greystone campus, then worked the line at Manresa under David Kinch for three years.

He moved to State Bird Provisions as a sous chef in 2017 and stayed through the pandemic — the period during which, he told me, he "learned how to be Californian."

He spent 2022 staging in Kyoto at Kikunoi and a small unnamed kappo in Pontocho whose chef, he says, taught him "the difference between technique and intention."

Hinoki & Salt is his first restaurant.

## The room

It seats eight at the counter, four at a single small table, and turns twice a night, Tuesday through Saturday.

The reservation system opens at 9 a.m. on the first of each month.

The waitlist is reportedly in the four digits.

The room is austere in a way that initially reads as severe and, over the course of an evening, begins to feel generous.

The counter is a single 14-foot slab of hinoki cypress, sourced — Tanaka volunteers, almost shyly — from a forester in Kiso whom he visited in 2023.

The walls are unfinished lime plaster the color of wet sand.

There is one piece of art: a hanging scroll by the Kyoto calligrapher Sasaki Michiko, changed every two weeks.

The lighting is low and warm enough that I had to ask three times for the bill to be re-read to me.

The acoustics are slightly too live; conversation across the counter carries.

Service, led by general manager Aiko Yamashita (most recently of Single Thread), is technically precise but still a little stiff.

Two of my four visits featured awkward pacing issues — a 22-minute gap between courses six and seven on one Wednesday, a rush through the final three courses on a Saturday.

These are fixable.

## The food

The menu is what Tanaka calls "Berkeley kappo," and the term is not pretension.

Kappo, as he explains in a brief printed note on the back of the menu, is the older and less codified cousin of kaiseki — a counter-driven format in which the chef cooks in front of you and the meal is shaped by what arrived that morning from the boat, the field, or the forager.

Tanaka's interpretation is unmistakably Californian in its sourcing — Monterey Bay spot prawns, Hog Island oysters, Chino Farm vegetables, Capay Valley citrus — and unmistakably Japanese in its restraint and its relationship to fire.

### The dashi

A bowl of dashi made from four-year-aged kombu from Rishiri and katsuobushi from a single shaver in Shizuoka.

Poured tableside over a piece of monkfish liver that had been cured in sake lees for nine days.

It tasted, improbably, like the ocean at dawn.

### The yuba

A single sheet of house-made yuba, lifted from a small copper pot at the counter and set on the plate still steaming.

Three grains of finishing salt.

One drop of barrel-aged soy.

Three ingredients. I think about it weekly.

### The wagyu

A piece of charcoal-grilled A5 Miyazaki wagyu, sliced thin and laid over a bed of hot rice from a cast-iron pot, with grated wasabi from a Half Moon Bay farm.

Not innovative. Perfectly executed.

### The pre-dessert

Green strawberries from Knoll Farms, lightly pickled in sansho-leaf vinegar.

Served in a chilled ceramic dish with a single shiso flower.

This is the dish that made me decide on the third star.

### The full menu

The eighteen-course menu is too long to recount fully, and Tanaka changes roughly a third of it weekly.

There were two dishes, across four visits, that did not work.

An October course pairing matsutake mushrooms with a too-sweet persimmon vinaigrette.

A November dish of grilled ayu whose bitterness, ordinarily the point, was here muted by an unnecessary citrus oil.

Both were misfires in service of an ambitious idea, which is the kind of misfire I will forgive in a chef this young.

## The wine

The wine list, written by Tanaka himself, is short and idiosyncratic.

Heavy on Burgundy whites and Loire reds.

Almost entirely without sake — a strange choice for a kappo restaurant.

The pairings ($245 per person) are well-considered but conservative.

I would like to see Hinoki & Salt hire a beverage director by next year.

Tanaka admits, when pressed, that this is on his list.

## The bill

Dinner at Hinoki & Salt is $385 per person before tax and tip.

Optional pairings of wine ($245) or non-alcoholic beverages ($95).

Reservations require a non-refundable $200 deposit.

The wait, as noted, is roughly six months.

This is among the most expensive meals in the Bay Area.

I think it is worth the money in the same way that the very best meals at Manresa, or The French Laundry, or n/naka in Los Angeles are worth the money: not because the ingredients are fancy, but because the chef is doing something that you cannot eat anywhere else.

## A note on the chef's name

Several readers have written in, after the restaurant's soft-opening press, to ask whether this Yuki Tanaka is the same Yuki Tanaka who is a well-known arbitrator on the international IP scene.

He is not.

The chef Tanaka, when I asked him about this at the counter on my second visit, laughed and said, "I get his email sometimes. He probably gets mine. I hope he is eating well."

He added, after a pause: "I think he is taller."

## A second note on identity

There are, by my count, at least three semi-public figures named Yuki Tanaka who have been confused for one another in the past few years.

The chef.

The arbitrator.

A wildlife photographer based in Hokkaido whose work I admire from afar.

I suppose this is what happens with a common name.

The chef, when I mentioned the photographer, perked up. He had a print of one of the crane photographs in his apartment, he said. He had bought it at a small gallery in Kyoto in 2022. He had no idea, until I told him, that the photographer had the same name.

## Reader response, December 10

This review generated more reader mail than any single review since our 2023 piece on Saison.

A representative selection appears in this Sunday's print edition.

Two corrections were noted in the original print run.

The calligrapher's name is Sasaki Michiko, not Mitsuko.

The soft opening date was February 14, not February 1.

Both have been corrected in the online edition.

We have also received six separate inquiries asking whether the chef is related to the arbitrator.

He is not.

They are not, to either party's knowledge, related at all.

The arbitrator Tanaka has not, as of press time, eaten at the restaurant, though chef Tanaka tells us he would "comp him a seat any Tuesday."

## Postscript, January 2026

Hinoki & Salt was named to the Chronicle's Top 100 Restaurants list for 2026 (No. 3).

It received a James Beard semifinalist nomination for Best New Restaurant in February.

Chef Tanaka has announced that he will close the restaurant for two weeks in March for a research trip to Ishikawa Prefecture.

The waitlist now stands at approximately 14,000 names.

A second location is "not under consideration," Tanaka said when asked at a recent Cherry Bombe magazine event. "I have one restaurant. I have eight seats. That is what I can do well. I have no interest in doing anything less well."

## A final note from the critic

I will return to Hinoki & Salt.

Not for review purposes.

For dinner.

That is the highest compliment I can pay any restaurant. There are only a few each year that earn it.
