### FINRA Arbitrator Hits Halberg Securities With $3.25M Award In Family Office Structured-Notes Suitability Case

By Constance Mehmetaj

*Law360 (March 16, 2023, 6:18 PM EDT)*

---

A solo FINRA arbitrator has ordered Jersey City-based broker-dealer
Halberg Securities Inc. and one of its registered representatives,
Daniel R. Voss, to pay $3.25 million in compensatory damages to a
New York-based family office that alleged the firm sold it approximately
$14.7 million in autocallable structured notes that were materially
mismatched to its stated capital-preservation objective.

The award, signed March 14 by sole arbitrator James R. Whitfield of
New York and entered in FINRA Arbitration No. 22-01847, resolves a
Statement of Claim filed in February 2022 by claimant Meridian Capital
Partners LP.

Meridian, which is run by principal Avi Reinholt, alleged that Halberg
and Voss recommended and sold the structured notes — which referenced a
basket of mid-cap regional bank equities — between June 2019 and
November 2020 in violation of FINRA Conduct Rule 2111's suitability
requirements.

The Meridian claim asserted four counts:

(i) violation of FINRA Conduct Rule 2111;
(ii) breach of fiduciary duty;
(iii) negligent misrepresentation; and
(iv) breach of contract.

Meridian sought compensatory damages, punitive damages, and
attorneys' fees.

Whitfield, a New York-based solo neutral who runs Whitfield ADR Services
LLC and who has handled FINRA arbitrations as a sole arbitrator and as a
panel chair for more than a decade, awarded compensatory damages of
$3.25 million but denied Meridian's claims for punitive damages and for
attorneys' fees, finding "no statutory or contractual basis" for the
latter.

Pre-award interest at the New York statutory 9% rate was awarded from
December 1, 2020, through the award date, and FINRA filing and forum fees
were ordered split equally between the parties as previously assessed.

Whitfield also denied a counterclaim for indemnification asserted by the
respondents, along with all other relief not specifically addressed in
the award.

### The Underlying Transactions

According to publicly available filings and the arbitrator's award,
Meridian — a family-office investment vehicle for the Reinholt family —
opened a brokerage account at Halberg in the spring of 2019 with the
stated objective, as reflected in account-opening documentation, of
"capital preservation with modest income."

Beginning in June 2019, Voss began recommending a series of autocallable
structured notes referenced to a basket of mid-cap regional bank
equities.

Between June 2019 and November 2020, Meridian purchased approximately
$14.7 million in notional value of such notes across what the award
characterizes as multiple separate tranches.

When the underlying basket declined sharply in 2020 — particularly in
March of that year, during the early phase of the COVID-19 market
disruption — the structured notes' principal-at-risk features triggered,
and Meridian was left holding instruments whose value had declined
substantially.

Several of the notes were never called and instead delivered the
underlying basket equities to Meridian at maturity, locking in losses.

The Statement of Claim alleged that Voss had not adequately disclosed
the principal-at-risk features and the correlation risk embedded in the
basket, and that the notes were not appropriate for an investor whose
stated objective was capital preservation.

### The Defense

Halberg and Voss, represented by Peter J. McAvoy of McAvoy Greene PLLC
in Stamford, Connecticut, asserted affirmative defenses of ratification,
failure to mitigate, and contributory fault.

The defense argued that Reinholt was a sophisticated investor who had
executed risk-acknowledgement documentation on three separate occasions
during the relevant period, and that the structured notes were consistent
with a portion of Meridian's overall investment program.

The defense also argued that the alleged damages were the product of an
extraordinary market event — the early-2020 COVID-19 disruption — rather
than any product-suitability failure.

### The Hearing

The evidentiary hearing was held in six sessions between January 30 and
February 8, 2023, at FINRA's New York hearing facilities.

Both parties submitted post-hearing briefs.

The award does not describe the witness testimony in detail, but
practitioners familiar with comparable structured-notes cases say the
hearings typically involve testimony from the customer, the registered
representative, expert witnesses on suitability and on structured-product
economics, and supervisory personnel at the broker-dealer.

Claimant Meridian was represented by Hannah Lieu of Goldfarb & Strauss
LLP in New York.

### Commentary

The award is notable for several reasons.

First, it reflects a substantial customer recovery in a case involving an
investor — a family office with a sophisticated principal — that
broker-dealers have historically argued should be held to a higher
standard of self-protection.

Plaintiffs' counsel have, in recent years, argued with increasing success
that the suitability rule's reasonable-basis prong applies even to
sophisticated investors when the stated objective and the recommended
product are materially misaligned.

"You see more of these cases now where the customer is, by any
conventional metric, sophisticated, but the panel still finds for the
customer because the documented objective and the product are just
irreconcilable," said Penelope Stern-Zaccaro, a partner at Stern Zaccaro
Saperstein LLP in New York who represents customer claimants in FINRA
cases but was not involved in this matter.

"The risk-acknowledgement documentation only goes so far. If the product
is genuinely mismatched, the panels are going to find for the claimant."

Second, the award's denial of punitive damages and attorneys' fees — even
alongside a substantial compensatory award — is consistent with the
general pattern observed in FINRA customer awards.

Punitive damages are awarded in only a small minority of cases that
produce a customer recovery, and attorneys' fees are rarely awarded in
the absence of a contractual or statutory basis.

Third, the structure of the award — a single solo arbitrator handling a
multimillion-dollar dispute over a six-session hearing — reflects an
increasingly common pattern in FINRA cases.

The parties had agreed to a single arbitrator under FINRA Code provisions
permitting that election, which is generally available where the claim
does not exceed certain thresholds or where the parties stipulate.

#### Related Awards

The award follows a small but meaningful cluster of comparable
structured-product awards issued by FINRA arbitrators over the trailing
twelve months.

In *Calhoun-Beckler Family Office LP v. Northcrest Securities Corp.*,
FINRA Case No. 21-02184 (Award entered November 2022), a three-arbitrator
panel awarded approximately $4.1 million on similar facts.

In *Kovalenko Investments LP v. Rotweiler Capital LLC*, FINRA Case No.
22-00591 (Award entered January 2023), a sole arbitrator awarded
approximately $2.8 million on similar facts.

Practitioners read the cluster as reflecting an emerging consensus among
FINRA arbitrators that the suitability rule meaningfully constrains the
recommendation of complex structured products to customers with
documented conservative objectives, regardless of the customer's general
sophistication.

### About the Arbitrator

Whitfield, who graduated from Yale Law School and who entered FINRA's
neutral roster in 2014 after more than two decades in private practice,
is one of a relatively small group of New York-based solo neutrals who
handle higher-stakes FINRA matters as sole arbitrator.

He maintains his practice through Whitfield ADR Services LLC,
headquartered at 489 Fifth Avenue in Manhattan.

He is reportedly active on the AAA and CPR rosters in addition to FINRA's
roster, and handles a small number of commercial post-acquisition
indemnification matters in addition to his FINRA practice.

### About the Parties

Halberg Securities Inc. is a FINRA-registered broker-dealer headquartered
in Jersey City, New Jersey, with approximately 140 registered
representatives across four U.S. offices.

The firm specializes in structured products, fixed income, and equity
research for institutional and high-net-worth clients.

Meridian Capital Partners LP is a New York-based family-office investment
vehicle.

Its principal, Avi Reinholt, is the founder of a privately-held
specialty-chemicals business that he sold in 2018.

### Counsel of Record

Meridian Capital Partners LP is represented by Hannah Lieu of
Goldfarb & Strauss LLP.

Halberg Securities Inc. and Daniel R. Voss are represented by
Peter J. McAvoy of McAvoy Greene PLLC.

The case is *Meridian Capital Partners LP v. Halberg Securities Inc. and
Daniel R. Voss*, FINRA Arbitration No. 22-01847, in FINRA Dispute
Resolution Services, Hearing Site: New York, New York.

#### Editor's Note

Law360 obtained the award through FINRA's online award database, which
makes customer-arbitration awards publicly available shortly after
issuance.

The award is captioned as the "Award" and is signed by James R. Whitfield
as sole arbitrator.

The award does not include findings of fact in the narrative form typical
of court opinions; FINRA arbitrators are not required to issue reasoned
awards unless requested by the parties.

A request for comment was directed to counsel for both parties.

Hannah Lieu of Goldfarb & Strauss said in a written statement:
"We are gratified that the arbitrator recognized the significant harm
caused to our client by the recommendation and sale of these inappropriate
products, and we believe the award speaks for itself."

Peter J. McAvoy of McAvoy Greene declined to comment.

Whitfield, through his office, also declined to comment, citing his
general practice of not commenting on cases he has decided.
