# FINRA Regulatory Notice 24-17

## Member Firm Electronic Discovery Obligations in Customer Arbitration Proceedings

**Notice Type:** Guidance

**Suggested Routing:** Compliance, Legal, Operations, Senior Management, Technology

**Key Topics:** Arbitration, Books and Records, Discovery, Electronic Communications, Information Governance, Supervision

**Referenced Rules and Notices:** FINRA Rules 2010, 3110, 4511, 4530, 12506, 12509, 12511; SEA Rules 17a-3 and 17a-4; Regulatory Notices 18-19, 21-31

**Effective Date:** September 1, 2024

**Publication Date:** May 14, 2024

---

### Executive Summary

FINRA is issuing this Notice to provide guidance to member firms concerning
their obligations relating to the preservation, collection, review, and
production of electronically stored information ("ESI") in customer
arbitration proceedings administered by FINRA Dispute Resolution Services
("DRS").

The Notice responds to increasing concerns identified through FINRA
examinations and enforcement actions, as well as through feedback from the
DRS Discovery Task Force convened in March 2023, that member firms'
e-discovery practices in arbitration are inconsistent, in some cases
deficient, and frequently the source of avoidable disputes between parties.

This Notice does not impose new requirements.

It clarifies existing obligations under FINRA Rules 2010, 3110, 4511,
12506, and 12509, and the SEC's books-and-records rules
(SEA Rules 17a-3 and 17a-4), as applied in the arbitration context.

Member firms should review their information governance, legal-hold, and
arbitration-response procedures against the guidance set forth below.

### I. Background

Customer arbitration cases administered by FINRA DRS routinely require
the production of substantial volumes of ESI by member firms.

The Discovery Guide adopted under FINRA Rule 12506 enumerates fourteen
presumptively discoverable document categories in customer cases, several
of which — most notably the firm's communications with the customer,
internal communications referencing the customer or the disputed
transactions, and supervisory and compliance review documentation —
implicate large volumes of email, instant messaging, mobile-device
communications, voice recordings, and structured-data systems.

FINRA's review of cases closed between January 2021 and December 2023
identified a meaningful subset — approximately 7.4% of cases that proceeded
to an evidentiary hearing — in which arbitration panels expressly addressed
deficiencies in a party's ESI production, either through sanctions orders,
adverse-inference rulings, or fee-shifting.

In the vast majority of those cases, the deficient party was the
respondent member firm.

The most commonly cited deficiencies fell into the following categories:

1. **Failure to issue or maintain a timely legal hold.**
   In multiple cases, member firms were unable to establish that a legal
   hold had been issued promptly upon receipt of the statement of claim,
   or upon earlier triggering events such as a written customer complaint.

2. **Incomplete collection from off-channel and personal-device
   communications.**
   Cases involving allegations of misconduct by individual registered
   representatives frequently revealed that text messages, WhatsApp
   messages, personal-email communications, and other off-channel
   communications were not collected, despite credible evidence that
   such communications existed and were responsive.

3. **Over-reliance on individual custodian self-collection.**
   Several cases involved productions in which the responsive ESI had
   been collected by the individual custodians themselves — often the
   representatives whose conduct was at issue — without supervision by
   compliance or counsel.

4. **Failure to preserve voice recordings.**
   Several broker-dealers maintain telephonic recording systems with
   relatively short retention periods (90 to 180 days). In cases where
   the precipitating events predated the receipt of a written complaint,
   voice recordings of relevant calls had often been auto-deleted before
   a hold was implemented.

5. **Inadequate documentation of search-and-review methodology.**
   In several cases, panels noted that the producing party had not
   documented the search terms, date ranges, custodians, or systems
   searched, making it impossible to assess the adequacy of the production
   or to evaluate claims of relevance and proportionality.

### II. Existing Obligations

#### A. Books and Records

SEA Rules 17a-3 and 17a-4 require broker-dealers to make and preserve
specified records for specified periods.

FINRA Rule 4511 incorporates and supplements those obligations.

Records subject to these rules must be preserved in a manner that ensures
their accessibility for the duration of the required retention period,
regardless of whether litigation or arbitration is pending or anticipated.

In the arbitration context, these record-retention obligations operate as
a floor — not a ceiling — on a firm's preservation obligations.

Once a firm has notice of an actual or reasonably anticipated arbitration
claim, additional preservation obligations attach to records that may or
may not be subject to the books-and-records rules but that are nevertheless
reasonably likely to be relevant.

#### B. Supervision

FINRA Rule 3110 requires member firms to establish and maintain supervisory
systems and written supervisory procedures ("WSPs") reasonably designed to
achieve compliance with applicable securities laws and FINRA rules.

WSPs must address the firm's procedures for responding to customer
complaints, including the issuance of legal holds and the preservation of
relevant records, when a complaint may foreseeably give rise to an
arbitration claim.

Failure to maintain adequate WSPs addressing arbitration preparedness —
including legal-hold procedures, custodian-identification procedures, and
ESI-collection protocols — may itself constitute a supervisory violation
independent of any underlying discovery dispute.

#### C. The Discovery Guide

Under the Discovery Guide adopted pursuant to FINRA Rule 12506, certain
document categories are presumptively discoverable in customer arbitration
cases.

A respondent member firm bears the burden of producing documents responsive
to those categories without the need for a specific discovery request.

Failure to produce responsive documents — or production of an incomplete
subset — may result in sanctions under FINRA Rule 12511.

The 2022 amendments to the Discovery Guide expanded several categories to
expressly include ESI, including off-channel communications maintained on
personal devices to the extent they relate to firm business.

### III. Guidance

Member firms should review their information governance, legal-hold, and
arbitration-response procedures against the following guidance.

#### A. Legal Hold Issuance

A written legal hold should be issued no later than the firm's receipt of
a Statement of Claim from FINRA DRS, and in many cases substantially
earlier — including upon receipt of a written customer complaint that
reasonably foreshadows an arbitration claim, or upon receipt of an
attorney representation letter accompanied by document-preservation
language.

The hold should be issued in writing, should identify with reasonable
specificity the categories of records to be preserved, the relevant time
period, the custodians subject to the hold, and the systems implicated.

Holds should be reissued or refreshed periodically, particularly in cases
that remain pending for extended periods.

Firms should maintain documentation sufficient to establish, in the event
of a discovery dispute, the date the hold was issued, the recipients, and
the firm's process for confirming compliance.

The hold notice should include, at a minimum: (i) a description of the
matter giving rise to the hold; (ii) instructions to suspend any
auto-deletion or routine destruction with respect to the held records;
(iii) instructions concerning the preservation of personal-device
communications used for firm business; and (iv) contact information for
follow-up questions.

#### B. Custodian Identification

Firms should establish procedures for identifying all custodians whose
records are reasonably likely to contain relevant ESI.

Custodian identification should not be limited to the registered
representative principally involved in the disputed transactions; it
should extend to supervisors, compliance personnel who reviewed the
relevant accounts, operations and back-office staff involved in trade
execution and settlement, and any other personnel with reasonably
foreseeable relevant communications.

Custodian identification should be documented contemporaneously and
revisited periodically as the case develops.

In matters where new claims are added by amendment, or where the operative
period is materially expanded, the custodian list should be revisited and
expanded as appropriate.

#### C. Off-Channel Communications

Consistent with FINRA's prior guidance on off-channel communications
(see Regulatory Notice 21-31), firms should establish procedures for
identifying, collecting, and reviewing relevant off-channel communications,
including text messages, WhatsApp messages, personal-email communications,
and other channels.

Where firm policy prohibits the use of off-channel communications for firm
business, the existence of relevant off-channel communications does not
relieve the firm of its production obligation; it may simultaneously expose
the firm to separate supervisory or recordkeeping concerns.

Firms should consider implementing mobile device management ("MDM")
solutions, or comparable technical controls, that enable the firm to
collect relevant communications from personal devices used for firm
business with appropriate consent.

#### D. Voice Recording Preservation

Firms that maintain telephonic recording systems should review the
retention periods applicable to those systems and should establish
procedures for extending retention for recordings that may be relevant to
anticipated or pending arbitration.

Where voice recordings have been auto-deleted prior to the issuance of a
hold, firms should be prepared to document the date of deletion, the
applicable retention policy at the time, and the date the firm first had
notice of the relevant dispute.

Firms operating recording systems with retention periods shorter than the
applicable books-and-records retention period for the underlying records
should evaluate whether the recording system itself complies with
applicable retention requirements.

#### E. Search Methodology

Firms should document, contemporaneously, the search-and-review methodology
applied to each arbitration production, including the systems searched,
the custodians included, the date ranges applied, the search terms used
(or, in the case of technology-assisted review, the methodology applied),
and the review protocols used to assess responsiveness and privilege.

Firms are encouraged, but not required, to disclose this methodology to
the opposing party in advance of production.

Voluntary disclosure may, in practice, reduce the likelihood of discovery
disputes and may provide a basis for the firm to defend its production
methodology if challenged.

#### F. Privilege Logs

Where documents are withheld on the basis of attorney-client privilege or
work product, firms should produce a privilege log meeting the standards
customarily applied in federal civil litigation.

Bulk or "categorical" privilege logs may be appropriate in cases involving
very large volumes of withheld documents, but only where the categorical
entries provide sufficient information for the opposing party and the
panel to evaluate the assertion of privilege.

#### G. Production Format

Firms should produce ESI in the format reasonably most useful to the
receiving party, taking into account the nature of the records, the
volume of the production, and the technical capabilities of the parties.

In most cases, this will mean production in a load-file format that
preserves metadata fields commonly used in litigation review, including
custodian, source, file path, sent and received dates, and message
identifiers for email and instant-messaging records.

Production solely in printed or scanned-PDF format, without accompanying
metadata, is generally not adequate and may be the subject of motion
practice.

### IV. Enforcement Considerations

FINRA's Department of Enforcement will continue to evaluate, in the context
of examinations and investigations, whether member firms have established
and maintained supervisory systems and WSPs reasonably designed to address
the firm's arbitration-related preservation and production obligations.

Findings of supervisory or recordkeeping deficiencies may result in formal
enforcement action, including the imposition of fines, censures, and
undertakings.

Separately, in the arbitration context, panels retain broad authority
under FINRA Rule 12511 to impose sanctions for discovery violations,
including monetary sanctions, adverse-inference rulings, evidence
preclusion, and, in extreme cases, default.

Nothing in this Notice limits the authority of arbitration panels to
impose such sanctions in appropriate cases.

---

### V. Implementation

This Notice is effective September 1, 2024.

Member firms should complete a review of their arbitration-response
procedures and WSPs in advance of the effective date and should be
prepared to demonstrate, in the context of any FINRA examination commenced
on or after the effective date, the steps taken to align their practices
with this guidance.

Firms with questions concerning the application of this Notice to specific
facts and circumstances are encouraged to contact the FINRA contacts
identified at the front of this Notice.
