### Feature: The Fifth Circuit's Approach to "Holder Claims" After Pinkerton Energy

**By Aaron R. Lindquist**

*Partner, Lindquist Verein PLLC, Houston*

The Fifth Circuit's decision in *Pinkerton Energy Investors v. Continental
Refining Partners*, 117 F.4th 442 (5th Cir. 2024), has reignited a
long-standing doctrinal debate about so-called "holder claims" — claims by
securities holders who, in reliance on alleged misrepresentations,
retained rather than sold or purchased securities.

The decision narrows but does not eliminate the availability of such
claims under Texas state law, and creates a meaningful circuit split with
the Seventh and Ninth Circuits.

#### Background

The plaintiffs in *Pinkerton Energy* — limited partners in a Houston-based
midstream master limited partnership — alleged that they retained their
LP units during the 2019-2021 period in reliance on management's public
statements about long-term contracted volumes, only to suffer substantial
losses when those contracts were not renewed and the partnership was
forced into a distressed restructuring.

The federal securities claims were dismissed on the pleadings as
time-barred.

The plaintiffs proceeded on common-law fraud and breach-of-fiduciary-duty
theories under Texas law.

#### The District Court's Ruling

The district court granted summary judgment to the defendants, holding
that Texas common law did not recognize a cause of action for fraud based
on inducement to retain (as opposed to purchase or sell) a security.

The court relied principally on the absence of any Texas appellate
decision affirmatively endorsing holder claims, and on the Restatement
(Second) of Torts' general restriction of fraud claims to the
purchase-or-sale context.

#### The Fifth Circuit's Reversal

The Fifth Circuit reversed in part and affirmed in part, holding that
Texas law does recognize holder claims in limited circumstances —
specifically, where the plaintiff can show "actual, contemporaneous
reliance" on a specific misrepresentation in deciding to retain the
security, and where the plaintiff would have had a realistic ability to
sell or otherwise dispose of the security at a higher price had the
misrepresentation not been made.

#### Significance

The decision is significant for several reasons.

First, it represents the first Fifth Circuit pronouncement on the
viability of holder claims under Texas law, an issue on which the Texas
state courts had been notably silent.

Second, the "realistic ability to sell" element introduces a quasi-loss-
causation requirement that may significantly limit the cases in which
holder claims are viable, particularly in the LP and other illiquid-
security contexts where exit options are constrained.

Third, the decision creates a meaningful split with the Seventh Circuit's
decision in *Yelich v. Adamson Holdings*, 79 F.4th 1102 (7th Cir. 2023),
which held that Illinois common law categorically does not recognize
holder claims, and with the Ninth Circuit's decision in *Krannich v.
Carrera Therapeutics*, 88 F.4th 1411 (9th Cir. 2024), which recognized
holder claims under California law without the "realistic ability to sell"
element.

A petition for certiorari is reportedly being prepared in
*Pinkerton Energy*, and the Committee will report on its disposition in
the next issue.

### Recent Developments in SEC Enforcement: The "Sweep Examination" Toolkit

**By Genevieve B. Polk-Daniels**

*Senior Counsel, Polk Daniels Strategies LLP, Washington, D.C.*

The Securities and Exchange Commission's increased reliance on the
so-called "sweep examination" — coordinated examinations of multiple
registrants on a common topic, often launched simultaneously across the
registrant population — has continued to be a defining feature of the
agency's enforcement approach in late 2024 and early 2025.

Recent sweeps have addressed, among other topics:

(i) the use of artificial-intelligence-related disclosures by investment
    advisers ("AI washing");

(ii) the supervision of registered representatives' use of personal-device
     communications for firm business;

(iii) the accuracy of marketing materials by private-fund advisers under
      the amended Marketing Rule (Rule 206(4)-1 under the Investment
      Advisers Act); and

(iv) the application of the Custody Rule to digital-asset holdings.

#### Practical Considerations

Counsel representing registrants subject to a sweep examination should
bear several considerations in mind.

First, sweep examinations frequently feed directly into enforcement
referrals.

Unlike routine cycle examinations, where the examination staff retains
broad discretion to resolve findings informally, sweep examinations are
typically conducted with enforcement-style documentation rigor and with
the expectation that some subset of examined registrants will be referred
to enforcement.

Second, the document requests issued in sweep examinations are typically
templated across registrants, which means that responses are likely to be
compared across the registrant population.

Registrants that provide unusually narrow responses — or that take
unusually aggressive positions on the scope of the document request — may
flag themselves for follow-up.

Conversely, registrants that provide unusually voluminous responses may
inadvertently disclose information that would not otherwise have been
obtained.

Third, the "no-action" disposition is increasingly rare in the sweep
context.

Registrants that successfully resolve sweep findings without enforcement
action typically do so through deficiency letters that include
undertakings, rather than through clean closing letters.

Counsel should counsel clients accordingly.

The current administration's announced enforcement priorities for fiscal
year 2025 suggest that sweep examinations will continue to be a meaningful
tool for the Division of Examinations through at least the next two years.

### Practice Note: Recent Trends in FINRA Customer Arbitration Awards

**By Beatriz Mendoza-Lim**

*Counsel, Mendoza Lim Avocats PLLC, San Diego*

Statistics compiled from FINRA Dispute Resolution Services for the
calendar year 2024 reflect several notable trends in customer arbitration
outcomes.

First, the overall customer recovery rate — defined as the percentage of
customer claims that result in an award of damages to the claimant — was
42.6% in 2024, up from 39.1% in 2023 and 37.8% in 2022.

This represents the highest customer recovery rate since 2009.

Second, the median compensatory damages award in cases where customers
prevailed was approximately $187,000, up modestly from $174,000 in 2023.

Third, the proportion of cases resolved at hearing (as opposed to by
settlement) declined to 11.4%, the lowest figure on record.

The remaining cases were resolved by settlement (74.2%), withdrawal (8.9%),
or other procedural disposition (5.5%).

Fourth, cases involving alternative investments — including non-traded
REITs, private placements, and structured products — continued to account
for a disproportionate share of customer recoveries.

Approximately 18.7% of customer awards in 2024 involved alternative-
investment claims, despite such claims constituting only approximately
11.2% of new case filings.

Fifth, the use of expungement requests by registered representatives in
connection with customer settlements has come under continued scrutiny.

The amended Rule 12805, which took effect in October 2023, has
substantially reduced the rate at which expungement requests are granted
by FINRA panels.

Pre-amendment grant rates exceeded 80%; post-amendment grant rates are
reportedly running closer to 55%, though FINRA has not yet released formal
statistics.

The decline in expungement grants has prompted a meaningful uptick in
court challenges to FINRA's expungement procedures, including a recent
decision by the Southern District of New York declining to vacate a
panel's denial of an expungement request on jurisdictional grounds.

*Matter of Calderon-Reyes*, No. 24-cv-3814 (S.D.N.Y. Jan. 28, 2025).

### Book Review

**By Tomasz Westmore**

*Associate, Castellan & Patel LLP, New York*

**Reviewed:** *The Disinterested Mind: Neutrality, Adjudication, and the American Securities Industry*, by Helena Forsythe. (Yale University Press, 2024, 412 pp., $32.00.)

Helena Forsythe's *The Disinterested Mind* is the most ambitious history
of FINRA arbitration — and its predecessor regimes — that has yet been
written.

Forsythe, a Stanford-trained historian who now teaches at the University
of Michigan Law School, draws on previously unexamined archival material
from the NASD's records, the New York Stock Exchange's arbitration files,
and the personal papers of several mid-twentieth-century arbitrators to
construct a layered account of how the securities industry's internal
dispute-resolution apparatus evolved from an informal exchange-floor
process in the 1920s into the highly proceduralized FINRA system of the
present day.

#### Central Argument

Forsythe's central argument is provocative but ultimately persuasive: that
the system's reliance on industry-affiliated arbitrators — a feature that
has been progressively dismantled over the last two decades — was not, as
is often supposed, a deliberate scheme to disadvantage public customers,
but rather a historical accident of the system's origins as an in-group
dispute-resolution mechanism for member firms.

The customer-protection rationale, she argues, came later, and the system
has been struggling to retrofit itself to that rationale ever since.

#### Strengths

The book's middle chapters — covering the 1987-1995 period and the
*Shearson/American Express v. McMahon* litigation — are particularly
strong.

Forsythe's account of the internal NASD debates following *McMahon* draws
on previously unreleased correspondence and offers a textured account of
an industry attempting, in real time, to convert a system designed for one
purpose into a system serving another.

#### Limitations

The book is occasionally too generous to its subjects — the chapter on the
2009 establishment of the all-public-panel option is too quick to credit
the industry's self-correction narrative — but these are minor flaws in an
otherwise indispensable contribution to the literature.

Recommended without reservation for practitioners, scholars, and
arbitrators.

### Practitioner Q&A: Class Certification After Goldman Sachs

**By Olamide Adekoya**

*Partner, Adekoya Halberstam LLP, New York*

The Supreme Court's decision in *Goldman Sachs Group, Inc. v. Arkansas
Teacher Retirement System*, 594 U.S. 113 (2021), held that defendants in
securities fraud class actions may, at the class certification stage,
introduce evidence concerning the "generic" nature of alleged
misrepresentations to rebut the presumption of price impact.

Five years on, the decision has shaped class certification practice in
ways that were not entirely predictable at the time.

This practitioner Q&A explores three of those developments.

#### Q1: Has *Goldman Sachs* meaningfully reduced class certification rates?

The empirical evidence is mixed.

Cornerstone Research's most recent annual report puts the post-*Goldman
Sachs* class certification grant rate at approximately 76%, compared to
82% in the four years preceding the decision.

That is a measurable but modest decline.

The reduction appears to be concentrated in cases involving so-called
"corrective disclosure" theories — that is, cases in which the alleged
fraud is uncovered through a partial disclosure that is then linked to a
specific stock-price decline.

#### Q2: What types of expert evidence have proven most effective?

On the defense side, expert testimony that establishes the "generic"
quality of alleged misrepresentations — by, for example, demonstrating
that similar language appears in the disclosures of comparator companies,
or that the alleged misrepresentation was qualitative rather than
quantitative — has had meaningful success.

On the plaintiff side, expert testimony tying specific corrective
disclosures to specific quantifiable price movements has remained the
standard rebuttal.

#### Q3: Are there strategic considerations for plaintiffs in pleading?

Yes.

Counsel for plaintiffs have, in some cases, narrowed the pleaded
misrepresentations to focus on the most specific, quantifiable statements
in order to reduce the surface area for "genericness" challenges at
certification.

That approach has costs — narrower misrepresentations may produce smaller
class periods or smaller damage estimates — but it may also produce
higher certification rates.
