# The Wall Street Journal

## Business & Finance | Deals

### As Strategic M&A Cools, Post-Closing Fights Heat Up

#### Indemnification claims under purchase agreements signed during the 2021-2022 deal boom are now reaching peak resolution; lawyers and insurers describe a quiet but expensive backlog of disputes shaping how the next generation of deals will be drafted

By Hilary Forsmark and Ngozi Eboh-Walters
Wall Street Journal Staff Reporters
April 8, 2025 12:01 a.m. ET

---

For three weeks in November 2021, the lawyers at Pierson Beauchamp & Frye
worked around the clock to close the $4.3 billion sale of a privately-held
industrial coatings business to a strategic acquirer based in suburban
Cleveland.

The deal, which was announced in early September and signed in mid-October,
was, by the standards of the time, neither unusually large nor unusually
fast.

It was, in the words of one lawyer who worked on it, "thoroughly typical
of the moment — too much money chasing too little time, and not nearly
enough diligence."

By the time the post-closing dust settled — meaning the working-capital
adjustment had been finalized, the escrow accounts established, and the
integration teams installed — both sides went home for what they expected
to be a quiet holiday season.

Three years later, the same lawyers are in a confidential arbitration in
midtown Manhattan, fighting over $87 million in disputed indemnification
claims arising from environmental conditions at three manufacturing sites
the acquirer says were materially misrepresented in the sellers'
disclosure schedules.

The buyer wants the entire indemnification cap.

The seller's representatives, a small group of former executives now well
into retirement, dispute that any breach occurred and have asserted
counterclaims for vexatious litigation conduct.

It is, depending on how you count, the seventy-third, or eighty-first, or
somewhere-in-that-range, large post-closing indemnification dispute that
has been initiated by acquirers in the U.S. private-strategic M&A market
over the trailing twelve months — a meaningful acceleration from the
historical baseline, according to data compiled by the law firm
Hodge Kirby LLP and shared with the Journal.

### A Backlog Coming Due

The deal boom of 2021 and early 2022 produced an unusually large cohort
of private-target M&A transactions executed under unusually compressed
timelines.

Many of those purchase agreements contained survival periods for general
representations and warranties of three years, with longer (and often
unlimited) survival for fundamental and tax representations.

Three years from the height of the boom is, by arithmetic alone, late
2024 and 2025 — precisely the moment in which acquirers are now compelled
either to bring indemnification claims or to forfeit them.

"The math was always going to produce this moment," said Quentin Hellisey,
a partner at Hodge Kirby and the author of an annual survey of
indemnification trends in private M&A.

"What is interesting is the magnitude. The 2024 claim volume is
approximately 2.1 times the rolling four-year baseline. The 2025
first-quarter pace, annualized, would put us at 2.6 times. That is
materially elevated."

Mr. Hellisey attributes the elevated pace to a combination of factors.

First, the 2021-2022 deals were, in the aggregate, less carefully
diligenced than the pre-boom and post-bust cohorts; valuation pressure
and time pressure conspired to produce diligence work product that, in
retrospect, missed things.

Second, the rep-and-warranty insurance ("RWI") market — which had been
expanding aggressively into smaller and lower-quality risks during the
boom — has tightened its claim-handling posture in response to a
deteriorating loss ratio, making it harder for acquirers to extract value
from RWI policies without an underlying dispute.

Third, the macroeconomic environment has made acquirers more sensitive to
small valuation impairments; when a deal is producing the synthetic
returns the model anticipated, post-closing claims tend to be ignored, but
when a deal is underperforming, acquirers tend to scrutinize the closing
more aggressively.

### Where the Money Is Going

The disputes themselves have, by most accounts, concentrated in a handful
of subject-matter areas.

Environmental and product-liability claims account for a meaningful
share — roughly 22% of the trailing-twelve-month claim volume, according
to Mr. Hellisey's data.

Tax-related claims, often arising from positions taken on the seller's
pre-closing returns that the IRS later contested, account for another 18%.

Customer-contract claims — typically alleging that revenue from key
customers was overstated or that long-term contracts contained termination
rights that had not been disclosed — account for approximately 17%.

The remainder is a long tail of working-capital disputes, IP-warranty
claims, employee-related claims, and miscellaneous others.

The dollar magnitudes are not trivial.

In the trailing twelve months, the median claimed amount in disputes of
this type was approximately $14.3 million, and the 90th-percentile claim
exceeded $60 million.

A meaningful subset of claims exceeds $100 million in claimed amount, with
the largest publicly-known recent dispute reportedly involving a claim of
approximately $480 million between two large industrial acquirers.

### Where the Disputes Are Resolved

The vast majority of these disputes are resolved through confidential
dispute-resolution mechanisms — most commonly arbitration before a single
neutral or a three-arbitrator panel, with the arbitrators selected from a
roster maintained by an arbitration provider (typically the American
Arbitration Association, JAMS, or the International Institute for
Conflict Prevention & Resolution).

A small subset of disputes — particularly those involving alleged fraud,
which is typically carved out of the contractual exclusive-remedy
provisions — proceed in court, most often in Delaware Chancery Court for
Delaware-domiciled targets.

The arbitrators who hear these cases tend to come from a relatively small
pool.

Several of the most prominent neutrals — the kind whose names appear in
the same set of confidential awards over and over — declined to comment
for this article, including former Delaware Chancellor Andre Bouchard
(now of JAMS), former Federal District Judge Shira Scheindlin (now of
JAMS), James R. Whitfield of Whitfield ADR Services in New York, former
Delaware Vice Chancellor Donald Parsons, and former Cravath partner
Allen Parker.

Several of them cited active engagements on the precise types of disputes
the article describes; others cited their general practice of not
speaking to the press about their adjudicative work.

Industry observers who do speak on the record describe the post-closing
indemnification arbitration practice as one of the most lucrative niches
in the broader ADR market.

"The cases are complex, the stakes are high, the parties can afford to
pay for serious neutrals, and the panels are typically empowered to issue
reasoned awards that the parties take seriously," said Margaret Lousteau,
a partner at McAvoy Pierce who has represented both buyers and sellers in
disputes of this kind.

"If you are a neutral with the right credentials and the right
disposition, this is some of the most interesting work in commercial
dispute resolution right now."

### Implications for the Next Cycle

The current claim wave is already beginning to reshape how deals are
drafted.

Lawyers describe a discernible tightening of the contractual language
around disclosure schedules — more specificity required, more
cross-references between schedules, less reliance on materiality qualifiers.

Indemnification caps and deductibles are being negotiated more carefully
on the seller side.

Survival periods for non-fundamental representations are, in some cases,
being shortened from three years to eighteen months or two years, with
the difference effectively pushed onto RWI coverage.

The RWI market itself is undergoing meaningful repricing.

Premium rates as a percentage of policy limit, which had fallen as low as
2.4% during the height of the soft market in late 2021, are now running
around 4.3% for comparable risks, according to brokers active in the
market.

"The 2021 vintage of RWI policies is going to produce a loss ratio that
will be talked about for years," said Felix Eberhardt-Cousins, the chief
underwriting officer for Stradwell Underwriting, a specialty RWI insurer
based in Hamilton, Bermuda.

"It will be the cycle that taught the market a lesson it had forgotten."

#### Drafting Implications

Mr. Eberhardt-Cousins identified several specific drafting and
underwriting changes his firm has implemented in response to the loss
experience:

First, longer materiality scrape exclusions have been added to many
policies, narrowing the range of breaches covered.

Second, the so-called "interim breach" exclusion — covering breaches
known to the buyer between signing and closing — has been tightened.

Third, certain industries, including specialty chemicals,
upstream oil and gas, and crypto-adjacent businesses, are now subject to
significantly higher minimum retentions and tighter underwriting review.

### Counsel Considerations

For deal counsel currently structuring transactions, the implications are
several.

First, more time should be allocated for the negotiation of disclosure
schedules and for the careful documentation of the diligence record.

Disclosure schedules that contain category-level descriptions without
specific data points are increasingly being attacked as inadequate.

Second, the choice of arbitration provider and the specification of
arbitrator qualifications in the dispute-resolution clause matters more
than it used to.

Counsel report that the cost differential between disputes resolved by
qualified former judges and disputes resolved by less-experienced
neutrals can run into the hundreds of thousands of dollars in legal fees,
to say nothing of the substantive outcomes.

Third, the seller-side practice of distributing closing proceeds
immediately to selling shareholders, without holdbacks, is being
revisited.

In several recent disputes, seller representatives have struggled to
fund litigation defense costs because the proceeds were already in the
hands of shareholders who declined to contribute.

### Looking Ahead

Industry observers expect the elevated claim pace to continue through at
least the first half of 2026, as the trailing edge of the 2022-vintage
deal cohort approaches the end of its three-year survival period.

After that, the dynamics are less clear.

The 2023 and 2024 deal cohorts were substantially smaller than the
2021-2022 boom — in some quarters less than half the deal count — and
were closed under tighter diligence conditions.

Whether those vintages produce the same density of post-closing disputes
is, in the words of Mr. Hellisey, "an empirical question we will not be
able to answer until 2027 or 2028."

In the meantime, the cohort of practicing neutrals who handle these
disputes is busy.

Several of them have closed their books to new engagements through the
end of 2025.

"The good ones are full," said Ms. Lousteau.

"If you have a deal that you think is going to produce a dispute, you
should be lining up your neutrals before you sign."
