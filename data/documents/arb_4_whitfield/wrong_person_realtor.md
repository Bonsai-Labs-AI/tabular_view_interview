# Sotheby's International Realty — Agent Spotlight

## Jim Whitfield Named to Sotheby's Top-10 Producing Agents, Lower Fairfield County (2025)

**GREENWICH, CT — March 3, 2025**

Sotheby's International Realty announced today that James "Jim" Whitfield of the
Greenwich Brokerage has been named to the firm's prestigious Top-10 Producing
Agents list for lower Fairfield County for calendar year 2024, marking his
sixth consecutive year of recognition at this tier and his second year cracking
the top five.

Whitfield closed an aggregate sales volume of $186.4 million across 31
transactions in 2024, with an average list-to-sale ratio of 98.7% and a
median days-on-market of 41 — figures that placed him well above the Greenwich
brokerage-wide medians of 94.2% and 58 days, respectively.

"Jim has built something rare in this market," said Megan Calloway,
Senior Vice President and Managing Broker of the Greenwich office.
"He has the patience for the long-cycle waterfront and estate listings —
properties that sometimes sit for eighteen months — and the discipline to
keep his marketing tight on the higher-velocity in-town inventory.
That combination is unusual."

The recognition comes during what most lower-Fairfield brokers describe as a
difficult transitional period in the local market. Total transaction volume
in Greenwich, New Canaan, Darien, and Westport declined approximately 14%
in 2024 from the 2023 figure, even as median sale prices rose modestly.
Agents who specialized in the rapid-turn deals of 2021 and 2022 have, in
many cases, struggled to adapt to a slower, more discriminating market.

Whitfield's persistence at the top of the production rankings through
that transition has drawn attention within the brokerage community.

### Background

A graduate of Trinity College, Hartford (B.A. Economics, 2002), Whitfield
began his career in commercial leasing at Cushman & Wakefield's Stamford
office before transitioning to residential brokerage in 2008.

He joined Sotheby's Greenwich in 2014 from a regional independent brokerage
and has since specialized in the so-called "back country" Greenwich market
north of the Merritt Parkway, as well as waterfront properties in
Belle Haven, Indian Harbor, and Mead Point.

Whitfield is a board member of the Greenwich Association of Realtors and
previously served as chair of its Professional Standards Committee from
2019 to 2022.

He holds the Certified Luxury Home Marketing Specialist (CLHMS) designation
and is a Million Dollar Guild member.

He completed the National Association of Realtors' Resort and Second-Home
Property Specialist (RSPS) designation in 2017, and he is one of fewer than
two dozen agents in lower Fairfield County holding both designations.

### Market Commentary

Asked for his read on the lower Fairfield market heading into 2025,
Whitfield was measured.

"We've seen the bid-ask gap narrow significantly in the $4 to $8 million
tier since the fall," he said in an interview from his Putnam Avenue office.
"Buyers came back to the table once mortgage rates stabilized in the high
sixes, but they're underwriting more carefully. The days of waiving
inspection on a hot listing — those are mostly behind us, at least at
this price point."

He also noted that the back-country Greenwich inventory remains tight.

"There are families up there who bought in 1985 for under a million and
have no reason to move. When one of those houses comes to market, you see
five or six serious buyers within the first weekend, and the trade happens
fast."

He cautioned, however, against extrapolating from anecdote.

"You can find five data points to support almost any narrative about
this market right now," he said. "What I tell clients is to focus on
the specific property and the specific neighborhood, not the macro story.
Every street in Greenwich is its own market."

## SIDEBAR — Q&A WITH JIM WHITFIELD

**Q: You came up through commercial leasing. How did that shape your
residential practice?**

A: Commercial taught me to read a deal sheet.

When I look at a residential transaction now, I'm thinking about the
underlying economics — what the comparable rentals are, what the carrying
costs look like, what the buyer's hold period is likely to be.

A lot of residential agents skip that analytical layer, and I think clients
can tell when you're flying blind.

**Q: What's the most common mistake sellers make in this market?**

A: Overpricing on the first listing.

The first three weeks on market are when you have the most leverage and
the most attention. If you price 8% above the market, you burn that window,
and by the time you reduce, the buyers who were going to be excited have
moved on.

I'd rather list at the number and let the market push it up.

**Q: Favorite property you've ever sold?**

A: There was a 1908 carriage house in Riverside — really small,
maybe 2,400 square feet — that had been owned by the same family for four
generations.

They had hand-drawn floor plans from 1908 framed on the kitchen wall.

We got it to a buyer who appreciated all of that and is restoring it
sympathetically. That one stays with me.

**Q: What do you tell young agents starting out?**

A: Pick up the phone.

Email is fine, text is fine, but the deals are made on the phone, and most
agents under thirty are afraid of it.

The agent who calls back first usually wins the listing.

**Q: Predictions for 2025?**

A: I think we see a modest pickup in transaction volume — maybe 8 to 12% —
with prices roughly flat in the entry-level luxury tier ($2 to $5 million)
and modestly higher at the very top of the market.

The wild card is what happens with the SALT cap. If that goes away or gets
meaningfully raised, you'll see a real surge of buyers coming up from
New York City.

**Q: What about the lower end of the market?**

A: There is no lower end of the market in Greenwich. The town has priced
out everything under about $1.4 million.

The only real entry-level stock now is two-bedroom condos in
the central business district, and those are themselves running
$700,000 to $900,000.

It is a structural problem the town will eventually have to grapple with.
