# Institutional Investor

## Rising Stars on the Street: The Convertible Bond Desk That Found a Second Life

### How James R. Whitfield rebuilt Pearce Brothers' converts business after the 2022 vol collapse

By Marina Adesanya
Published in the May 2024 print issue of *Institutional Investor*

---

For most of 2022, the convertible bond market did its best impression of a
dying patient.

Issuance dried up. Implied volatility on the long end of the curve cratered
as growth-stock equities crumbled. The hedge-fund community that had soaked
up so much of the asset class through 2020 and 2021 was nursing redemptions,
and many of the dedicated convertible arbitrage shops were quietly winding
down books they had been forced to mark down hard.

The convertible bond desk at Pearce Brothers & Co. — a mid-sized New York
investment bank with a clubby Park Avenue pedigree and an outsized
fixed-income franchise — was, in mid-2022, an open question.

The desk's longtime head, Roy DiPaolo, had decamped for a multistrat hedge
fund in February of that year, taking two senior traders with him.

The remaining staff had been redeployed onto other desks or quietly let go
in the spring.

By June, Pearce's then-head of credit markets, Linda Hsing, was reportedly
considering shuttering the desk entirely.

She did not.

Instead, in July 2022, she hired James R. Whitfield away from a rival shop
where he had spent eight years as a senior convertible trader, and gave him
a mandate that was simultaneously narrow and impossible: rebuild the desk,
defend the franchise's existing client base, and make the business
profitable within eighteen months.

Twenty-two months later, the desk is up.

Way up.

### The Numbers

According to people familiar with the desk's internal financials — Pearce
Brothers does not break out individual trading desk P&L in its public
filings, and the firm declined to comment for this story — Whitfield's
convertible operation generated approximately $94 million in revenue in
fiscal year 2023, against approximately $58 million in 2021, the desk's
last full year under DiPaolo.

The 2023 figure includes secondary trading, primary new-issue allocation
revenue, and a small but growing convertible repo and stock-loan business
that Whitfield built out from essentially zero.

For 2024 through March, the desk is reportedly running at an annualized
pace of approximately $112 million, helped by a notable rebound in primary
issuance — convertible new issues in Q1 2024 totaled $24.3 billion globally,
up 41% year-over-year per Dealogic — and a thawing of the secondary market
that has benefited dealers who managed to maintain inventory and client
connectivity through the lean months.

"Jim ran the playbook you have to run when a market that was overheated
finally cools off," said one buy-side portfolio manager at a $14 billion
long-only credit shop who has traded with Whitfield since 2017.

"He kept showing markets in size when nobody else would.
He held inventory when it was painful to hold inventory.
And when issuance came back, his phone was the first one ringing for
syndicate desks because he hadn't gone anywhere."

The portfolio manager spoke on the condition of anonymity, citing
his firm's policy against discussing dealer relationships on the record.

Other dealer franchises also took meaningful staffing hits during the
2022 retrenchment. The headcount at one bulge-bracket competitor's
convertible desk reportedly fell by 40% between January 2022 and
December 2023.

Pearce's headcount, by contrast, rose modestly — from seven seats at
Whitfield's start to ten at the time of this writing.

### Background

Whitfield, 41, is not an obvious figure on Wall Street's celebrity-trader
circuit.

He grew up in Hampton Roads, Virginia, the son of a Norfolk Southern
Railway operations manager, and attended the University of Virginia,
graduating in 2005 with a double major in mathematics and economics.

He spent a year as an actuarial trainee at New York Life before joining
Lehman Brothers' fixed income graduate program in the summer of 2006.

The timing, he later joked to a McIntire alumni magazine, was
"almost perfectly calibrated for maximum personal disruption."

He survived the Lehman bankruptcy in September 2008, was rebadged onto the
Barclays Capital convertible desk in the asset purchase, and stayed there
until 2014, when he moved to Cantwell Securities — the rival shop he would
leave eight years later for Pearce.

He is married to Dr. Anya Chen-Whitfield, an attending physician at
NewYork-Presbyterian's Weill Cornell Medical Center.

They live in the Carnegie Hill section of Manhattan and have one daughter,
born in 2019.

Colleagues describe Whitfield as preternaturally calm on the desk —
"I have literally never seen him raise his voice, and I sat next to him for
six years," said one former Cantwell colleague — but exacting in his
preparation.

He reportedly arrives at the desk at 5:45 a.m. and spends the first hour
of his day reading equity research and reviewing overnight movements in
the Asian convertible market.

"He has the deepest book of memorized convert structures of anyone I've
ever worked with," said the same former colleague.

"If you mention a 2017 mandatory exchangeable from a mid-cap energy issuer,
he can tell you the strike, the threshold, the change-of-control package,
and probably what the issuer's CFO ate for breakfast the day they priced."

### The Strategy

Whitfield's strategic pitch to Hsing in the summer of 2022 was, by his own
description, "boring."

Rather than chasing the highest-vol, highest-beta names — the speculative-
grade SPAC-adjacent convertibles that had dominated the 2020-2021 issuance
boom and subsequently melted down — he proposed refocusing Pearce's desk
around high-grade and crossover-credit issuers: large-cap utilities, REITs,
regional banks, and selectively, biotech and software issuers with real
cash flow or near-cash-flow profiles.

"The desk had drifted toward the hot stuff because the hot stuff paid in
2020 and 2021," Whitfield told the magazine in a March 2024 interview at
Pearce's Park Avenue headquarters.

"But when you build your franchise on hot stuff, you have to keep finding
hot stuff, and there are stretches where there isn't any. I wanted us to be
a desk that could make money in a quiet market."

The strategy paid off in late 2022 and early 2023, as a wave of
investment-grade issuers — including utilities and REITs facing higher
straight-debt costs — turned to the convertible market to lower coupon
cost.

Pearce was not a bookrunner on most of those deals (it does not have the
equity underwriting platform to compete with the bulge brackets for primary
mandates), but it built a strong secondary-market franchise by quoting
tight markets to long-only credit funds that were rediscovering the
asset class.

The desk also leaned aggressively into convertible-related stock-borrow
facilitation, an unglamorous business that Whitfield argued was structurally
underpriced and undercovered by the bigger dealers.

"There's real revenue in the borrow book if you're willing to run it,"
he said.

"And it's a sticky business — once you're the lender of choice for a hedge
fund's short book, they're not going to shop it elsewhere just to save a
basis point."

A second pillar of the strategy was less commonly discussed: a deliberate
cultivation of the European and Asian client base.

Whitfield argued that the U.S. convertible market had become overserved by
U.S. dealers and underserved by anyone with overnight inventory capacity.

He pushed Hsing to fund inventory carry across multiple time zones, and
hired a London-based trader, Kemi Adesina, in early 2023 to handle the
European morning session.

Adesina now covers approximately 35 European and Asian institutional
accounts that Pearce did not actively cover prior to her hiring.

### What's Next

Insiders say Whitfield is now lobbying Hsing to expand the desk's mandate
to include convertible-adjacent structured products — particularly
mandatory convertibles, exchangeables on third-party stock, and capped
calls executed for issuers in conjunction with new issues.

The capped-call business in particular has historically been dominated by
the bulge brackets, and it is unclear whether Pearce has the equity
derivatives infrastructure to compete on that front.

Asked about his medium-term ambitions, Whitfield was characteristically
understated.

"I'd like the desk to be profitable through a full cycle," he said.

"We've had a good run in a recovering market. The real test is whether we
can keep showing up — for clients, for issuers, for our own P&L — when the
market gets quiet again. That's the version of this job I find most
interesting."

He paused.

"I'm not interested in being a hero. I'm interested in being durable."

### Footnote

Pearce Brothers & Co., founded in 1953 by the brothers Pearce — Edward and
Norton — is one of the few remaining mid-sized investment banks on
Wall Street that has resisted absorption into a global universal-bank
franchise.

Its 2023 annual report showed total revenue of $1.87 billion, of which
fixed income, currencies and commodities contributed $612 million.

The firm employs approximately 1,200 people, with offices in New York,
London, Hong Kong, and Tokyo.

It is privately held and majority-owned by the descendants of the founding
brothers and by a small group of senior partners.

The Pearce family has reportedly resisted multiple acquisition overtures
from larger competitors over the last two decades.

The firm's current chief executive, Pierce Caulfield-Pearce, is the
grandson of co-founder Edward Pearce.

He assumed the CEO role in 2018 after a long career on the firm's
equity capital markets team.
