# Sundance Institute — Filmmaker Profile

## James Whitfield: The Quiet Archivist of American Jazz

**Filmmaker Profile | 2025 Sundance Short Film Program | Park City, Utah**

When James Whitfield was nine years old, his grandfather — a part-time
bandleader and full-time letter carrier in Trenton, New Jersey — handed
him a worn cardboard box containing 184 acetate transcription discs.

The discs, recorded between 1947 and 1956 at long-defunct rehearsal halls
and after-hours clubs across the Mid-Atlantic, captured musicians whose
names had never made it into the canon: trumpeters who played one session
and disappeared, vocalists who never recorded commercially, pianists who
taught school by day.

"He told me, 'These are the people who didn't get famous,'" Whitfield,
now 38, recalled in an interview at the Sundance Institute's offices in
Park City.

"'Someone should remember them.' I didn't really understand what he was
asking me to do until I was in my twenties."

What he eventually did is the body of work that has, over the last
seven years, established him as one of the most thoughtful documentary
filmmakers working in American music.

His three short films — *Tin Pan and the Ghost Notes* (2019),
*The Stenographer's Solo* (2022), and the 2025 Sundance selection
*We Listened in the Margins* — together form a kind of triptych about the
people, places, and economic structures that produced mid-century
American jazz, and about the historians, collectors, and amateur archivists
who have refused to let any of it disappear.

### *We Listened in the Margins* (2025)

His latest, *We Listened in the Margins*, is twenty-six minutes long and
tells the story of the Hyacinth Tea Room — a Black-owned music venue and
restaurant that operated in Camden, New Jersey from 1948 to 1971 —
through the lens of three of its surviving regulars, none of whom are
professional musicians, and all of whom are now in their late eighties
or nineties.

The film centers on Mavis Sutton-Reeves, 91, a retired school librarian
who attended approximately 400 nights of music at the Hyacinth between
1953 and 1968, taking handwritten notes in a series of pocket diaries.

Sutton-Reeves's diaries, twenty-three of which survive, are the only
known continuous documentary record of the venue's programming.

They sit, alongside set lists, photographs, and the handful of surviving
recordings, at the heart of Whitfield's film.

"What Mavis did, without ever calling it archival work, was the single most
important act of preservation that anyone performed for that scene,"
Whitfield said.

"She made it possible, sixty years later, for us to know who played,
when they played, what they played, and even — in some of the diaries —
what the audience response was like. Without those diaries, the Hyacinth
would essentially be lost."

The film, edited by Whitfield's longtime collaborator Anais Verma, weaves
together Sutton-Reeves's diary entries (read aloud by the actress
LaTanya Richardson Jackson), restored fragments of the surviving recordings,
and contemporary interviews with the surviving regulars and a small group
of music historians.

The cinematography, by Sayed Mokhtari, favors long, patient takes — the
camera lingering on a hand turning a page, a coffee cup being refilled,
a window slowly going dark as afternoon turns to evening.

There is no narrator.

### Critical Reception

*We Listened in the Margins* premiered in the U.S. Documentary Shorts
Competition on January 24, 2025, and received warm critical reception
from trade press.

*IndieWire*'s Eric Kohn called it "a small, exact, profoundly humane piece
of work" and noted that "Whitfield has the rarest of documentary
instincts — the willingness to get out of the way of his subjects."

*Variety*'s Tomris Laffly wrote that the film "achieves something most
music documentaries fail at: it makes you feel the silence around the
sound."

"The Hyacinth Tea Room is gone. The musicians are gone. Most of the
audience is gone. What's left is Mavis's voice, and a few minutes of
crackling tape, and the camera's patient attention. It's enough."

*The Hollywood Reporter*'s David Rooney called the film
"miniature in scale but enormous in implication" and predicted
that it would have a long life in classroom and library distribution.

The film won a Special Jury Award for "Outstanding Use of Primary
Source Material" at the festival and has been acquired by HBO Documentary
Films for streaming distribution starting in late 2025.

### Earlier Work

Whitfield's first short, *Tin Pan and the Ghost Notes* (2019), was a
14-minute essay film about the disappearance of New York's mid-century
music publishing district, framed around a single 1953 piano roll that
Whitfield purchased at a Newark estate sale.

It premiered at the Hot Docs festival in Toronto and screened subsequently
at SXSW, BAMcinemaFest, and the New York Film Festival's "Convergence"
sidebar.

The film was acquired by the Criterion Channel in 2020 and remains
available for streaming on that platform.

His follow-up, *The Stenographer's Solo* (2022), is a 31-minute hybrid
documentary about Estelle Marchetti, a Camden-based legal stenographer
who, in her off-hours during the 1950s, transcribed jazz solos by ear
and sold the transcriptions to music students through classified ads
in *Down Beat* magazine.

The film premiered at Sundance in 2022 in the U.S. Documentary Shorts
Competition and won the Short Film Jury Award for Nonfiction.

It is currently available on the Criterion Channel as part of its
"Music in Documentary" collection.

Both prior films draw on the same cardboard box of acetate transcription
discs that Whitfield's grandfather gave him in childhood.

He has digitized approximately 110 of the 184 discs to date, in
collaboration with the audio preservation lab at the Smithsonian's
National Museum of African American History and Culture, where he has been
an artist-in-residence since 2021.

### Background

Whitfield was born in Trenton in 1987 and raised primarily in the city's
Mill Hill neighborhood.

He attended the Lawrenceville School on scholarship and went on to
Wesleyan University, where he double-majored in film studies and
African American studies, graduating in 2009.

He worked for three years as an associate producer at the documentary
production company Maysles Films in New York before leaving in 2013
to pursue his own projects.

He is married to the literary translator Ines Carrasco-Whitfield and lives
in the Bedford-Stuyvesant section of Brooklyn.

He is a 2022 Guggenheim Fellow and a 2024 MacArthur Fellow.

In addition to his filmmaking, Whitfield maintains a small newsletter,
*Marginalia*, in which he publishes occasional essays on archival practice,
music history, and the economics of preservation.

The newsletter has approximately 8,200 subscribers and is supported
entirely by reader contributions.

He has also taught seminars in documentary practice at the Tisch School
of the Arts and at Wesleyan, where he was a visiting artist during the
2023-2024 academic year.

### Process

Asked about his working process, Whitfield said he typically spends two to
three years on each short film before beginning principal photography.

Most of that time is devoted to archival research and to what he calls
"earning trust" with the surviving members of the communities he documents.

"I have not made a film yet where the people in it weren't, at the start,
deeply skeptical of why I wanted to talk to them," he said.

"Mavis took eleven months to agree to be on camera. We had probably forty
hours of conversation before she said yes. That's how it should be.
The film is a small thing. The trust is the big thing."

He continued: "The mistake a lot of music documentary makes is treating
the music as the story."

"The music is almost never the story. The story is the people who made it
possible — the audiences, the club owners, the bookkeepers, the bartenders,
the people who kept the doors open and the lights on. The music is what's
left when those people are remembered. If you start with the music, you've
already lost the thread."

He acknowledged that the approach has limits, particularly when it comes
to commercial distribution.

"I am not trying to make films that fit a theatrical window. I am trying
to make films that one of Mavis's grandchildren can watch in thirty years
and feel like their grandmother was seen accurately."

"If that turns out to be commercial, fine. If it doesn't, also fine."

### What's Next

Whitfield is currently in pre-production on his first feature-length
project, a documentary tentatively titled *The Trenton Sessions*, which
will examine the broader cultural economy of mid-century Black music
venues across the Delaware Valley.

The project is supported by grants from the Ford Foundation's JustFilms
program, the National Endowment for the Humanities, and the
Sundance Documentary Fund.

He expects principal photography to begin in late 2026, with a target
festival premiere in 2028.

"It's the biggest thing I've ever taken on," he said.

"I'm honestly a little scared of it. But I think my grandfather would say
that's the right scale of fear for this work."

Whitfield's grandfather, Lemuel Whitfield, died in 2008, the year before
his grandson graduated from college.

He never saw any of the films that grew out of his cardboard box.

"He would have hated being filmed," Whitfield said.

"That is part of why I think he gave the discs to me. He understood that
there is a kind of recording that respects the subject and a kind that
violates it. He trusted me to know the difference."

"I am still trying to live up to that."
