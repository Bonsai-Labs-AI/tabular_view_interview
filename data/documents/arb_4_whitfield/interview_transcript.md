# "Twenty-Five Years In, And Still Surprised": A Conversation With James Whitfield

*The following is an edited transcript of an interview conducted on September 12, 2023, for the New York City Bar Association's ADR Committee oral-history series. The interviewer is Lauren Park, Esq.*

---

**Park:** Jim, thanks for sitting down with me. Let's start broad. You've been on the neutral side for about six years now. How do you describe what you do when you're at a cocktail party?

**Whitfield:** [laughs] I usually just say I'm a referee for grown-ups who don't want to go to court. Which is approximately accurate. More precisely, I sit as a chair or sole arbitrator on commercial cases — heavily weighted toward securities and broker-dealer matters, some M&A indemnification work, a fair amount of post-closing earn-out disputes. About sixty percent of my docket is FINRA; the rest is AAA, with a little CPR work mixed in.

**Park:** You came to ADR after a long run at Hartwell & Brennan. Twenty-five years of practice before you put the shingle out. Any regrets about not making the switch earlier?

**Whitfield:** None. I think the reason I'm useful as a neutral now is precisely because I spent that quarter-century in the trenches. I represented broker-dealers, I represented institutional claimants, I sat second chair on a couple of really ugly merger-fraud trials in Delaware Chancery. You can't fake that exposure. When a young associate stands up and tries to walk me through a Bloomberg terminal screenshot of a structured note, I already know what I'm looking at.

**Park:** Speaking of structured products — you had the Meridian award earlier this year. What's your sense of where the structured-products docket is heading?

**Whitfield:** I won't comment on a specific matter, obviously. But generally — I think we're going to see a continued surge through 2024, particularly involving the autocallable products that were aggressively marketed in 2019 and 2020. The disclosures on those products are technically adequate but functionally opaque, and arbitrators are going to keep being asked to draw lines about what "sophisticated" means. It's harder than it looks.

**Park:** What about M&A work? Earn-outs?

**Whitfield:** Earn-out disputes are a growth industry. The pandemic-era deal vintage is starting to produce litigation now. I just finished an AAA matter — I won't name the parties — where the disputed milestone was a regulatory approval that the seller said was within "commercially reasonable efforts" and the buyer said wasn't. We ended up with a forty-million-dollar award. Those cases turn on contract interpretation, but they also require you to understand the underlying business, which is why I think you're seeing more former deal lawyers and litigators with hybrid backgrounds on these panels.

**Park:** Practical question — how do parties typically engage you? Do you do a flat day rate?

**Whitfield:** It depends on the forum. FINRA and AAA cases I take at the schedule rate set by the institution. For ad hoc matters and CPR-administered cases, I bill at $1,250 an hour, with a half-day study minimum once the matter is appointed. I try to be transparent about it upfront — nobody likes a fee surprise at the end of a long case.

**Park:** Last one. Best advice for a young lawyer who wants to do what you do?

**Whitfield:** Try cases. Read awards. And — this is the unfashionable one — stay at a firm long enough to actually become a lawyer. The shortcut doesn't exist.

**Park:** Jim, thank you.

**Whitfield:** My pleasure.
