# "You Stop Wearing the Robe, You Don't Stop Thinking Like a Judge"

### An Interview with Hon. Michael Torres (Ret.)
*Conducted by Rachel Imanishi for the* California Dispute Resolution Quarterly *— Vol. 18, No. 2 (Spring 2024)*

---

**CDRQ:** Judge Torres, thank you for sitting down with us. You've now been off the bench for about four years. Does it still feel like a transition, or has the new chapter become the normal one?

**TORRES:** It's the normal one. I'll tell you, the first six months I kept reaching for the gavel out of habit, and there is no gavel in a hotel conference room in Embarcadero. But after thirty-plus years on the bench, you internalize a certain rhythm — read the papers, frame the issues, get to the point — and that rhythm translates pretty cleanly into arbitration. The cases are different, but the discipline is the same.

**CDRQ:** Many former judges say the transition is harder than they expected.

**TORRES:** I think the surprise for me wasn't the work — it was the marketing. Sitting judges don't have business cards. I had to learn how to talk to general counsel as a service provider, not as a tribunal. That took some adjustment. I lean on my associates at the Group for the administrative side. I'd rather be reading a 60-page brief than sending invoices, and at $1,450 an hour I'd like to think clients prefer it that way too.

**CDRQ:** Looking back at your bench years, what cases shaped you most?

**TORRES:** Two stand out. The *Halverson* antitrust matter — the indirect-purchaser certification — taught me a lot about how to manage a record that has more economists than fact witnesses. And on the employment side, *Reyes v. Aldritch* was instructive because it forced me to confront how mechanically courts sometimes apply the FLSA collective-action standard. Both of those have come up obliquely in arbitrations I've handled since.

**CDRQ:** Do you see meaningful differences between mediating and arbitrating?

**TORRES:** They use different muscles. As an arbitrator, I'm decisional — I'm going to write an award, and the parties want my judgment, not my therapy. As a mediator I'm trying to find the deal that everyone can hate equally. I prefer arbitration, frankly, because I'm a decision-maker by training. But I take both, and a lot of my mediations these days are med-arb hybrids.

**CDRQ:** What's the most common mistake you see from counsel in front of you?

**TORRES:** Overbriefing. People file 50 pages where 15 would do. I tell counsel up front: I read everything, but I weight the first ten pages most. If you've buried the lede, you've already lost ground.

**CDRQ:** You've also been outspoken about confidentiality norms in commercial arbitration.

**TORRES:** I think the profession needs to grow up about it. Confidentiality protects the parties, not the process. We can publish redacted awards. We can talk about doctrine. The mystique hurts us more than it helps.

**CDRQ:** Any plans to slow down?

**TORRES:** I'll be 69 in November. My calendar is full through next spring. I told my wife I'd cut back at seventy, but I've been saying that for a while.

**CDRQ:** Thank you for your time, Judge.

**TORRES:** My pleasure.
