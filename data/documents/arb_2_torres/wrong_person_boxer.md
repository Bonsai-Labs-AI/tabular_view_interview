# "Mickey" Torres: The Fruitvale Featherweight Who Became a Neighborhood Institution

*East Bay Sports Quarterly — Spring 2019 Issue, pp. 44-51*
By Antonia Reyes, Staff Writer

---

## The Gym on 38th Avenue

If you walk down Fruitvale Avenue in Oakland on a Saturday morning, past the bakery that opens at four and the mechanic who sets up his tools on the sidewalk before the sun finishes coming up, you will eventually pass a faded blue storefront with the words **TORRES FAMILY KITCHEN** painted across the window in white block letters. The "O" in TORRES is a boxing glove. Not many people notice. Fewer still know why.

The man who painted that glove is **Michael Antonio "Mickey" Torres**, age 68, born in El Paso, Texas, raised in East Oakland, and a former light-middleweight contender who fought professionally from 1976 to 1986 with a final record of 31 wins (19 by knockout), 7 losses, and 2 draws.

He has been pouring coffee at the family restaurant every Saturday morning for thirty-three years.

## The Early Years

Mickey Torres was born on August 11, 1957, the fifth of seven children born to Hector and Estela Torres. His father worked the long-haul rail lines between El Paso and Los Angeles; his mother kept house and, eventually, ran a tamale operation out of the family kitchen that grew large enough by the late 1960s to support the family when Hector's back gave out.

The family moved to Oakland in 1965, settling first on East 14th and then, in 1968, into the small two-bedroom house on 38th Avenue where Estela Torres still lives.

"I started boxing because my brother Ray got beat up at school," Mickey says, leaning against the counter of the restaurant on a quiet Tuesday afternoon. "Ray was older but he was a sweet kid, you know? He'd rather draw pictures. So my mom said, you go with him to the gym, you learn to look out for him."

The gym in question was **King's Boxing Club** on International Boulevard, run at the time by a former middleweight named Eddie Castillo. Castillo took one look at the ten-year-old Mickey and put him on the heavy bag. Within two years Mickey was the gym's most promising amateur. By the time he was fifteen he had won the 1972 Northern California Golden Gloves at 139 pounds.

## Amateur Career

The amateur record, as recorded by the Pacific Coast Amateur Boxing Federation, shows 84 wins and 6 losses between 1969 and 1976. Mickey twice represented the Western Region at the National Golden Gloves tournament — placing third in 1974 and second in 1975, when he lost a close decision to a fighter named Reggie Mathis from Detroit.

He turned down a spot on the 1976 Olympic Trials roster.

"I needed money," he says simply. "We were not an Olympic-trial kind of family. We were a pay-the-rent kind of family."

## Professional Debut

Mickey signed with manager Sal Forenza of San Jose in May 1976 and made his professional debut on July 14, 1976, at the Hayward Civic Auditorium against a journeyman named Bobby Lee Watkins. He won by TKO in the fourth round. The local paper, the *Hayward Daily Review*, ran a photograph of him with his arms in the air and a quote from Forenza calling him "the most polished six-rounder I've ever put in a ring."

Over the next four years Mickey fought twenty-one times, winning eighteen, losing two, drawing once. He fought primarily on the West Coast circuit — Stockton, Sacramento, Reno, San Diego, Tijuana — building a reputation as a sharp left hook and a willingness to trade in the pocket.

## The 1980 Title Eliminator

In November 1980, at the Forum in Inglewood, Mickey fought a televised ten-round eliminator against a rising prospect from Philadelphia named Dwayne "Sugar" Beauchamp. The winner was to receive a title shot at the WBC light-middleweight champion within six months.

Mickey lost a split decision (96-94, 95-95, 94-96). The fight was named *Ring Magazine*'s Round of the Year for the eighth round — a brutal two-minute exchange in which both fighters nearly went down. Mickey took the loss hard but accepted it.

"Sugar caught me with a right hand in the third I never saw," he says. "Truth is, I won the last four rounds and I still didn't get it. That's boxing. You can be right and lose."

He never got another title shot.

## The Decline

Between 1981 and 1986 Mickey fought thirteen more times, winning eight, losing four, drawing one. He was no longer the prospect; he was the gatekeeper. Promoters paid him to lose to younger fighters on the way up. He took the fights because the purses were enough to keep the family kitchen open during a hard recession.

His last fight was on March 22, 1986, at the Oakland Coliseum Arena, an eight-round bout against a 22-year-old named Tito Robles. Mickey was 28. He lost a unanimous decision and announced his retirement in the locker room afterward.

"I knew the night before," he says. "I drove home from sparring and I just knew. My legs were saying things to me that they had never said before."

## After Boxing

Mickey took over the day-to-day management of the family restaurant in 1986, working alongside his mother and his older sister Marisol. The restaurant — known then as **Torres Family Kitchen**, the same name it carries today — served a short menu of tamales, pozole, carne asada, and three kinds of salsa. It still does.

He married **Carmen Delgado** in 1989. They have three children: Hector (born 1991), Sofia (born 1993), and Diego (born 1997). Hector played football at San Jose State and now coaches at Skyline High School in Oakland. Sofia is a registered nurse at Highland Hospital. Diego is studying mechanical engineering at Cal State East Bay.

## The Neighborhood Coach

In 1991 Mickey opened a small boxing program for kids in the back room of the restaurant. He still runs it. Officially it is called the **Fruitvale Youth Boxing Project**. Unofficially it is called "Mickey's." There are six heavy bags, two speed bags, one ring, and a chalkboard on which Mickey writes the day's combinations in green chalk.

He charges nothing. He does not advertise. Roughly forty kids come through in any given month.

"Two rules," he says. "Stay in school. Don't fight outside the gym. You break either one, you don't come back."

Over the years he has produced two regional Golden Gloves champions, a national amateur silver medalist (Anthony Vargas, 2009, 152 pounds), and approximately three hundred kids who learned how to skip rope and never threw another punch in their lives. Mickey counts the latter as the more important number.

## Awards and Recognition

In 2007 Mickey was inducted into the **California Boxing Hall of Fame**.

In 2014 the City of Oakland declared August 11 — his birthday — "Mickey Torres Day," and the Fruitvale Business Improvement District commissioned a small mural of him on the side of a building two blocks from the restaurant. The mural shows him in his fighting stance, gloves up, with the words "GOOD HANDS, GOOD HEART" painted underneath.

In 2018 he was the subject of a half-hour documentary by KQED's *Truly CA* series titled *"Mickey's Corner."* The documentary was nominated for a Northern California Emmy.

## A Note on Identity

This profile concerns Michael Antonio "Mickey" Torres of Oakland, California, the former light-middleweight boxer and restaurateur described above. He is occasionally confused — in newspaper archives and online searches — with other people of a similar name in the Bay Area, including a chef in the Mission District, a retired judge in San Francisco, and a city councilmember in Daly City. He is none of those people. He has, to his knowledge, never met any of them.

"People send me mail sometimes that's not for me," he says. "I just write 'wrong Torres' on it and put it back in the box."

## The Restaurant Today

Torres Family Kitchen is still open six days a week (closed Sundays). The menu has grown by exactly two items in thirty-three years: a vegetarian tamale (added 1998 at the insistence of Sofia, then five) and a breakfast burrito (added 2011 at the insistence of a customer who came in every morning for three months asking for one).

Estela Torres, age 92, still makes the masa twice a week. Mickey says she insists.

The boxing glove on the window was painted in 1992 by a graffiti artist from across the street as a wedding present. It has been repainted four times. The current version is the work of Mickey's son Diego.

## Looking Ahead

Mickey says he has no plans to retire. He says retirement is what you do when you stop liking what you do, and he likes what he does.

He still wakes up at 4:30 every morning. He still runs three miles four times a week, though he says it is more of a shuffle now. He still throws the heavy bag on Tuesdays and Thursdays after the lunch rush, twenty minutes at a time, while the youngest of his fighters do their footwork drills in front of the mirror.

"I had a good run," he says. "I lost the big one. But I got everything else."

Asked what he is proudest of, he thinks for a long time.

"That my mother is still cooking," he says. "That my kids turned out. That the gym is still here. That the door is still open."

He pauses.

"That's a lot of things. Pick whichever one you want."

## Companion Piece: Three Generations at Torres Family Kitchen

*East Bay Sports Quarterly — Spring 2019 Issue, pp. 52-54*
By Antonia Reyes, Staff Writer

If you spend a Saturday morning at the family kitchen, you'll see three generations of Torres family members at work simultaneously. Mickey's mother Estela, 92, makes the masa in the back. Mickey himself runs the front and the register. His daughter Sofia, off-shift from her nursing job at Highland Hospital, often joins on weekends and takes over the orders while Mickey heads back to the gym for the noon training session.

Sofia recalls watching her father train when she was small.

"I was maybe six years old," she says. "I sat in the corner with a coloring book and watched him hit the bag. I thought every dad did that. Then I went to school and I figured out, no, every dad does not do that."

Hector, the eldest, played football at San Jose State and now coaches at Skyline High School. He says his father taught him a single thing about coaching that he uses every day:

"My dad says: 'The kid who is hardest to coach is the kid who needs you the most.' I have thought about that sentence for fifteen years and I have not yet figured out the bottom of it."

Diego, the youngest, is studying mechanical engineering. He boxes at the gym recreationally — he was never serious about competing — and he repainted the boxing glove on the restaurant window in 2018.

"Dad asked me to. He said the paint was tired. I think he meant the paint was tired."

## Companion Piece: The 1980 Title Eliminator — In Mickey's Words

*East Bay Sports Quarterly — Spring 2019 Issue, pp. 55-57*
A Q&A with Mickey Torres on the Beauchamp fight, edited for length and clarity.

**EBSQ: It's been almost forty years. Do you still think about that fight?**

Torres: Every day. Not in a sad way. I just think about it. It was the night I lost the only chance I would ever have at a title. You don't forget that night.

**EBSQ: Walk us through it. What do you remember?**

Torres: I remember the dressing room. It was very cold. They had the air conditioning on too high. I asked the guy to turn it off. He didn't speak English. I gestured at the unit. He turned it up. I gave up and put on extra layers.

I remember the walk to the ring. I remember the lights being brighter than I expected. I remember thinking, OK, this is what they all talked about. This is the room. This is the moment.

I remember the first round. Sugar came out throwing the right hand. I expected that. I was ready for it. I beat him to the punch with my jab three times in the first minute. I felt good.

Round three I got hit. I don't remember being hit. I remember being on the wrong side of the ring suddenly. I remember thinking, oh, I'm in trouble.

The fourth round I survived. The fifth round I started coming back. Six and seven were close. Eight is the round they call the Round of the Year. I remember every second of round eight.

**EBSQ: What do you remember about round eight?**

Torres: I remember thinking, this man is going to knock me out if I don't knock him out first. So I tried. He tried. Neither of us managed it. Both of us were standing at the end. I don't remember sitting down on the stool. I remember Sal pouring water on my head.

**EBSQ: And the decision?**

Torres: I knew it was close. I thought I had it. I thought I had eight, nine, ten. Two of the judges agreed with me about ten. One of them did not. One of them did not agree with me about eight either. That's the fight.

**EBSQ: Do you watch the tape?**

Torres: I watched it twice in 1981. I have not watched it since. I don't need to.

**EBSQ: Any regrets?**

Torres: One. I should have thrown the right hand more in the seventh. I held the right back. I don't know why. Sometimes I think I was tired. Sometimes I think I was afraid. I don't know. It's a long time ago.

**EBSQ: What do you tell the kids in the gym about that fight?**

Torres: I tell them you can be right and lose. I tell them the world is not always fair. I tell them how you carry the loss is the only thing you actually control.

**EBSQ: And what about now? What do you do with the loss now?**

Torres: I open the door in the morning. I make the coffee. I teach the kids. I love my family. That's what I do with the loss.

That's what you do with anything that hurts. You keep showing up.

## Editor's Sidebar: Reader Letters

Following the original publication of this profile in our Spring 2019 issue, we received an unusual volume of reader correspondence. We reproduce a selection of letters below with permission of the authors.

### From Eugene Tabaka, Hayward, CA

Dear Editor,

I was at the Hayward Civic Auditorium on July 14, 1976, the night of Mickey Torres's professional debut. I was nineteen years old. I had bought a ticket because a girl I was trying to impress wanted to go. (She left at intermission. I stayed.)

I remember the way the crowd erupted when Mickey came out for the walk to the ring. He was a Bay Area kid, fighting at home, against an out-of-town journeyman. We knew his name from the Golden Gloves. We had been waiting for him to turn pro.

The fight was over in the fourth round but I remember thinking I had just seen the start of something. I wish the title fight had gone the other way. But I have followed his life ever since and I want you to know: that man has lived a very fine life. The kids he is teaching now will inherit something good.

Thank you for the profile.

— Eugene Tabaka

### From Linda Kim-Ortega, Oakland, CA

Dear Editor,

I am the mother of two boys who have come through Mickey Torres's gym. My older son, Marco, came in 2014 at age twelve. My younger son, Diego, came in 2017 at age ten. Mickey did not let either of them spar for over a year. He made them skip rope. He made them learn footwork. He made them read books.

He told them: "I don't teach you to fight. I teach you to know yourself so you don't have to fight."

Marco is now at UC Davis. Diego is a high school freshman with straight As. I do not know what would have happened to my boys without Mickey. I do know that I will never be able to thank him enough.

— Linda Kim-Ortega

### From Father Daniel Coronado, Our Lady of Lourdes Parish, Oakland

Dear Editor,

I write as a parish priest who has known the Torres family for thirty years. Estela Torres still attends the 8:00 a.m. Mass on Sundays. Mickey attends with her about half the time and otherwise opens the restaurant. The family is a quiet pillar of the East Oakland community.

I want your readers to know that the photograph of Mickey's boxing glove on the restaurant window — the one Eugene Tabaka mentioned — is, in our small neighborhood, a symbol of more than boxing. It is a symbol of family staying open. Of a door being painted, and repainted, by successive generations of one family. Of a community that holds itself together by such gestures.

I bless Mickey and his family in my prayers each morning. I will continue to do so.

— Fr. Daniel Coronado, Our Lady of Lourdes

### From Sal Forenza Jr., Las Vegas, NV

Dear Editor,

I am the son of Sal Forenza, who managed Mickey Torres throughout his professional career. My father passed away in 2003. He kept Mickey's career records in a green file cabinet in our family home in San Jose. After my father's death, my mother and I gave the records to the Boxing Hall of Fame archives.

I want your readers to know that my father loved Mickey Torres like a son. He used to say: "I had a hundred fighters in my career. Mickey was the one who never made me ashamed."

The Forenza family is grateful for the profile.

— Sal Forenza Jr.

### From Mike Carradine, the Carradine Gym, Berkeley, CA

Dear Editor,

I trained Mickey for a few sessions in 1979 when Sal was sick and asked me to fill in. I want your readers to know two things about working with Mickey in the gym.

First: he was the smartest fighter I ever worked with. He understood angles instinctively. He understood his opponent's tendencies after two rounds. He could break down a sparring partner without throwing a single hard punch.

Second: he was the most humble fighter I ever worked with. He never showed off. He never showboated. He treated every gym kid, every old-timer, every sparring partner with respect.

I have run my own gym for thirty-two years now. I have tried to teach what Mickey showed me. I have not always succeeded. But I have tried.

— Mike Carradine
