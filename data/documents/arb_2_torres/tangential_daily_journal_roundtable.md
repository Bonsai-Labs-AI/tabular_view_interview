# Northern California ADR Roundtable Tackles Disclosure, Diversity, and the Boom in Mass Arbitration

*The San Francisco Daily Journal — Friday, October 25, 2024 — Section B, Front Page*
By Nathaniel Park-Hwang, Senior Reporter

---

## At the City Club, a Conversation about the Future

SAN FRANCISCO — On the seventh floor of the **City Club of San Francisco** on Wednesday evening, a panel of nine attorneys, four sitting judges, six retired neutrals, and the general counsel of three California companies gathered around a configuration of tables shaped to keep almost every speaker visible to almost every listener. The result was an animated, occasionally contentious, three-hour conversation about the state of alternative dispute resolution in Northern California — and where it is heading.

The event was the **2024 Northern California ADR Roundtable**, an annual gathering convened by the **Bar Association of San Francisco** in cooperation with the **Northern California ADR Forum**, the **California Lawyers Association ADR Section**, and the *Daily Journal* itself. The roundtable, now in its eleventh year, is held under a modified Chatham House Rule: speakers may be identified, but specific attribution of remarks is permitted only with the speaker's consent.

Some forty-five additional members of the bar and judiciary attended as observers, including this reporter.

## A Crowded Agenda

The agenda, distributed in printed form at the door, listed six discussion topics:

1. The arc of arbitrator disclosure requirements after *Honeycutt v. Vertix Solutions* (Cal. Ct. App., June 2024);
2. The status of mass-arbitration cost-shifting in California consumer matters;
3. The expansion of the JAMS, AAA, and Signature Resolution panels and the emerging market for retired-judge neutrals;
4. Diversity, equity, and inclusion on neutral panels;
5. The effect of AI on document review, drafting, and award preparation in the arbitration context;
6. The future of mediation confidentiality after the State Bar's 2023 study commission report.

By the end of the evening, items one through four had received considerable air time. Items five and six were briefly addressed in the final twenty minutes.

## Disclosure After Honeycutt

The roundtable opened with a thirty-minute conversation on the implications of *Honeycutt v. Vertix Solutions*, the June 2024 Court of Appeal decision that vacated a $42 million arbitration award on the grounds of the chair's failure to disclose a prior mediation involving a parent entity of the prevailing respondent.

**Hon. Cassandra L. Lin (Ret.)**, a JAMS neutral and former presiding justice of the Court of Appeal's Sixth District, called *Honeycutt* a "wake-up call disguised as an outlier." Justice Lin argued that the court's reasoning, while framed narrowly, signaled an underlying judicial impatience with neutrals who treat the California disclosure standards as a checklist rather than a continuing inquiry.

"Disclosure is a culture," she said. "If you only think about it the day the case opens, you are doing it wrong. You should be thinking about it every time a new exhibit list comes in, every time you read a witness name you didn't expect."

**Daniela Marchetti-Ruiz**, a partner at **Quinn Emanuel** who has represented parties opposing major arbitration awards, agreed but pressed further. "The standard is now, in effect, that 'I didn't realize the connection' is no longer a defense. The question is what an experienced neutral *should* have realized."

Counsel on the other side — **Adrián O. Vega**, a partner at **Kirkland & Ellis** who frequently represents prevailing arbitration parties in vacatur litigation — pushed back. "We are going to manufacture a vacatur industry," he warned. "Every losing party will now spend three months data-mining the chair's prior cases for an undisclosed connection. The cost of awards will go up. The finality of awards will go down. That is not what the FAA was designed to deliver."

The conversation continued for the better part of forty minutes before the moderator, **Prof. Marisol Echeverria-Kim** of UC Hastings, moved the group to the next agenda item.

## Mass Arbitration and the Cost Question

The mass-arbitration discussion was framed around the increasingly common practice of plaintiff-side firms filing tens of thousands of individual consumer arbitration demands against a single corporate respondent simultaneously, often with the effect of triggering very large administrative-fee obligations under the respondent's own arbitration agreement.

**Hannah Reilly-Petrov**, general counsel of the consumer-electronics company **Solstice Devices, Inc.** (which has faced a 14,000-claim mass-arbitration filing in the past 18 months) called the trend "a perfectly logical response to a regime we, the corporate defense bar, designed." She drew a small laugh from the room.

"We wanted arbitration. We got arbitration. We got it 14,000 times. Now we have to figure out how to make it work."

**Eleanor Goss-Adisa**, a partner at **Gibbs Law Group** who has represented plaintiffs in three of California's largest mass-arbitration matters, was unsentimental. "The fee schedules are what the companies wrote. If they want to renegotiate, the time to do that is in good faith with class counsel, not in a motion to compel arbitration designed to escape class treatment."

The panel discussed at length the AAA's and JAMS's experimentations with **mass-arbitration protocols**, including bellwether-track procedures and modified fee structures. **Carlos Reyes-Lim**, the AAA's regional vice president for the Pacific Coast, sketched the AAA's most recent (September 2024) protocol revisions and took questions from the room for nearly twenty minutes.

## The Panels Boom

The third topic — the rapid expansion of the major neutral-services providers' panels — produced perhaps the evening's most candid exchange.

**Hon. Rosalind Quirk (Ret.)**, who retired from the Alameda County Superior Court in 2018 and now mediates and arbitrates with **Judicate West**, observed that the practical effect of the panel expansion has been a flooding of the market with retired judges, many of whom market themselves identically and few of whom have meaningfully differentiated practices.

"There are now over three hundred and fifty retired California judges holding themselves out as full-time neutrals," Quirk said. "I doubt the market needs that many. The next five years will be a reckoning."

She added: "The retired-judge brand still carries weight. But it carries less weight than it did a decade ago. Parties are starting to ask what you actually *do* with disputes, not just what your title used to be."

Several members of the panel agreed. **Marcia Quan**, general counsel of **Bayhill Bioworks**, said her company's selection process had become considerably more granular over the past five years: "We used to pick a former judge with a good Yelp page. We now do back-channel calls, we review past awards if we can find them, and we ask about hearing room demeanor specifically. The market matured because we, the buyers, matured."

**Prof. Echeverria-Kim** asked the panel to address the specific question of how the expansion had affected diversity on the panels.

The conversation that followed lasted nearly forty-five minutes.

## Diversity on Neutral Panels

By common consensus in the room, the major providers have made measurable progress on demographic diversity at the entry tier of the panels but considerably less progress on what one speaker called the "rotation tier" — the subset of neutrals who actually receive the majority of party selections in commercial matters.

**Karima Diop-Engelhart**, who chairs the **National Association of Women Lawyers**' arbitration subcommittee, offered statistics: of approximately 1,400 neutrals on the major California-active commercial panels, women constitute approximately 31% of total listings but only 18% of selected appointments in matters with claims over $5 million. Underrepresented minority neutrals constitute approximately 17% of listings and 9% of selected appointments at the same claim-size threshold.

"The roster is one thing," Diop-Engelhart said. "The pipeline of who actually gets picked is another. We have lots of names on the roster. We have very few on the working panel."

Several proposals were canvassed during the discussion, including JAMS's pilot **"diverse striker rule"** (under which the AAA and JAMS would, in agreed circumstances, expand party-strike lists to include additional diverse candidates), corporate-side commitments to include at least one diverse candidate on every strike list, and the use of **disclosure-of-prior-selections** statistics by the major providers.

Vega from Kirkland was skeptical of the most aggressive proposals: "If I am defending a $40 million dispute, my obligation is to my client. I have to pick the neutral I think gives my client the best chance. I support a wider roster. I oppose anything that constrains the strike list."

Goss-Adisa pushed back: "What you call 'best chance' is, in practice, 'most familiar.' That is the problem we are trying to solve."

The exchange continued without easy resolution.

## A Word on Absences and Panelist Logistics

Echeverria-Kim, the moderator, noted near the end of the evening that the convening had been timed to coincide with the **Northern California ADR Forum's** broader fall programming and to involve as many of the region's senior neutrals as possible. Several anticipated panelists, she noted, had been unable to attend due to scheduling conflicts: among them **Hon. Patricia M. Reyes (Ret.)**, **Hon. Brian Hutch (Ret.)**, and **Hon. Michael Torres (Ret.)** of Torres ADR Group LLC, all of whom had been listed in earlier drafts of the program. The roundtable continued as planned in their absence.

## AI in Drafting and Document Review

In the final twenty minutes, **Prof. Echeverria-Kim** turned to the question of AI's impact on the practice. The discussion was brief but pointed.

**Justice Lin** said she had begun, very cautiously, using a commercial LLM-based tool for first-pass summary of multi-thousand-page transcript excerpts in larger matters, with careful human verification. She described the tool as "a junior associate who never sleeps and occasionally tells you something that isn't true."

**Quan** of Bayhill said her company had updated its outside-counsel guidelines in 2024 to require disclosure of any use of generative AI in drafting filings, including arbitration briefs. The provision, she said, had been largely uncontroversial in negotiation.

**Vega** worried that AI tools, if used by the neutral in award drafting, raise unsettled disclosure and waiver questions. "I do not know what I would do if I learned a panel had used an LLM to draft my client's award," he said. "I am not sure my motion to vacate writes itself. I am not sure it doesn't."

The conversation was cut short by the closing-time announcement from the City Club staff.

## Mediation Confidentiality, Briefly

In the closing minutes, **Hon. Sanjay Singh-Allard (Ret.)**, a former Contra Costa County Superior Court judge now affiliated with **ADR Services, Inc.**, briefly addressed the State Bar's 2023 study commission report on **mediation confidentiality**.

"The legislature has shown no appetite for the *Cassel v. Superior Court* fix the commission recommended," Singh-Allard observed. "Practitioners should plan accordingly. If you want a malpractice claim against your mediation counsel preserved, you may need to negotiate it expressly. The default rule has not moved."

## Coda

The roundtable ended a few minutes after 9:00 p.m. Many participants moved to an informal post-event reception in the City Club library, where, on a tray near the bar, a small printed card listed each of the evening's discussion topics and the suggested follow-up readings for each.

The 2025 Roundtable is scheduled for late October. Registration is expected to open in mid-summer.

## Postscript: Statistics and Sources

A printed handout distributed at the registration desk summarized the following statistics, drawn from the September 2024 report of the **California Lawyers Association ADR Section** and the AAA's 2023 annual report:

- Total California ADR filings, 2023: approximately **24,800** across the AAA, JAMS, ADR Services, Inc., and Signature Resolution
- Median resolution time (commercial track): **9.4 months** (down from 10.1 in 2022)
- Median resolution time (employment track): **7.3 months**
- Median award size (commercial track): approximately **$2.4 million**
- Percentage of California-seated awards subject to court vacatur motions: approximately **5.6%**
- Percentage of motions to vacate that succeed: approximately **1.4%** of awards

The handout further noted that mass-arbitration filings — defined as ten or more individual demands by the same plaintiff-side firm against a single respondent within a six-month window — had risen from an estimated **3,400 individual demands** in 2020 to **41,200** in 2023, with an estimated **53,000** projected for 2024.

## Sidebar: A Brief History of the Northern California ADR Roundtable

The Northern California ADR Roundtable began in 2014 as a small invitation-only gathering convened by then–BASF President **Tomas Lim-Cardoza** in collaboration with the Northern California ADR Forum. The first Roundtable was held at the Olympic Club and drew approximately 22 participants.

By 2018 the Roundtable had grown to roughly 50 participants and had moved to the larger ballroom at the City Club. By 2022, with the addition of a third co-sponsor (the CLA ADR Section), the Roundtable had grown to roughly 75 participants and a more formal panel format.

In 2023 the Roundtable adopted the modified Chatham House Rule under which it now operates: speakers may be identified, but specific attribution of remarks is permitted only with the speaker's consent.

The 2024 Roundtable was the eleventh in the series and the second under the modified Chatham House Rule.

## Sidebar: A Reporter's Notebook — Three Observations from the Floor

Throughout the evening I made notes that did not fit cleanly into the main reporting. Three of those notes are reproduced below.

### Observation 1: The Generational Shift Is Real

Of the approximately seventy-five attorneys and judges in the room, I counted roughly eighteen under the age of forty. That is more than I have seen at any prior Roundtable. The generational shift in the California ADR community — from a profession dominated by retired-judge neutrals trained in the 1970s and 1980s to a profession increasingly populated by younger practitioners — is becoming visible.

The younger attendees, in conversation at the post-event reception, were notably more interested in AI tools, mass-arbitration economics, and the structural questions raised by the diversity discussion than in disclosure doctrine or the case-law of vacatur. This is unsurprising. But the generational gap between what the senior practitioners want to discuss and what the junior practitioners want to discuss is now wide enough that a future Roundtable might productively break into two simultaneous tracks.

### Observation 2: The Corporate Counsel Are Driving the Market

I was struck by how forcefully Marcia Quan of Bayhill Bioworks and Hannah Reilly-Petrov of Solstice Devices spoke during the panel diversity and panel-expansion discussions. The corporate counsel in the room were not a passive consumer base. They were articulating very specific demands: better metrics, more transparent disclosure of selection statistics, more accountability for the major providers.

This is, I think, the under-reported story of the past three years in California ADR. The supply side of the neutral market has been the focus of trade-press coverage. The demand side — corporate America, increasingly sophisticated and increasingly willing to use its leverage — is reshaping the market more aggressively than the supply side knows.

### Observation 3: The Absent Speakers Got Discussed Anyway

Several of the practitioners on the published-but-not-attended list — Hon. Patricia M. Reyes (Ret.), Hon. Brian Hutch (Ret.), and Hon. Michael Torres (Ret.) of Torres ADR Group LLC — were mentioned only once in the official discussion, in the moderator's housekeeping notes early in the evening. None of their absences became substantive topics.

This is, I think, the right outcome. The Roundtable is not about which neutrals are most prominent; it is about the structural questions facing the profession. The convening organizations should be commended for keeping the discussion at that altitude.

## Sidebar: Glossary for the General Reader

For readers without a deep background in ADR practice, a brief glossary of terms used in this article:

- **ADR (Alternative Dispute Resolution):** The umbrella term for processes — primarily arbitration and mediation — used to resolve disputes outside court.

- **Arbitration:** A private adjudicative process in which a neutral or neutrals decide a dispute based on evidence and argument presented by the parties. The result is, with very limited exceptions, binding.

- **Mediation:** A facilitated negotiation process in which a neutral helps the parties reach their own agreement. The result is binding only if the parties so agree.

- **Neutral:** The professional (arbitrator, mediator, or both) selected by the parties to resolve their dispute.

- **Panel (provider sense):** The roster of neutrals affiliated with a major ADR provider (AAA, JAMS, ADR Services, Inc., Signature Resolution, etc.).

- **Panel (case sense):** The single arbitrator or three-person panel assigned to a particular case.

- **Vacatur:** A court proceeding to overturn an arbitration award. The grounds are very limited under the Federal Arbitration Act (9 U.S.C. § 10) and similar state statutes (e.g., California's CCP § 1286.2).

- **Disclosure:** The obligation of a neutral to disclose any matter that might create a reasonable impression of bias or any prior relationship with the parties or counsel. The California disclosure obligations under CCP §§ 1281.9 and 1281.85 are among the most rigorous in the United States.

- **Mass arbitration:** The practice of filing tens or hundreds or thousands of individual arbitration demands against a single respondent simultaneously, often with the effect of triggering large administrative-fee obligations.

- **Strike list:** A list of candidate neutrals from which the parties strike candidates they object to and rank the remaining candidates. The neutral with the lowest combined rank is typically appointed.

## Sidebar: Three Questions for the Field

Based on the Roundtable's discussion, three questions seem to me to merit further work by the field:

**1. What does a workable disclosure standard look like after *Honeycutt*?** The case has been widely read as a tightening of disclosure obligations. But the operational question — how does a busy neutral, with a hundred or two hundred prior matters, actually run a disclosure check that meets the new standard? — has not been resolved. The major providers' compliance infrastructure will need to catch up.

**2. How should the major providers respond to mass-arbitration filings?** Both AAA and JAMS have published mass-arbitration protocols. But the protocols differ. The lack of standardization creates strategic forum-shopping opportunities for both plaintiff-side firms and corporate respondents. A coordinated approach across providers — perhaps facilitated by the State Bar or the CLA — would benefit the field.

**3. How can the corporate purchasers of ADR services drive panel diversity without running afoul of constitutional or statutory anti-discrimination concerns?** The corporate counsel commitments referenced during the discussion — to include diverse candidates on every strike list, for example — are constructive but raise legal questions of their own. The field would benefit from a clear, well-reasoned analysis of what is, and is not, permissible.

These are not questions for journalism. They are questions for the profession. The Roundtable was, I think, a good first step.

## Companion Piece: A Brief History of California ADR Practice

For readers who would like broader context on the California ADR field as it stands today, a brief survey of its history.

### The Pre-Modern Era (Before 1985)

Before 1985, alternative dispute resolution in California was a relatively niche practice. Arbitration was used in construction and labor contexts but rarely in commercial or consumer matters. Mediation was largely the province of family-law practitioners and a small group of community-organizing-rooted neutrals.

The California Code of Civil Procedure's Title 9 (the predecessor of the modern arbitration provisions) had been on the books since 1961, but its use was modest. The Federal Arbitration Act, enacted in 1925, had not yet been read by the Supreme Court to apply broadly in state-court arbitration matters.

### The Pre-emption Decade (1985-1995)

The U.S. Supreme Court's decision in *Southland Corp. v. Keating*, 465 U.S. 1 (1984), holding that the FAA applies in state courts and pre-empts state law inconsistent with arbitration, transformed the landscape. The 1985 decision in *Mitsubishi Motors v. Soler Chrysler-Plymouth*, 473 U.S. 614, opened international commercial arbitration in the United States. The 1991 decision in *Gilmer v. Interstate/Johnson Lane Corp.*, 500 U.S. 20, opened employment arbitration.

By 1995, arbitration had become a major feature of American commercial and employment practice. California's ADR community grew correspondingly.

### The Growth Era (1995-2010)

The 1995-2010 period saw the emergence of the major ADR providers (JAMS, founded in 1979; AAA's expansion of West Coast presence in the 1990s; ADR Services, Inc., founded 1989) as significant institutional players in California. The retired-judge model — pioneered by JAMS — established itself as the dominant supply-side structure for high-end commercial work.

The State Bar's adoption of the **Ethics Standards for Neutral Arbitrators in Contractual Arbitration** in 2002 created what remains among the strictest disclosure regimes in the United States.

### The Contestation Era (2010-2020)

The decade of the 2010s saw the Supreme Court's *AT&T Mobility v. Concepcion* (2011) and *American Express v. Italian Colors* (2013), which broadly enforced class-action waivers and arbitration agreements in consumer and small-business contexts. California responded with a series of legislative attempts to limit arbitration, most of which were struck down on FAA-preemption grounds.

The decade also saw the *Iskanian* decision (2014) and its eventual partial abrogation in *Viking River Cruises v. Moriana* (2022), the rise of mass arbitration as a strategic response by plaintiff-side firms, and growing attention to the diversity of the neutral profession.

### The Current Era (2020-Present)

The current era is characterized by:

- The fast-growing supply of retired-judge neutrals (the supply-side expansion mentioned at the Roundtable)
- The fast-growing sophistication of corporate counsel as consumers of ADR services (the demand-side maturation also mentioned at the Roundtable)
- The integration of AI tools into legal and ADR practice
- The ongoing pressure for greater panel diversity
- The continued tension between arbitration as a feature of the commercial marketplace and arbitration as a feature of employment and consumer contracts

The 2024 Roundtable is, in many ways, a snapshot of where this era currently stands. Whether the field will move toward consolidation, fragmentation, or transformation remains to be seen.
