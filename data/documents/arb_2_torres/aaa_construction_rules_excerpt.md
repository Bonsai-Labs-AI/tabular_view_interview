# American Arbitration Association
## Construction Industry Arbitration Rules and Mediation Procedures
### Practitioner Manual — Selected Sections with Commentary

*Excerpt from the AAA-ICDR Practice Manual, Section 7 ("Construction Industry Rules — Annotated"), 2024 Edition. Reproduced under license. Not legal advice. Refer to the current published Rules at adr.org for the controlling text.*

---

## Introduction to Section 7

The American Arbitration Association first promulgated dedicated **Construction Industry Arbitration Rules** in 1966, in response to growing demand from the construction sector — and particularly from the American Institute of Architects, the Associated General Contractors of America, and the American Subcontractors Association — for a procedural framework calibrated to the realities of construction disputes. The Rules have been amended on average every five to seven years since, with the most recent amendments effective **March 1, 2024**.

This Section reproduces selected key Rules with brief practitioner commentary, but does not substitute for direct consultation of the published Rules. Practitioners are reminded that the AAA's published Rules are amended periodically and that the version in force at the time of the demand controls.

---

## R-1. Agreement of Parties; Standard, Procedures for Large, Complex Construction Disputes

### Rule Text (Summary)

The Construction Industry Arbitration Rules apply whenever the parties have agreed to arbitration under the AAA's Construction Industry Rules. Where the disputed amount exceeds **$500,000** exclusive of interest, attorneys' fees, and costs (or where the parties stipulate), the **Procedures for Large, Complex Construction Disputes** ("LCC Procedures") apply in addition to the regular Rules.

### Practitioner Commentary

The LCC Procedures are not optional in qualifying matters. They impose additional case-management requirements, including:

- A **preliminary management hearing** within **fifteen (15) calendar days** of the appointment of the panel;
- The appointment of a **three-arbitrator panel** as the default (subject to party agreement to the contrary);
- A presumption in favor of a **reasoned award**, written and dated, rendered no later than thirty (30) days after the close of the hearing.

Where the disputed amount is uncertain at the time of filing — a common circumstance in payment-cascade construction disputes — the practitioner should plead the claim under both tracks and request a case-management ruling on track designation at the earliest opportunity.

## R-2. AAA and Delegation of Duties

The AAA, in administering an arbitration, shall be responsible for the proper application of the Rules and the proper appointment of the panel. The AAA's Construction Division operates from offices in **New York, Atlanta, Dallas, and Los Angeles**, and is staffed by case managers with subject-matter training in construction practice.

### Footnote 7.2: AAA Case Manager Roles

A AAA case manager is not an arbitrator and does not decide substantive issues. The case manager's role is logistical: scheduling, fee collection, panel rotation, and communication facilitation. Practitioners should not direct substantive correspondence to the case manager.

---

## R-3. National Roster of Arbitrators

### Rule Text (Summary)

The AAA maintains a **National Roster** of approximately 4,800 arbitrators, of whom approximately 1,200 are credentialed on the **Construction Panel**. A subset of approximately 240 arbitrators is further credentialed on the **Large, Complex Case Panel**.

Construction Panel credentialing requires (i) a minimum of fifteen years of construction-related professional experience; (ii) demonstrable arbitration experience or completion of the AAA's Construction Arbitrator Training program; and (iii) successful completion of a periodic re-credentialing review.

### Practitioner Commentary

The practical effect of the credentialing structure is that party-list selection in construction matters draws from a smaller and more specialized pool than in commercial matters generally. Practitioners should not assume that an arbitrator's listed availability on the National Roster signals construction credentialing.

## R-7. Initiation Under an Arbitration Provision in a Contract

### Filing Demand

The claimant initiates arbitration by filing a written **Demand for Arbitration** with the AAA, accompanied by (i) the applicable filing fee per the fee schedule in effect on the filing date; (ii) a copy of the arbitration agreement; and (iii) a description of the dispute, the relief sought, and the names of the parties.

### Service

The AAA serves the Demand on the respondent by **certified mail, return receipt requested**, and provides the respondent with **fourteen (14) calendar days** to file a written response. The response is not pleaded in the strict civil-procedure sense; the AAA does not require a formal answer with affirmative defenses.

---

## R-10. Administrative Conference

Within fourteen (14) days of the filing of a Demand, the AAA case manager will convene an **administrative conference** with the parties (or counsel) to discuss:

- The applicable Rules track (regular, LCC, or the "Fast Track" for disputes under $100,000);
- Locale designation;
- The panel size (one or three);
- Preferred dates for the preliminary management hearing.

### Footnote 7.10: Locale Disputes

Where the parties have not agreed on a locale, the AAA will determine the locale considering: (i) the location of the project; (ii) the location of the parties and counsel; (iii) the location of relevant witnesses; (iv) the location of relevant documents; and (v) any locale provisions in the underlying contract. Locale disputes are heard by the AAA's administrator; the determination is administrative and not subject to interlocutory review.

---

## R-13. Direct Appointment by a Party

In a three-arbitrator construction case, the parties may have agreed in their contract to a "party-appointed" structure, in which each party directly appoints one arbitrator and the two party-appointees select the chair. The AAA defaults, however, to a **fully neutral, AAA-appointed** structure under which all arbitrators are selected from the National Roster by a party-strike process.

### Practitioner Note

The 2024 amendments emphasized the presumption of fully-neutral panels even in cases governed by older "party-appointed" contractual language, by providing that party-appointed arbitrators are subject to the same disclosure and impartiality obligations as the chair. The practitioner should not assume the kind of "advocate-arbitrator" structure familiar from older construction practice.

---

## R-15. Number of Arbitrators

The default panel size for matters under **$1,000,000** is one arbitrator; the default for matters between $1M and $10M is one or three by AAA determination; the default for matters over $10M is three.

These defaults are subject to party agreement.

---

## R-19. Disclosure

Each appointed arbitrator must complete a **Notice of Appointment and Disclosure Statement** within **seven (7) days** of notice of appointment. The disclosure must include:

1. Any financial, business, professional, family, or social relationships with the parties or counsel within the prior two years;
2. Any matter in which the arbitrator has previously served as a neutral involving any of the parties or counsel;
3. Any other circumstance reasonably likely to create a presumption of bias or affect the arbitrator's impartiality.

The duty of disclosure is **continuing** and extends through the rendering of the final award.

### Footnote 7.19: Standards Beyond the AAA Rules

The disclosure standards under California's **Code of Civil Procedure §§ 1281.9 and 1281.85**, and the **Ethics Standards for Neutral Arbitrators in Contractual Arbitration** promulgated by the California Judicial Council, may impose disclosure obligations beyond those of the AAA Rules. Arbitrators serving in California-seated matters must comply with the higher standard.

## R-23. Preliminary Management Hearing

Within **fifteen (15) days** of the appointment of the panel (LCC track) or **twenty-one (21) days** (regular track), the panel will convene a preliminary management hearing. The PMH agenda typically includes:

- Identification of issues and a preliminary statement of claims and defenses;
- Discussion of any anticipated dispositive motions;
- A discovery plan, including a document-exchange schedule and the scope (if any) of depositions;
- A site-visit protocol, if a site visit is anticipated;
- Identification of expert witnesses and any phased exchange of expert reports;
- Scheduling of the merits hearing;
- Discussion of the form of the final award.

### Practitioner Commentary

The PMH is the single most important procedural event in a construction arbitration. Practitioners who treat it as administrative invite avoidable cost and delay. Pre-PMH meet-and-confer between counsel is strongly recommended, and the proposed scheduling order should be filed in joint form, with disputed items clearly marked.

---

## R-24. Discovery; Information Exchange

The Construction Rules contemplate a **proportional** approach to discovery. The default scope is the exchange of documents on which a party relies for its claims or defenses, plus targeted document requests. Depositions are not assumed and are typically limited (e.g., one to three per side) absent good cause shown.

### Footnote 7.24: E-Discovery

Where electronic information is significant — which in construction practice has become the rule rather than the exception, given email-heavy project files and BIM (Building Information Modeling) data — the panel will typically issue an **ESI protocol** at the PMH, drawing on the Sedona Conference's *Commentary on Proportionality in Electronic Discovery*.

---

## R-31. Evidence

The arbitrator(s) shall determine the admissibility, relevance, and materiality of evidence. The strict rules of evidence are **not applicable**. The arbitrator may receive hearsay, opinion evidence, and evidence in summary form, weighing each according to its reliability.

### Practitioner Commentary

Notwithstanding the relaxed evidentiary standards, panel-experienced counsel will continue to lay foundation for expert testimony and to object to demonstrably unreliable evidence. The relaxed standards govern *admissibility*; they do not preclude argument about *weight*.

---

## R-39. Time of Award

The panel shall render the final award **no later than thirty (30) days from the date of the close of the hearing**, or — if briefs are submitted post-hearing — thirty (30) days from the submission of the briefs.

### Footnote 7.39: Extensions

The thirty-day deadline is subject to extension by the AAA upon a showing of good cause. In LCC matters with extensive records, extensions of an additional thirty (30) to sixty (60) days are not uncommon.

---

## R-44. Form of Award

A construction award may be (i) a **standard award** (operative provisions only); (ii) a **reasoned award**; or (iii) **findings of fact and conclusions of law**. The default in LCC matters is the reasoned award. The practitioner should clarify form at the PMH to avoid surprise at the back end of the case.

---

## R-49. Administrative Fees

### Filing Fees

The AAA's filing fee for a Construction Industry Demand is calibrated to the amount in controversy, ranging from **$1,750** for matters under $10,000 to **$15,500** for matters between $10,000,001 and $50,000,000. Matters over $50,000,000 are charged on an hourly or flat-fee negotiated basis.

### Case Service Fee

In addition to the filing fee, the AAA charges a **case service fee** payable at the time the case is scheduled for hearing. The case service fee is also calibrated to the amount in controversy.

### Footnote 7.49: Fee Allocation

The default allocation is that the parties share administrative fees equally, unless the contract or applicable law (e.g., consumer-protection statutes) requires otherwise. The panel may, in the final award, reallocate administrative fees as part of the costs of the proceeding.

---

## R-58. Fast Track Procedures (Disputes Under $100,000)

The Fast Track applies, by default, to construction disputes in which no party has asserted a claim or counterclaim exceeding $100,000, exclusive of interest and fees. The Fast Track provides:

- **A single arbitrator** by default;
- **A telephonic preliminary management hearing** within seven (7) days;
- A merits hearing **no later than sixty (60) days** from the appointment of the arbitrator;
- An award **no later than fourteen (14) days** after the close of the hearing.

## Section 7.A — Sample Scheduling Order (Reference Form RF-7.A)

The following is a reference scheduling order frequently used by the AAA's Construction Division. It is illustrative only.

> ORDER NO. 1 — PRELIMINARY SCHEDULING ORDER
>
> 1. **Document Exchange.** The parties shall exchange documents on which they intend to rely no later than [DATE].
>
> 2. **Site Visit.** A site visit shall occur on [DATE] at [LOCATION], jointly coordinated by counsel. The arbitrator(s) may attend.
>
> 3. **Expert Reports.** Initial expert reports shall be served by [DATE]. Rebuttal reports by [DATE].
>
> 4. **Depositions.** Each party may take up to [N] depositions, each not to exceed seven (7) hours.
>
> 5. **Dispositive Motions.** Any dispositive motion shall be filed by [DATE].
>
> 6. **Merits Hearing.** The merits hearing shall commence on [DATE] at [LOCATION] and shall continue for [N] business days.
>
> 7. **Post-Hearing Briefs.** Simultaneous opening briefs by [DATE]; reply briefs by [DATE].
>
> 8. **Form of Award.** The panel shall render a [STANDARD / REASONED / FF&CL] award.
>
> 9. **Confidentiality.** All filings and materials produced in this proceeding are confidential and shall not be disclosed to third parties except as required by law.

- **Total construction-caseload filings, 2023:** 1,114 (down 4.8% from 2022)

- **Percentage of motions to vacate that succeed:** approximately 1.5% of awards (i.e., roughly 20% of motions filed)

## Section 7.C — Glossary of Key Terms

- **BIM (Building Information Modeling):** An integrated digital model of a project's physical and functional characteristics, used in design, construction, and operation.
- **CPM Schedule (Critical Path Method):** A scheduling methodology used to identify and manage the sequence of project activities that determines minimum project duration.
- **Differing Site Conditions Clause:** A contractual clause allocating risk between owner and contractor for site conditions that materially differ from those indicated in the contract documents.
- **Liquidated Damages Clause:** A contractual clause specifying a per-day or per-event sum payable as agreed damages for delay, in lieu of actual proof of damages.
- **Mechanic's Lien:** A statutory security interest in real property in favor of contractors, subcontractors, and material suppliers who have not been paid for work or materials.
- **Pay-When-Paid / Pay-If-Paid Clauses:** Contractual provisions that condition a contractor's obligation to pay a subcontractor on the contractor's receipt of payment from the owner. Treatment varies by jurisdiction.
- **Schedule of Values:** A contractor's allocation of the total contract price among the various components of the work, used to support progress payments.

## Section 7.E — Case-Management Checklists

The following checklists, drawn from AAA case-manager training materials, summarize the procedural milestones the panel and the parties should track in a typical regular-track and LCC-track matter.

### 7.E.1 — Regular-Track Construction Arbitration

- [ ] Demand filed and served
- [ ] Filing fee paid
- [ ] Respondent's answer received (Day 14)
- [ ] Administrative conference held (Day 14-21)
- [ ] Panel size and locale determined
- [ ] Arbitrator strike lists circulated
- [ ] Strike lists returned (Day 30-37)
- [ ] Panel appointed
- [ ] Notice of Appointment and Disclosure circulated (Day 38)
- [ ] Disclosure responses received from parties (Day 45)
- [ ] Preliminary management hearing scheduled (within 21 days of appointment)
- [ ] Order No. 1 — Preliminary Scheduling Order entered
- [ ] Document exchange deadline (typically 60-120 days)
- [ ] Site visit (if applicable)
- [ ] Expert reports exchanged (initial + rebuttal)
- [ ] Deposition phase complete
- [ ] Dispositive motion phase complete (if applicable)
- [ ] Final pre-hearing conference
- [ ] Pre-hearing briefs filed
- [ ] Merits hearing held
- [ ] Post-hearing briefs filed
- [ ] Hearing closed
- [ ] Award rendered (within 30 days)
- [ ] Award transmitted by AAA

### 7.E.2 — LCC-Track Construction Arbitration

The LCC track shares the regular-track milestones but adds:

- [ ] LCC-track designation confirmed at administrative conference
- [ ] Three-arbitrator panel selected
- [ ] Chair designation confirmed (typically by AAA from highest-ranked listed candidate or by party agreement)
- [ ] Preliminary management hearing held within **15 days** of panel appointment (shorter than regular track)
- [ ] ESI protocol entered
- [ ] BIM-data protocol entered (if applicable)
- [ ] Multi-phase scheduling order entered, often including:
  - Liability phase
  - Damages phase
  - Allocation phase (in multi-party matters)
- [ ] Expert phasing protocol (often multiple rounds)
- [ ] Hot-tubbing protocol entered (if used)
- [ ] Mid-case status conference (typically every 60 days)
- [ ] Reasoned award rendered

### 7.E.3 — Fast-Track Construction Arbitration

- [ ] Demand filed and served
- [ ] Filing fee paid
- [ ] Respondent's answer received (Day 14)
- [ ] Telephonic administrative conference held (Day 7-14)
- [ ] Single arbitrator appointed (Day 21-30)
- [ ] Telephonic preliminary management hearing held within 7 days
- [ ] Limited document exchange complete (typically 30-45 days)
- [ ] Merits hearing held (within 60 days of appointment)
- [ ] Award rendered (within 14 days of close of hearing)

## Section 7.F — Frequently Asked Questions

### Q: Can a party invoke the Construction Industry Rules where the contract refers only to "AAA arbitration"?

A: Where the contract refers to AAA arbitration without specifying a rule set, the AAA administrator will determine which rule set best fits the dispute based on the nature of the underlying contract and the subject matter of the dispute. A pure construction dispute under a construction contract will almost always be administered under the Construction Industry Rules. Mixed disputes — for example, a construction contract with a significant ancillary services component — may be administered under the Commercial Rules with construction-specific procedural orders.

### Q: Can a respondent compel arbitration of a claim filed in court?

A: Yes, subject to a properly-pleaded motion to compel arbitration in the relevant court under Section 4 of the Federal Arbitration Act or, for California-seated matters, Code of Civil Procedure § 1281.2. The Rules themselves do not address the motion-to-compel procedure; that is a matter of court procedure.

### Q: What is the effect of a contractual waiver of consequential damages?

A: A contractual waiver of consequential damages, common in construction contracts, is generally enforceable in arbitration under the Construction Rules. The arbitrator will give effect to the contractual allocation of risk. The arbitrator may, however, scrutinize a damages-waiver clause where the asserted damages overlap categories ("delay damages" vs. "consequential damages") or where the clause is alleged to be unconscionable.

### Q: How does the Construction Rules' treatment of dispositive motions differ from the Commercial Rules?

A: The Construction Rules contemplate dispositive motions but do not require any particular procedural framework. The panel's case-management discretion is broad. Practice has converged, in larger construction matters, on a model that requires (i) advance leave from the panel to file a dispositive motion; (ii) a clear identification of the specific issue or claim the motion seeks to dispose of; and (iii) a showing of why early disposition will materially advance the proceeding. The panel may decline leave where it concludes the issue is better addressed on a full evidentiary record.

### Q: When does an arbitrator's duty to disclose end?

A: The duty is **continuing**. It extends through the rendering of the final award (and, in some circumstances, through the resolution of any post-award proceedings). A circumstance arising during the proceeding that would have required disclosure had it existed at the time of appointment must be disclosed at the time it arises.

### Q: What is the difference between a "reasoned" award and "findings of fact and conclusions of law"?

A: A **reasoned award** sets out, in narrative form, the basis for the panel's decision. It need not include formal findings of fact in numbered paragraphs or conclusions of law in the manner of a federal court opinion. **Findings of fact and conclusions of law** ("FF&CL") are more formal and resemble the output of a court trial. Parties should clarify the desired form at the preliminary management hearing.

### Q: Are arbitration awards under the Construction Rules confidential?

A: The Rules contain a confidentiality provision binding the AAA and the arbitrators. The Rules do not, of their own force, bind the parties to maintain the confidentiality of the proceedings or the award. Where confidentiality is important, the parties typically enter a separate confidentiality order at the preliminary management hearing.

## Section 7.G — Selected Case Law on Construction Arbitration

The following cases are useful starting points for the practitioner researching common issues in construction arbitration. (This is illustrative only and is not a comprehensive citation list.)

- **Stolt-Nielsen S.A. v. AnimalFeeds Int'l Corp.**, 559 U.S. 662 (2010) — class arbitration availability.
- **Lamps Plus, Inc. v. Varela**, 587 U.S. ___ (2019) — ambiguity in arbitration agreements and the class question.
- **Henry Schein, Inc. v. Archer & White Sales, Inc.**, 586 U.S. ___ (2019) — delegation clauses and the "wholly groundless" exception.
- **Coinbase, Inc. v. Bielski**, 599 U.S. ___ (2023) — automatic stay of district-court proceedings on interlocutory appeal of order denying motion to compel arbitration.
- **Smith v. Spizzirri**, 601 U.S. ___ (2024) — mandatory stay (rather than dismissal) after order compelling arbitration.
- *(California-specific)* **Iskanian v. CLS Transportation Los Angeles, LLC**, 59 Cal. 4th 348 (2014) — PAGA carve-out, partially abrogated by Viking River Cruises.
- *(California-specific)* **Viking River Cruises v. Moriana**, 596 U.S. ___ (2022) — PAGA arbitration.
- *(California-specific)* **Adolph v. Uber Technologies, Inc.**, 14 Cal. 5th 1104 (2023) — standing after Viking River.
- *(California-specific)* **Honeycutt v. Vertix Solutions**, 102 Cal. App. 5th 412 (2024) *(hypothetical citation for illustration)* — disclosure obligations.

The construction practitioner should additionally be familiar with the principal cases on:

- **Differing site conditions** doctrine
- **Spearin doctrine** (owner-supplied specifications)
- **No-damages-for-delay** clauses and exceptions
- **Mechanics' lien** waivers under the various state lien statutes
- **Pay-when-paid / pay-if-paid** enforceability variations
- **Mutual mistake** in construction contract formation
- **Liquidated damages** vs. penalty doctrines

## Section 7.I — Practitioner Tips

Drawn from interviews with experienced construction arbitrators and counsel conducted for the 2024 edition of this manual:

1. **"Pleading the dispute" matters.** A clear, well-pleaded Demand reduces administrative back-and-forth and frames the dispute for the panel from Day 1.

2. **The PMH is not pro forma.** Treat it as the single most important procedural event in the case.

3. **Joint scheduling orders save time.** Counsel who work out a joint proposal in advance — with disputed items clearly marked — save the panel meaningful time and earn credibility.

4. **Expert phasing reduces cost.** Phased exchange of expert reports (initial, rebuttal, sur-rebuttal where allowed) generally reduces total expert spend versus a single round.

5. **Schedule hot-tubbing.** In matters with technical disputes, joint expert testimony ("hot-tubbing") often produces clearer panel understanding than sequential direct-and-cross.

6. **Do not over-discover.** Construction matters generate vast paper. Counsel who discipline their requests focus the panel on what actually matters.

7. **Treat the panel as a panel.** In three-arbitrator matters, counsel who treat all three panelists as decision-makers (rather than addressing only the chair) tend to be more successful.

8. **Get the form of award right at the PMH.** Surprise about award form at the back end of the case is avoidable.

9. **Plan for post-award.** Even confirmed awards may need additional procedural steps. Counsel who anticipate the post-award phase reduce friction.

10. **Read the contract.** It is surprising how often counsel arrive at the PMH without a clear command of the controlling contractual provisions.
