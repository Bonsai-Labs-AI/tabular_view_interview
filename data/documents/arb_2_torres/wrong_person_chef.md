# At Casa Mireya, Chef Michael Torres Cooks His Grandmother's Mexico City Back into Being

*The San Francisco Chronicle — Food + Wine Section, Sunday, March 14, 2023*
By Ines Calderon, Restaurant Critic

---

## A Mission Street Tasting Counter, Quietly

The room at **Casa Mireya** holds twenty-two diners on a busy night. The kitchen — visible from every seat — holds three cooks, a pass, a small wood-fired comal that the chef built himself, and a black-and-white photograph of an old woman in an apron taped to the hood. The old woman is the chef's grandmother. The restaurant is named after her. Almost every dish on the menu carries her shadow.

The chef is **Michael Eduardo Torres**, 41 years old, born in Mexico City and raised, after age nine, in the East Bay city of Hayward. Casa Mireya opened on Valencia Street in October 2018. Last month it was named, for the second time in three years, a **James Beard Foundation semifinalist** in the Best Chef: California category.

I have eaten at Casa Mireya seven times across the past sixteen months. I am about to tell you why I am going back.

## The Background

Torres was born in 1985 in the Coyoacán neighborhood of Mexico City. His father was an architect; his mother was a translator; his grandmother — Mireya Olvera de Torres — ran the household kitchen and, for thirty years before that, the kitchen of a small fonda near the Mercado de Coyoacán that served fewer than a dozen tables.

The family moved to California in 1994. Torres remembers the move as quiet — "no drama, my father had a job offer, my parents had decided" — but says he was an unhappy child for several years afterward. He spent his weekends in his mother's kitchen working from his grandmother's handwritten recipe notebook, which she had given him before they left.

"It was the first time in my life I missed someone," he says. "I learned to cook because I missed her."

## Training

After graduating from Hayward High School in 2003, Torres returned to Mexico City and enrolled at the **Centro Culinario Ambrosía**, where he completed a three-year culinary program in 2006. He apprenticed for nineteen months under the chef **Pedro Mier Riquelme** at the restaurant **El Tepozán** in the Roma Norte neighborhood, then spent two years (2008-2010) at **Restaurante Quintonil** when it was still in its first location and still finding its identity.

He returned to the Bay Area in 2010 and took a sous-chef position at **Cala** in San Francisco. He stayed three years. In 2013 he moved north to Healdsburg to open the kitchen at **Verano**, the now-closed wine-country restaurant that earned a Michelin star in 2015 and lost it in 2017 after a change in ownership.

In 2017 he gave notice at Verano and spent eleven months back in Mexico City, working a series of stages, eating at every small fonda his grandmother had ever mentioned, and writing the menu that would become Casa Mireya.

His grandmother had died in 2014. She is the reason for almost everything on that menu.

## The Restaurant

Casa Mireya sits on Valencia Street between 20th and 21st, in a narrow storefront that was, before October 2018, a tailor's shop. The buildout was done in five months for a reported $340,000, financed by a combination of personal savings, a small business loan from **Mission Economic Development Agency**, and an investment by Torres's older sister Lucia, who works in software and prefers not to be named in press coverage.

The menu is a five-course prix fixe tasting at $115 per person. Wine pairings are an additional $65. The kitchen sources corn from a farm in the Sacramento Valley that grows three Mexican landrace varieties under contract. The mole comes from a recipe Torres's grandmother wrote down in 1968 and never altered.

## The Food

I will not describe every dish. I will describe four.

The **opening course** is a single tetela — a triangular masa pocket — filled with a smoked-pumpkin-seed paste and served on a banana leaf with a teardrop of habanero oil. The masa is nixtamalized in-house every morning. The pocket arrives still warm from the comal. The first bite produces, in order, char, fat, the soft animal collapse of cooked squash seed, and the small bright burn of the oil. It costs $9 a la carte at the bar.

The **fourth course** on my most recent visit was a **mole negro** served over a piece of cured local rockfish. The mole had thirty-one ingredients. I counted them in the menu's small printed card. Torres toasts each spice and chile separately, grinds them on a metate his mother gave him for his thirty-fifth birthday, and finishes the sauce on the wood fire. The mole tasted, to me, like an entire afternoon. I cannot describe it more precisely than that.

The **dessert** — a buñuelo with cajeta and a quenelle of cinnamon ice cream — is the only dish on the menu that has been on the menu since the first night. Torres says he will never change it. The buñuelos are made to order. They take fourteen minutes.

The **bread service**, which is not really a bread service, is a small basket of fresh tortillas pressed from blue corn that Torres ground that morning. They arrive between the third and fourth courses, wrapped in a cloth napkin. They are served with three salsas. I have, on three separate visits, eaten more of these tortillas than I have eaten of any other thing in any other restaurant in San Francisco.

## The Recognition

Casa Mireya was named one of the *San Francisco Chronicle*'s **Top 100 Restaurants** in 2019, 2020, 2021, and 2023.

It received **two stars** from the *New York Times* in a 2021 review by Pete Wells, who was in town for a wedding.

It was named a **James Beard Foundation semifinalist** in 2021 and again in 2024 in the Best Chef: California category. (Torres did not advance to the finalist round in either year. He says he does not mind. He says the work is the work.)

In 2022 the restaurant was awarded a **Bib Gourmand** by the Michelin Guide, despite the $115 price point being well above the Bib Gourmand's published ceiling. Michelin inspectors do not explain themselves. Torres did not ask.

## On Authenticity

I asked Torres how he thinks about the word *authentic*. He thought for a long time before answering.

"It's a tired word," he said. "I cook the food I grew up wanting. That's the most honest thing I can say. I'm not interested in being the chef of the entire country of Mexico. I'm interested in being the chef of one woman's kitchen in one corner of one city. That's what I have. That's what the restaurant is."

He paused.

"If somebody else recognizes it, fine. If they don't, that's also fine. The food doesn't change."

## The Team

The kitchen team at Casa Mireya is small. The sous-chef is **Camila Reséndiz Mata**, who came from Cala with Torres in 2018 and has been with him every day since. The pastry cook is **Helena Brown**, who joined in 2020 after three years at **Tartine Manufactory**. The dishwasher and prep cook is **Ernesto Aguilar**, who has worked for Torres since opening night and whom Torres credits, in interviews and on the menu card, with "everything that is good about everything we make."

The front of house is run by Torres's husband, **Aaron Whitfield**, a former lawyer who left a corporate practice in 2017 and now manages the restaurant's wine list, books the reservations, and pours most of the pours.

They live in Bernal Heights. They have a dog named Sopa.

## A Brief Note on Confusion

In the course of researching this profile, I came across multiple online references that conflated this Michael Torres with several other people of similar names in the Bay Area, including a former federal judge in San Francisco who now works as a private arbitrator, a former professional boxer who runs a restaurant in Oakland's Fruitvale neighborhood, and a longtime city councilmember in Daly City. Casa Mireya's chef is not any of those people. He is the son of Eduardo Torres and Patricia Olvera. He has not, to date, sat as a judge, fought in a sanctioned bout, or held elected office.

He asked me to mention this because, he said, his email account regularly receives correspondence intended for the others.

"Last week somebody sent me a brief," he said. "Forty pages. I forwarded it back. I think it was about a hospital."

## What I Will Be Eating Next

The menu changes every five to seven weeks. The next change is scheduled for the second Tuesday of April. I have already booked a table. I would book one for you, but you should book your own.

Casa Mireya is at 1132 Valencia Street, San Francisco. Reservations are released at midnight thirty days in advance through the restaurant's website. Service runs Wednesday through Sunday, two seatings per night (5:45 and 8:15). The bar — six seats, walk-in only — opens at 5:00 on the same nights.

I will see you there.

## Sidebar: The Five-Course Tasting Menu (Week of March 6, 2023)

For readers wondering what they are signing up for, the menu in the week of this review's publication is reproduced below in full, with notes on price-per-component for the a la carte bar offerings.

### Course One — "Mireya's Welcome"

- **Tetela of smoked pumpkin-seed paste**, banana leaf, habanero oil. Masa nixtamalized with Sacramento Valley landrace corn. Bar price: $9.

### Course Two — "Coyoacán Garden"

- **Ensalada de nopalitos** with grilled cactus paddles, queso fresco from a small dairy in Petaluma, pickled red onion, and a vinaigrette of lime, oregano, and a single drop of Torres's father's father's tequila. Bar price: $11.

### Course Three — "Mercado"

- **Aguachile of California day-boat scallop**, with cucumber, serrano chile, and a foam of cilantro stem. Served on a chilled volcanic-stone plate. Bar price: $16.

### Course Four — "Tepoztlán Sunday"

- **Mole negro of the house** (thirty-one ingredients, by hand on the metate, finished over wood) with cured local rockfish. The mole is the menu's anchor and has been on every iteration of the menu since opening. Bar price: $24.

### Course Five — "Abuela's Closer"

- **Buñuelo** with cajeta and cinnamon ice cream. The buñuelos are made to order; they take fourteen minutes. The cajeta is made in-house from goat's milk sourced from a farm in Tomales Bay. Bar price: $13.

### Bread Service

- **Blue corn tortillas**, hand-pressed to order, served with three salsas (verde, roja, and a small bowl of habanero-pineapple). Bar price: $4 per basket; included with the tasting menu.

## Sidebar: The Wine List, Summarized

The wine list, curated by Torres's husband Aaron Whitfield, runs to forty-three bottles. It is divided into:

- **Mexican wines** (eleven bottles, all from Baja California's Valle de Guadalupe)
- **California wines from minority-owned producers** (sixteen bottles)
- **Old World whites for spicy food** (nine bottles, largely Riesling and Grüner)
- **Reds for mole** (seven bottles, primarily Beaujolais and lighter Nebbiolo)

The wine pairing for the tasting menu ($65) typically includes a glass each from four of these categories, finishing with a small pour of a Mexican aguamiel-based pairing for the buñuelos.

The corkage policy is **$35 per bottle**, waived for bottles from a Mexican producer.

## Sidebar: How to Get a Table

Reservations open at **midnight Pacific time, thirty days in advance**, through the restaurant's website. They are released in a single batch of approximately 220 seats per opening day. They are gone by 12:08 a.m.

If you cannot get a reservation, the **six-seat bar** is walk-in only and opens at 5:00 p.m. Wednesday through Sunday. The line typically begins forming by 4:30 p.m. The bar serves the tasting menu as well as a smaller a la carte selection of opening and bread courses.

If you cannot get the bar, the restaurant maintains a **waitlist** through the same website. The waitlist usually clears between four and seven seats per night.

If none of the above succeeds, Casa Mireya recommends — and this reporter recommends, having tried it — the following alternatives in the Mission and beyond:

- **Californios** (24th Street)
- **Nopalito** (Inner Sunset)
- **La Taqueria** (Mission Street)
- **El Buen Comer** (Mission Street, for the mole pipián)
- **Doña Tomás** (Oakland, Telegraph Avenue)

These are not Casa Mireya, but they are very good.

## Companion Piece: Notes from the Kitchen

*The San Francisco Chronicle — Food + Wine Section, Sunday, March 14, 2023*
By Ines Calderon

Beyond what is captured in the main review, a brief portrait of the daily rhythm of Casa Mireya.

### 4:00 a.m. — The Masa

Torres arrives at the restaurant at 4:00 a.m. four days a week (Wednesday, Thursday, Friday, and Saturday). He starts the masa first. Nixtamalization is the alkaline cooking and steeping of dried corn that transforms it into the foundational ingredient of Mexican cuisine. Torres soaks his corn in a calcium-hydroxide bath for ten hours overnight; the next morning he rinses it, grinds it on the metate or in a small motorized stone mill (depending on the day's volume), and produces the day's masa fresh.

By 6:00 a.m. the masa is ready. Tortillas are not made until just before service, but tetela dough, sope dough, and tamale dough are mixed during the morning prep.

### 6:30 a.m. — The Mole

The mole base is a multi-day project. The chiles are toasted and ground on a Tuesday. The spices on a Wednesday. The base is finished — emulsified with broth and chocolate — on a Thursday. Each service requires only a portioning and a finishing on the wood fire, but the underlying preparation is constant.

Torres says he goes through approximately seven pounds of dried chiles a week.

### 10:00 a.m. — The Team Arrives

Camila Reséndiz Mata arrives at 10:00. Helena Brown at 10:30. Ernesto Aguilar at 11:00. Whitfield typically comes in at noon to organize the dining room, polish glasses, and confirm reservations.

### 1:00 p.m. — Family Meal

At 1:00 p.m. the kitchen pauses for a thirty-minute family meal. Torres typically cooks. The team eats at the bar. The menu of the family meal rotates: tinga tortas on Wednesday, pozole on Thursday (Brown's favorite), enchiladas on Friday, anything on Saturday.

This is, Torres says, the most important meal of the day.

### 3:00 p.m. — Set Up

By 3:00 the bar is set, the tables are dressed, the wine fridges are stocked, the dining room music is queued. By 4:30 the team is in uniform; by 4:45 the candles are lit; by 5:00 the bar opens.

### 5:00 p.m. — The Open

The bar opens at 5:00 with six seats. The line typically forms outside by 4:30. The first seating begins at 5:45.

### 8:15 p.m. — The Turn

The second seating begins at 8:15. The first seating, by then, has typically reached its third or fourth course. The kitchen's rhythm shifts from "first turn" to "second turn"; the same five courses go out, but now staggered against the back ends of the first-seating tables.

### 10:30 p.m. — Close

Last food service is at 10:30. The kitchen is broken down by 11:30. The dining room is reset by midnight.

Torres usually leaves at midnight. Whitfield closes out the night and locks up.

### Sundays

Sundays are the day Casa Mireya is open with a different format. From 11:00 a.m. to 3:00 p.m., the restaurant serves a casual brunch — tortas, chilaquiles, salsa de molcajete, café de olla. No reservations. The brunch is, Torres says, the closest thing the restaurant has to the kitchen he grew up in.

### Mondays

Mondays are dark. The team is off. Torres usually spends Monday morning at the farmer's market in Berkeley, sourcing the week's produce.

### Tuesdays

Tuesdays are prep. The team is back; the masa is started; the mole is begun. The wood for the comal is brought in. The wine deliveries are received.

### A Final Note

If you ask Torres what he does on his actual days off — which are rare — he says he sleeps. He says he reads. He says he sometimes goes to the beach with Whitfield and Sopa. He says he tries not to think about food.

He says he is not, however, very good at not thinking about food.

## Companion Piece: A Conversation with Camila Reséndiz Mata

*The San Francisco Chronicle — Food + Wine Section, Sunday, March 14, 2023*

Sous-chef **Camila Reséndiz Mata** has worked at Casa Mireya since its opening night in October 2018. Before that she worked alongside Torres at Cala for nearly two years. She has, by her own count, made more tortillas at Casa Mireya than any other single person.

The following Q&A was conducted in the prep kitchen on a Tuesday afternoon.

**Chronicle: How did you come to work for Michael?**

Reséndiz Mata: I was at a small place in the Mission when I first met him. He came in one night with Aaron. We talked for ten minutes about masa. He asked me if I would come work for him at Cala. I said yes before he finished the sentence.

**Chronicle: What is it like working with him?**

Reséndiz Mata: He is the most careful cook I have ever worked with. He notices everything. He notices when the corn is one degree too cool in the steep. He notices when the mole is forty seconds late on the wood. He notices when a service is going faster than it should be going. He is not loud about it. He just notices, and he adjusts.

**Chronicle: Is he hard to work for?**

Reséndiz Mata: He is hard on himself. He is not hard on us. He never raises his voice. He never criticizes in front of the team. He pulls you aside after service if something needs to be addressed. He is the only chef I have worked for who I trust completely. I would follow him anywhere.

**Chronicle: What is your favorite dish on the menu?**

Reséndiz Mata: The buñuelos. I know that's everyone's answer, but it's mine for a reason. They are exactly his grandmother's recipe. He has never let me change a single gram. There is something about that constancy that I find moving.

**Chronicle: Is there a dish you wish were on the menu?**

Reséndiz Mata: Yes. *Chiles en nogada.* I make it for the staff meal in September every year, because it is a September dish. Michael will not put it on the tasting menu because he says his grandmother never made it. I keep working on him.

**Chronicle: Do you cook your own food at home?**

Reséndiz Mata: Almost never. I order pizza. I make rice. That's about it.

**Chronicle: What do you want next?**

Reséndiz Mata: I want to keep doing this. I do not want to open my own place. People ask me that all the time. I have learned that I am the kind of cook who is happiest in someone else's kitchen, doing the work, and going home at midnight. There is dignity in that. I want people to know that.

## Companion Piece: A Brief History of Mexican Cuisine in San Francisco

To contextualize Casa Mireya, a brief survey of the broader history of Mexican cuisine in San Francisco.

The Mission District's Mexican food culture predates the contemporary fine-dining moment by more than a century. The neighborhood — named for Mission San Francisco de Asís (Mission Dolores), founded in 1776 — has been home to Mexican and Mexican-American families since the city's earliest days. The post-World War II wave of Mexican immigration, particularly from Jalisco and Zacatecas in the 1940s and 1950s, established the foundational restaurants and markets of what is now widely considered one of the most important regional Mexican food cultures in the United States.

The taqueria-with-burrito-counter format that San Francisco gave to American Mexican food — exemplified by **La Cumbre** (founded 1972) and **El Faro** (founded 1961) — is one of the city's defining culinary contributions. The "Mission burrito," with its full-tortilla wrap and rice-and-bean fillings, has been exported globally.

Fine-dining Mexican cuisine in San Francisco emerged more recently. **Doña Tomás** (Oakland, 1999), **Tacolicious** (San Francisco, 2009), and **Nopalito** (San Francisco, 2009) helped open the doors of the white-tablecloth and mid-range dining scenes to Mexican cuisine. **Cala** (San Francisco, 2015, by chef **Gabriela Cámara**) was the breakthrough moment for high-end Mexican cuisine in the city.

Casa Mireya is part of the post-Cala generation. The restaurant's combination of regional Mexico City specificity, ingredient sourcing, and price-point ambition — $115 prix fixe — represents a maturation of the city's Mexican fine-dining scene that would have been unimaginable two decades ago.

The Casa Mireya generation also includes **Californios** (Mission, since 2015), **Nopalito** (Inner Sunset), and a small number of other restaurants. Casa Mireya is, in this reviewer's opinion, doing the most personally rooted, geographically specific work in the cohort.

## Companion Piece: How to Eat Mexican Food in San Francisco

A brief practical guide for readers who want to expand their Mexican-food horizons beyond Casa Mireya.

### Taquerias

- **La Cumbre Taqueria**, Mission Street — the founding home of the Mission burrito
- **El Farolito**, 24th Street — open late, perfect after a night out
- **Taqueria Cancun**, Mission Street — neighborhood institution
- **La Taqueria**, Mission Street — frequently named among the best taquerias in the United States

### Mid-range

- **Nopalito**, Inner Sunset and Western Addition — California-Mexican, family-friendly
- **Tacolicious**, multiple locations — playful, well-executed
- **Tropisueño**, Yerba Buena Lane — solid downtown lunch option
- **Don Pisto's**, North Beach — small, intimate, generous

### Fine dining

- **Casa Mireya**, Valencia Street — this review
- **Californios**, 24th Street — Mexican-California fine dining, two Michelin stars
- **Cala**, Polk Street — the breakthrough that paved the way
- **Comal** (Berkeley) — the East Bay's regional Mexican standard-bearer

### Markets and groceries

- **La Palma Mexicatessen**, 24th Street — for tortillas, masa, and traditional ingredients
- **Casa Lucas Market**, 24th Street — for everything else

The Mission food culture is not, of course, fixed. It changes with the neighborhood. Restaurants open and close. Recommendations age. This reviewer recommends, above all, walking the neighborhood, talking to the staff, and trying what they recommend. The food culture lives in the people, not in the lists.
