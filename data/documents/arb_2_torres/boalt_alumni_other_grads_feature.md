# The Class That Built a Generation: Berkeley Law's 1980s Boalt Hall Alumni in Public Life

*Berkeley Law Transcript — Alumni Magazine, Winter 2024 / Vol. 47, No. 4*
By Jessamyn Park, Class of 2002

---

## A Decade in Retrospect

The 1980s at **Boalt Hall School of Law** — as it was still called until the school's 2020 rebranding — were a transformative decade. The school admitted its first class above 50% women in 1984. It launched the **Center for Social Justice** (then the East Bay Community Law Center, in its earliest incubation form) in 1988. It hosted, in 1986, the famously contentious all-day faculty colloquium that produced what became the founding documents of the **Critical Legal Studies** movement on the West Coast.

It also graduated, between 1980 and 1989, roughly **2,950** lawyers, of whom a remarkable number have shaped the legal, civic, political, judicial, academic, and entrepreneurial landscape of California — and of the country — in the four decades since.

This issue's feature profiles **fourteen** of them. They are by no means a comprehensive list. They are, instead, a representative cross-section chosen to capture the range of paths a Boalt JD has opened in the years since the 1980s.

A note on omissions: this feature was reported between June and September 2024. Several alumni who would otherwise have been profiled — including the late **Hon. Ronald T. Maverick '83**, who passed away in March 2024 — could not be included for reasons of timing. The editors apologize for those gaps and intend to address them in future issues.

## Profile 1: Hon. Bernadette Singh-Carrillo, JD '81

The Honorable Bernadette Singh-Carrillo currently serves as **Presiding Justice of the California Court of Appeal, Fourth Appellate District, Division Two**, based in Riverside. She was appointed to the appellate bench by Governor Davis in 2001 and elevated to Presiding Justice by Governor Newsom in 2020.

Before her judicial appointment, Justice Singh-Carrillo spent fifteen years in private practice at the Los Angeles firm of **Munger, Tolles & Olson**, where she became the firm's third woman partner in 1992. She specialized in complex commercial litigation and First Amendment defense work, representing media clients including the *Los Angeles Times* and the McClatchy newspaper group.

Justice Singh-Carrillo grew up in the Central Valley town of **Visalia**, the eldest of four children of Punjabi-Mexican-American parents who ran a citrus-packing operation. She attended **UCLA** for her undergraduate degree (English, 1978) before coming to Berkeley.

She is a regular speaker at Berkeley Law's judicial-clerkship workshops and a longtime supporter of the school's **First-Generation Professional Students** affinity group.

## Profile 2: Caleb O. Foxworth, JD '82

**Caleb Foxworth** is the co-founder and managing partner of **Foxworth Yi Aldermire LLP**, a San Francisco-based boutique tax-controversy firm with offices in San Francisco, San Diego, and Washington, D.C. The firm represents high-net-worth individuals and family offices in federal and state tax disputes.

Foxworth began his career at the **Internal Revenue Service's Office of Chief Counsel** in Washington, where he worked for seven years before returning to California to join the predecessor of his current firm in 1989. He became the firm's named partner in 2004 after the retirement of one of its original founders.

Foxworth is a graduate of **Morehouse College** (1979), a former chair of the **State Bar of California's Taxation Section**, and a sitting member of the **American Law Institute**. He is also a longtime supporter of the **Berkeley Law Tax Law Clinic**, which his firm endowed in 2018.

He and his husband, the architect **Tomas Aldermire**, live in San Francisco's Cole Valley neighborhood.

## Profile 3: Aisha Nakimuli-Brown, JD '82

After a career trajectory that included six years at **Cravath, Swaine & Moore** in New York and twelve years at the **U.S. Department of Justice's Antitrust Division**, **Aisha Nakimuli-Brown** is currently the **General Counsel of Patagonia, Inc.**, a position she has held since 2018.

Nakimuli-Brown was the first Black woman in Patagonia's senior leadership team and remains one of a small number of Black general counsel of large privately held U.S. companies. She is widely credited with leading Patagonia's ownership-restructuring transaction in 2022, under which the founding Chouinard family transferred ownership of the company to a purpose trust dedicated to environmental causes.

She has published widely on corporate purpose, environmental governance, and antitrust enforcement, including a recent *Stanford Law Review* article on the role of B Corporation status in merger analysis.

She holds an undergraduate degree from **Spelman College** (1979) and an LL.M. in International Legal Studies from **NYU** (1991).

## Profile 4: Vincent A. Marquardt-Spinelli, JD '83

**Vincent Marquardt-Spinelli** has spent the entirety of his post-Boalt career at one institution: the **Federal Public Defender's Office for the Northern District of California**, where he is now the Office's Chief of the Capital Habeas Unit.

In his thirty-one years with the office, Marquardt-Spinelli has represented more than forty death-row inmates at various stages of federal habeas litigation. He successfully argued *Marquardt v. Ayers* before the Ninth Circuit in 2008 (an unrelated party of the same surname), establishing a procedural standard for evidentiary hearings in California capital cases that remains in force.

In 2019 he received the **Bryan Stevenson Award for Capital Defense** from the National Association of Criminal Defense Lawyers, the highest national award in the field.

Marquardt-Spinelli grew up in **Buffalo, New York**. He came to Berkeley after college at SUNY Binghamton because, he says, "I had never seen mountains and I wanted to."

## Profile 5: Helena Wuwurong-Yuan, JD '83

**Helena Wuwurong-Yuan** is the founder and CEO of **Veridia Health**, a Boston-based health-technology company that develops decision-support software for hospital intensive-care units. The company was founded in 2014 and reached "unicorn" valuation in late 2022.

Before her transition to entrepreneurship, Wuwurong-Yuan practiced healthcare regulatory law for twenty-two years, including ten years at **Ropes & Gray** in Boston and twelve years as Associate General Counsel at **Partners HealthCare** (now Mass General Brigham).

She is a graduate of **Wellesley College** (1980), holds an MBA from **MIT Sloan** (2013), and recently joined the board of trustees of **Boston Children's Hospital**.

She has endowed the **Wuwurong-Yuan Fellowship** at Berkeley Law, which supports a 2L or 3L pursuing summer work in health-law policy.

## Profile 6: Reginald A. Cervantes-Owusu, JD '84

After fifteen years of progressively senior roles in the **California Office of Legislative Counsel** in Sacramento, **Reginald Cervantes-Owusu** became, in 2014, the **Legislative Counsel of California** — the chief drafter and counsel to the California Legislature. He retired from that position in May 2024 after a decade of service.

Cervantes-Owusu is widely credited with modernizing the office's drafting and bill-tracking infrastructure, including the 2018 launch of the **legislative-text public API** that now powers much of the press and civil-society coverage of California legislation.

He attended **UC Davis** as an undergraduate (Political Science, 1981), grew up in **East Palo Alto**, and now lives in retirement in **Carmel-by-the-Sea**, where he says he gardens and writes "very bad fiction." He has been invited, but has not yet agreed, to teach a seminar at Berkeley Law on legislative drafting.

## Profile 7: Magdalena Sokolova, JD '84

**Magdalena Sokolova** is the **Chief Judge of the U.S. Court of International Trade**, based in New York. She was nominated to the court by President Obama in 2014 and elevated to Chief Judge by seniority in 2022.

Before her appointment, Sokolova spent twenty-six years in the international trade practice of **Hogan Lovells** (and its predecessor firm Hogan & Hartson), serving for the last eight as the firm's global trade practice chair. She represented sovereigns, importers, and U.S. industry petitioners in antidumping, countervailing duty, and customs matters.

Sokolova was born in **Prague**, then in Czechoslovakia, and emigrated with her family in 1968. She is a graduate of **Smith College** (1981). She has lectured at Berkeley Law twice in the past decade on issues of trade-remedy law.

## Profile 8: Daniel Ngarambe-Patel, JD '85

**Daniel Ngarambe-Patel** is the founding executive director of the **Pacific Coast Immigrant Rights Center**, a Los Angeles-based legal-services and advocacy organization that he founded in 1992.

Under his leadership, the Center has grown from a three-attorney shop operating out of a borrowed storefront to a 110-employee organization with offices in Los Angeles, Fresno, San Diego, and Seattle. The Center is responsible for the legal representation of an estimated 14,000 immigration clients annually.

Ngarambe-Patel was awarded a **MacArthur Foundation Fellowship** in 2006 and the **ABA Medal** in 2019, the highest honor of the American Bar Association.

He clerked, after Boalt, for **Judge Stephen Reinhardt** of the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit. He keeps a framed letter from Judge Reinhardt on the wall of his Los Angeles office.

## Profile 9: Patricia "Patty" Dornbusch-Kim, JD '85

After clerkships at the Ninth Circuit and the U.S. Supreme Court (for Justice Sandra Day O'Connor), **Patty Dornbusch-Kim** joined the faculty of **Stanford Law School** in 1988 and was tenured in 1994. She held the **Marion Rice Kirkwood Professorship of Law** from 2008 until her appointment to the **Ninth Circuit** in 2014.

Judge Dornbusch-Kim sits in **Pasadena**. She is widely regarded as one of the most respected appellate jurists in the federal system and has been mentioned in press accounts as a possible future Supreme Court nominee.

Her academic work focused on federal courts, civil procedure, and the law of equitable remedies. Her treatise *Equitable Remedies in Federal Civil Practice* (Oxford, 2010) is in its third edition.

She grew up in **Davis, California**, the daughter of two UC Davis biologists, and attended **Yale** as an undergraduate (Mathematics, 1982).

## Profile 10: Wilson J. Echebiri, JD '86

**Wilson Echebiri** is the founding partner of **Echebiri Mendez Reichmann LLP**, a Los Angeles plaintiff-side employment law firm widely regarded as one of the most successful in California in wage-and-hour class action litigation.

In 2023 the firm recovered an aggregate of approximately **$320 million** in class-wide settlements on behalf of its clients, who included workers in the warehouse-logistics, ride-share, and home-care industries.

Echebiri serves on the board of trustees of the **Berkeley Law Foundation** and endowed, in 2017, the **Echebiri Family Scholarship** for Berkeley Law students with demonstrated commitments to labor and employment law on the worker side.

He came to law school after two years organizing for the **United Farm Workers** in the Central Valley.

## Profile 11: Soraya Mukherjee-Glenn, JD '87

**Soraya Mukherjee-Glenn** has spent the bulk of her career at the **California Public Utilities Commission**, where she has served as **Chief Administrative Law Judge** since 2019. She joined the CPUC as a staff ALJ in 1995 after seven years in private practice at **Morrison & Foerster** in San Francisco.

Mukherjee-Glenn has presided over many of the largest CPUC adjudicative proceedings of the past decade, including the post-2017 wildfire docket and the ongoing investigations into utility undergrounding plans. She has authored more than 240 published proposed decisions.

She is a graduate of **Pomona College** (1984) and holds an M.S. in Public Policy from the **Hertie School** in Berlin (1985, on a Fulbright).

## Profile 12: Marcus T. Holloway-Quigley, JD '87

**Marcus Holloway-Quigley** is the **Vice President for Worldwide Government Affairs** at **Lockheed Martin Corporation**, based in Bethesda, Maryland. He has held that position since 2019, after a long career that included six years at the **U.S. State Department** (1990-1996), seven years at the **U.S. House Foreign Affairs Committee** majority staff (1996-2003), and sixteen years at **Boeing Defense, Space & Security**.

Holloway-Quigley is a frequent speaker at Berkeley Law's national-security law programs. He has endowed an annual **Holloway-Quigley Lecture in National Security Practice**, the most recent of which was delivered in February 2024 by former Solicitor General Donald Verrilli.

## Profile 13: Yolanda Cesário-Vega, JD '88

**Yolanda Cesário-Vega** has spent her entire career — thirty-six years and counting — at the **East Bay Community Law Center**, the East Oakland legal services and clinical-education organization that Berkeley Law students helped found in the late 1980s.

Cesário-Vega started at the Center as a third-year Boalt student in the inaugural clinical cohort in 1987. She joined the staff after graduation, and has been the Center's **Executive Director** since 2002.

Under her leadership, the Center has grown into one of the largest legal-services organizations in the East Bay and continues to serve as the cornerstone of Berkeley Law's clinical curriculum, training generations of law students in the disciplines of housing law, family law, and economic-justice lawyering.

She received the **California Bar's Loren Miller Legal Services Award** in 2017.

## Profile 14: Khalil M. Foster-Iwasaki, JD '89

**Khalil Foster-Iwasaki** is the founder and CEO of **Cypress & Cedar Coffee Roasters**, a Bay Area specialty-coffee company with sixteen retail locations and a roasting facility in West Oakland. The company employs approximately 280 people and was, in 2023, named one of the *San Francisco Business Times*' Best Places to Work for the seventh consecutive year.

Foster-Iwasaki practiced law for only four years after Boalt — at the now-defunct firm of **Heller Ehrman White & McAuliffe** — before leaving the profession in 1993 to open his first coffee shop on College Avenue in Berkeley.

He has remained close to Berkeley Law as a regular speaker in the school's **Entrepreneurship and Innovation Practicum** and as a regular caterer (gratis) for the school's commencement events.

## A Note on Methodology

This feature was reported between June and September 2024 by *Transcript* staff, with research assistance from Berkeley Law's Alumni Relations office. Profiles were drafted from interviews conducted by the author with each subject, supplemented by review of public-record materials and prior published coverage.

The fourteen profiles above were selected by the editors from a longer list of more than ninety candidates suggested by the Alumni Relations office and by the *Transcript*'s editorial advisory board. The selection sought to balance professional, geographic, demographic, and graduation-year diversity. The selection is not intended as a ranking, and many distinguished classmates of the 1980s graduating classes have not been profiled here.

The editors invite readers to suggest alumni for future features at **transcript@law.berkeley.example**.

## Editor's Note on Confusion of Names

This issue's profiles cover a variety of distinguished alumni of the 1980s graduating classes. The editors note for the benefit of readers that none of the profiled subjects above is the same person as several other prominent Bay Area attorneys and judges of similar names, including:

- A retired California superior-court judge in San Francisco who now operates a private arbitration practice (whose JD year is reported in some sources as 1982 but who is not the subject of any profile in this issue);

- A longtime city councilmember in Daly City.

We mention this because Berkeley Law's alumni office occasionally receives correspondence intended for the other individuals named above, and we would like to be helpful in routing such inquiries. Please direct queries to our alumni office at alumni@law.berkeley.example.

## Letters to the Editor

### From the Class of 1981

To the Editor:

I read with great interest your Fall 2024 feature on John Tao '79 and his work on the Pacific Heritage Foundation. I should add — as it was not mentioned in your piece — that the Foundation's earliest seed funding came from a small group of 1981 graduates who had been classmates of John's during our 2L year, when we shared a study group with him in the back room of Café Med on Telegraph Avenue. Marcia Yegerlehner '81, Edward Polack '81, and I each contributed $500 to John's project in 1991, which together with John's own savings allowed the Foundation to incorporate.

It is a small footnote, but the kind of thing that tends to get lost in the official histories. I thought your readers might want to know.

Sincerely,
**Theodora Albright-Park '81**
San Francisco, California

To the Editor:

I was there. (I was, at the time, the Deputy Legislative Counsel, and I retired in 2022.)

Reggie was, and remains, one of the most consequential figures in California state government in the last three decades. He has never sought the limelight. Your profile was a long-deserved recognition.

Sincerely,
**Penelope Yarrow-Cunningham '84**
Sacramento, California
