# Goldman's Lagos Bet: Inside Amara Okonkwo's Push Into West African Infrastructure Finance

*BusinessDay (Lagos) — Weekend Cover Feature, 14 February 2026*

---

## A Long Way From Victoria Island

The view from the 17th floor of the Civic Towers building on Ozumba Mbadiwe Avenue is, if you catch it in the right light at the right time of evening, almost spectacular. The Lagos lagoon glints copper in the late sun, the snarl of traffic on the Lekki-Epe Expressway softens to a distant rumble, and the new mass-transit pylons running south toward Ajah are barely visible against the haze.

It is from this office — a corner suite that Goldman Sachs took on a ten-year lease in 2022, when the firm announced its expanded West African presence — that Amara N. Okonkwo, the bank's recently promoted Managing Director and Head of Sub-Saharan African Infrastructure Finance, has spent the past eighteen months trying to build a business that did not, until very recently, really exist.

"There has always been infrastructure money in Africa," she says, when asked how the desk has changed since she took it over in November 2024. "There has not always been infrastructure capital. Those are different problems. We are trying to solve the second one."

The distinction is one she draws often. Money, in her vocabulary, is what banks lend. Capital is what builds — patient, structured, blended, often with insurance wrapped around it and a public-sector anchor underneath. Her desk's mandate, as she explained to BusinessDay in an interview at the Civic Towers offices last week, is the latter.

## Background

Ms. Okonkwo, 41, joined Goldman Sachs in 2010 as a first-year analyst in the firm's London office, on the Emerging Markets Financing team within the Investment Banking Division. She holds a BSc in Economics from the London School of Economics (first class, 2007), an MSc in Finance from Imperial College London (2008), and an MBA from Wharton (2014, on a leave of absence from the firm).

She made vice president in 2015, executive director in 2018, and managing director in November 2024, when the firm reorganised its sub-Saharan Africa coverage into a single product-and-coverage line headquartered in Lagos. She is one of three MDs in Goldman's Lagos office and the most senior woman in the firm's sub-Saharan Africa business.

Her appointment was noted, somewhat unusually, in a brief item in the November 2024 issue of *Euromoney*, which described it as "evidence, finally, that the bulge-bracket firms are taking Lagos seriously as a regional booking centre rather than as a satellite of Johannesburg."

## The Portfolio

The desk's most visible recent transaction was the financing, announced in September 2025, of a 1,800-megawatt combined-cycle gas plant in the Ogun State free trade zone — a deal that involved Goldman as lead structuring agent for a $2.1 billion debt package syndicated across the International Finance Corporation, Standard Bank, MUFG, and the African Export-Import Bank. The transaction, which closed in early December 2025, was named "African Infrastructure Deal of the Year" by Project Finance International in its January 2026 awards.

A second transaction — the financing of an upgrade to the rail link between Apapa Port and the Tin Can Island container terminal — closed in March 2025 with Goldman as joint book-running manager on a $480 million infrastructure-bond placement underwritten under the African Trade Insurance Agency's political-risk cover.

A third, smaller but in Ms. Okonkwo's view more strategically important, was the structuring of a $145 million blended-finance facility for a fibre-optic backbone project in the Sahel — a transaction that brought together the European Investment Bank, the U.S. Development Finance Corporation, and a syndicate of three African commercial banks, with Goldman acting as financial adviser to the sponsor.

"The fibre deal will not show up in any league table," she said. "It is too small. But it is the kind of deal we have to learn to do well, because the next decade of African infrastructure finance is going to be many small deals stitched together, not one big deal that solves everything."

## On Capital Structure

Asked what surprises her most about the West African infrastructure-finance market, she paused for nearly a full minute before answering.

"The mismatch," she said eventually. "Most of the available local-currency capital is short-dated. Most of the available long-dated capital is in hard currency. Almost no one wants to take the FX risk for thirty years. So we structure around it — currency hedges, partial credit guarantees, MIGA insurance, escrow accounts at offshore banks. And every time we structure around it, the deal gets more expensive, and we wonder why African infrastructure is more expensive than it should be."

Her view, expressed at a panel at the African Finance Corporation's annual conference in Kigali in October 2025, is that the development-finance community has been too slow to deploy local-currency-denominated long-dated capital at scale. She has been a vocal advocate for the African Development Bank's local-currency lending pilot and has, behind the scenes, argued that Goldman should make a more aggressive market in naira-denominated infrastructure bonds.

The firm's appetite for such an expansion remains, as one senior Goldman executive in New York put it in a recent internal call, "constructive but not enthusiastic."

## Culture and Hiring

The Lagos office now employs 47 people across investment banking, global markets, and operations. Ms. Okonkwo has overseen the infrastructure finance team's expansion from three professionals in late 2024 to twelve as of February 2026, with hires from Standard Chartered, the African Finance Corporation, and the World Bank Group.

Her hiring philosophy, she said, has been deliberately tilted toward what she called "structuring brains, not relationship brains."

"You can teach someone to take a CFO to dinner," she said. "You cannot teach someone to read a force-majeure clause. I would rather hire the second person and send them to dinner once a week."

The Lagos office is, by most accounts, one of the more diverse Goldman offices globally. As of December 2025, women constituted 43 per cent of the office's professional staff and 50 per cent of its managing directors — figures Ms. Okonkwo describes as "fine, but not done."

## Personal

Ms. Okonkwo was born in Enugu in 1984 and educated at Queen's College Lagos before going to LSE. She is the eldest of three children. Her father, a retired federal civil servant, lives in Enugu. Her mother, a retired secondary-school principal, lives with her in Ikoyi.

She is married to Tunji Adekoya, the chief financial officer of a Lagos-based logistics holding company. They have two children, aged 8 and 5. She has spoken publicly about her difficulty in balancing the demands of her role with parenting and has, since taking the MD title, declined all weekend speaking engagements that are not held in Lagos.

She is a non-executive director of the LSE African Development Foundation, a member of the board of trustees of the African Leadership Academy in Johannesburg, and an alumna mentor for the Wharton School's African Student Association.

She does not, she says, sit on any corporate boards in Nigeria, in order to avoid any appearance of conflict with her client coverage responsibilities at Goldman.

## On Being Mistaken for Someone Else

Ms. Okonkwo has, on at least three documented occasions, been confused in international media with an internationally prominent arbitrator of the same name and middle initial — Dr. Amara N. Okonkwo, the London- and Lagos-based investment-treaty arbitrator.

Asked about the confusion, she laughed.

"I have been asked at conferences about ICSID cases I have never read," she said. "I have been seated next to ambassadors who clearly expected someone else. I have, twice, been congratulated on awards I did not write."

She added: "I have met her. She is a wonderful woman. She is not me. I am not her. We are not related. I am told this happens to many Okonkwos."

The confusion has not been, in her view, professionally damaging. "Bankers and arbitrators do different things on different days," she said. "Our calendars do not really clash."

## What Comes Next

Asked where she sees the desk in five years, she was characteristically reluctant to make a forecast.

"I would like us to have closed twenty billion dollars of capital that built things that worked," she said. "I would like at least half of that to be local currency. I would like at least half of the bankers on my team to be from West Africa, trained in West Africa, and committed to staying in West Africa. If I get even half of those, I will be content."

The Lagos sky over the lagoon was, by then, turning purple, and the traffic on Ozumba Mbadiwe was starting to slow. She glanced at her watch, said something to her assistant about a 7 p.m. call with Hong Kong, and stood.

The interview was over. The desk, she reminded us politely, had not finished its day.

## Sidebar: The Ogun Combined-Cycle Gas Deal — A Closer Look

The $2.1 billion senior debt package supporting the Ogun State combined-cycle gas plant, which closed in December 2025, has been described by people involved in the transaction as one of the most structurally innovative power-sector financings ever closed in West Africa.

The transaction structure, as publicly described in lender materials:

- **Sponsor equity:** $620 million from a consortium led by a Nigerian energy holding company, with minority participation by a Gulf-based infrastructure fund and the African Finance Corporation.
- **Senior debt:** $2.1 billion at financial close, structured as a syndicated facility with a 17-year tenor and a five-year availability period.
- **Mezzanine debt:** $310 million provided by a club of three African development finance institutions, with subordination and equity-conversion features.
- **Currency split:** approximately 78 per cent U.S. dollar denomination, 22 per cent naira denomination — a deliberate choice driven by the project's mixed offtake structure.
- **Tenor:** Senior tenor of 17 years, with a balloon payment of approximately 8 per cent of principal at maturity.
- **Coupon:** A floating-rate structure with a spread that, the lead arrangers say, was tighter than any comparable West African power-sector financing in the past decade.

Insurance and credit-enhancement features:

- A Multilateral Investment Guarantee Agency (MIGA) political-risk insurance wrap covering approximately $640 million of the senior debt.
- A partial credit guarantee from the African Development Bank covering approximately $185 million of the naira-denominated tranche.
- A long-term offtake agreement with the Nigerian Bulk Electricity Trading Company (NBET) underpinning approximately 65 per cent of the projected revenue.

The transaction is, in Ms. Okonkwo's view, a template for what she calls "the next decade of West African power."

"We have spent twenty years trying to make IPP financings work in this market," she said. "The Ogun deal is the first one in which we got the structure right on the first pass. The next one will be easier. The one after that will be easier still. That is how a market gets built."

## Sidebar: Goldman's Africa Strategy in Context

Goldman Sachs's expanded sub-Saharan Africa strategy, announced in mid-2022 by then-Co-Head of Investment Banking Services Stephanie Cohen, has had a deliberately patient cadence.

The Lagos office was opened in early 2022 with a small team and a focused initial mandate covering Nigerian sovereign capital-markets work, a small number of financial-institutions coverage relationships, and the firm's growing infrastructure-finance business.

The 2024 reorganisation that brought all of sub-Saharan African coverage into a single Lagos-headquartered line was, in Ms. Okonkwo's words, "an acknowledgement that the satellite-office model was not going to be enough."

The firm now competes head-to-head with Standard Bank, FirstRand's RMB, Absa CIB, and the major French and U.K. lenders in the West and East African mid-cap and large-cap mandates. Its market share remains modest by comparison with the dominant African banks but, by the firm's internal measure, has grown steadily over the past three years.

Internal Goldman strategy documents reportedly contemplate a doubling of the Lagos office headcount over the next 36 months and the possible opening of a representative office in Nairobi by the end of 2027. The firm has declined to confirm or deny those reports.
