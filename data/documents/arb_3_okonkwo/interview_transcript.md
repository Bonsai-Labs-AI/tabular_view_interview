# "The treaty network is not the problem — the drafting is."

*An interview with Dr. Amara Okonkwo*
*Conducted for the* Journal of African Dispute Resolution*, Vol. 6, Issue 2 (Spring 2024). Interview held by videoconference, 12 February 2024. Lightly edited for clarity.*

---

**JADR: You've been on the LCIA Court for some time now. How has your view of investor-state arbitration in Africa evolved?**

**Okonkwo:** I've been on the Court for — let me think — about twelve years now, which is longer than it feels. When I started, the conversation in African capitals was overwhelmingly defensive: "How do we get out of these treaties? How do we limit exposure?" That has shifted. The conversation now is much more sophisticated. States want to know how to use the system, not just survive it. South Africa terminated; Tanzania terminated; but most of the continent has decided the better path is to renegotiate and to draft better. I agree with that view. The treaty network is not the problem — the drafting is.

**JADR: You've sat in both commercial and investor-state matters. Do you have a preference?**

**Okonkwo:** Honestly, no. The intellectual work is different. A commercial case is usually about the discipline of the contractual record; an investment case is about the discipline of the regulatory record and the customary international law overlay. Both reward careful reading. I do think the African infrastructure docket sits interestingly at the boundary — concession disputes often raise both contract and treaty arguments in the same proceeding. The Atlantic Gateway matter I recently chaired had that flavour, although obviously I cannot say more.

**JADR: Practitioners sometimes complain about the cost of African-seated arbitration. What's your reaction?**

**Okonkwo:** Two reactions. First, the cost of African-seated arbitration is, in my experience, lower than London or Paris, not higher — the institutional fees at CRCICA and the LCA are competitive, and local counsel rates in Lagos or Nairobi are a fraction of Magic Circle rates. Second, arbitrator fees are arbitrator fees; the seat does not change them very much. I charge GBP 750 an hour whether I'm sitting in London or Lagos, and that's broadly in line with the market for someone at my stage. If a tribunal is expensive, it's usually because the case is being run inefficiently, not because of geography.

**JADR: You did your doctorate in Geneva. Did you ever consider an academic career?**

**Okonkwo:** I did. I had an offer from Leiden in 2013 and one from SOAS a few years later. I turned both down. I enjoy writing — I try to publish one substantial piece a year — but I would have missed the practice. There is something about being in the room when a tribunal is actually deciding a case that a classroom cannot replicate.

**JADR: Final question — what should young African arbitration lawyers focus on?**

**Okonkwo:** Languages, first. If you can work in English, French, and Portuguese, you can take instructions from any government on the continent. Second, learn the OHADA system properly — most non-OHADA lawyers, including me when I started, underestimate it. And third, sit in real cases as a tribunal secretary before you ever try to get appointed. There is no shortcut to seeing how it actually works.
