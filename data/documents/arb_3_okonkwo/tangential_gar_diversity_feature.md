# Africa's Arbitration Renaissance: Diversity, Capacity, and the Tribunals of the Next Decade

*Global Arbitration Review — Long Feature*
*By Helena Marston, Senior Correspondent, GAR Africa Desk*
*Published 4 March 2026*

---

When the African Union's High Level Panel on Illicit Financial Flows out of Africa published its now-famous 2015 report, it estimated that the continent was losing approximately $50 billion a year to illicit outflows — a figure widely cited, occasionally contested, and broadly accepted within the African development community.

What the report did not say, but what has become increasingly clear in the decade since, is that an analogous, less publicly visible, and perhaps even larger leakage was occurring through the African investment-treaty arbitration system. Not in the form of corrupt outflows, but in the form of expertise — African disputes, often involving African parties, frequently arising out of investments in African economies, being adjudicated by tribunals containing almost no Africans.

That, finally, is changing. The change has been slow, uneven, and contested. But it is happening, and the GAR Africa Desk's twelve-month investigation into the composition of investment-treaty tribunals in Africa-related matters between 2014 and 2024 confirms it.

## A Decade of Numbers

Of the 247 publicly known investment-treaty arbitrations registered between 1 January 2014 and 31 December 2023 in which the respondent State was an African State or in which the substantive dispute concerned an investment located in an African State, GAR has identified the composition of 218 tribunals.

The summary numbers tell a story.

In the five-year period 2014–2018, of the 318 tribunal seats filled in Africa-related matters for which composition data is available, 27 — or 8.5 per cent — were occupied by arbitrators who hold African nationality. Two of those 27 were women.

In the five-year period 2019–2023, the comparable figure was 71 of 339, or 20.9 per cent. Fourteen of the 71 were women.

The shift is real, but it is incomplete. Sub-Saharan African nationals remain underrepresented as a proportion of arbitrator appointments relative to the proportion of Africa-related cases on the docket. African women remain particularly underrepresented. And while the trend line is encouraging, the absolute numbers — fourteen seats over five years, across an entire region of fifty-four countries — remain humblingly modest.

## The Generational Shift

Conversations with practitioners, institutional officials, and arbitrators conducted for this feature point to several drivers of the shift.

The most frequently cited is generational. The first generation of African international arbitrators — figures such as the late Judge Kéba Mbaye of Senegal, Professor Doudou Thiam of Senegal, and Professor Mohammed Bedjaoui of Algeria — built their reputations primarily through international public-law practice, often through the International Court of Justice, the International Law Commission, or senior diplomatic appointments. Their successors operated within a smaller and more institutionally constrained pipeline.

The current generation has grown up within a more institutionalised African investment-treaty practice. Many have come through major international law firms, through the legal departments of African ministries of justice and trade, through the African Development Bank's legal team, or through the secretariats of regional arbitration institutions in Cairo, Lagos, Mauritius, and Kigali. They have, in many cases, both common-law and civil-law training. They have, increasingly, post-graduate degrees from European, North American, and African institutions.

"There used to be a feeling that to be appointed as an arbitrator in a treaty case, you had to first be appointed to a UN body," said Salim Moollan KC, who has acted as both counsel and arbitrator in numerous Africa-related matters. "That has changed. The credibility now flows the other way. People are being appointed to UN bodies because they have proven themselves as arbitrators."

## The Counsel Pipeline

A second driver is the maturation of the African counsel bar. Major international firms — Allen & Overy (now A&O Shearman), Clifford Chance, Norton Rose Fulbright, White & Case, Herbert Smith Freehills, Three Crowns, and Volterra Fietta among others — have expanded their Africa-related practices substantially over the past decade, both through London-based teams and through formal or de facto presences in African legal markets.

This has had a virtuous effect on arbitrator appointments. Counsel familiar with African parties, with African legal systems, and with African contract drafting are better placed to advocate for the appointment of African arbitrators. And African arbitrators, in turn, have come to know counsel — and to be visible to them in a way that the previous generation often was not.

"We used to be invisible," said one prominent African practitioner who asked not to be named. "Not deliberately invisible — just invisible. The pipeline did not exist. The pipeline exists now."

## The Pan-African Institutions

A third driver is the growth and increasing credibility of regional African arbitration institutions. The Lagos Court of Arbitration, the Cairo Regional Centre for International Commercial Arbitration, the Kigali International Arbitration Centre, and the Mauritius International Arbitration Centre have, over the past decade, built up serious caseloads, panels of arbitrators, and procedural infrastructure.

Of particular interest is the growth in commercial caseload at the Lagos Court of Arbitration, which registered 312 cases in 2023, up from 78 in 2018. While the LCA is principally a commercial rather than investment-treaty institution, its growth has produced a deeper pool of experienced African arbitrators with the procedural sophistication required for institutional treaty work.

The Kigali International Arbitration Centre, which is younger, has positioned itself as a hub for East African commercial work and increasingly for investment-related disputes connected to the African Continental Free Trade Area's evolving dispute-settlement architecture. The Centre's panel of arbitrators currently includes 47 practitioners, of whom 19 are women — among the strongest gender-balance ratios of any African arbitral institution.

## The Rising Practitioners

A consistent theme of the practitioners interviewed for this feature was the emergence of a cohort of African investment-treaty practitioners in the mid-career bracket — broadly, those aged between 40 and 55 — who are increasingly being appointed to investment-treaty tribunals and who are likely to become the dominant African voices in the field over the coming decade.

Asked to name names, our interviewees were generous and varied. Among those most consistently mentioned — by counsel, by institutional officials, and by other arbitrators — as "rising practitioners worth watching" were:

- **Dr. Mouhamadou Ndour** (Senegal), partner at Cabinet Ndour & Associés (Dakar) and visiting professor at the Université Cheikh Anta Diop. Recently appointed as co-arbitrator in two ICSID matters involving Francophone African respondent States.
- **Ms. Funke Adekoya SAN** (Nigeria), founding partner of Æ-LEX (Lagos) and a member of the LCIA Court. Has acted as both counsel and arbitrator in numerous Africa-related matters and was the subject of GAR's *In Profile* feature in October 2024.
- **Dr. Amara N. Okonkwo** (Nigeria), London- and Lagos-based independent arbitrator with door tenancy at Gray's Inn Square Chambers. Investment-treaty and energy/mining specialist.
- **Mr. Henri Lagarde** (Côte d'Ivoire), independent arbitrator based in Paris and Abidjan, formerly of an international firm's Paris arbitration practice.
- **Dr. Tendai Biti** (Zimbabwe), formerly the Republic of Zimbabwe's Minister of Finance and now in private practice as both counsel and arbitrator in commercial and investment-treaty matters.
- **Ms. Wanjiru Kamau-Rutenberg** (Kenya), formerly director of African Women in Agricultural Research and Development and now active in arbitration and ADR practice.
- **Mr. Sipho Dlamini SC** (South Africa), senior counsel at the Johannesburg Bar with an international investment-treaty practice.
- **Dr. Sonia Nour** (Egypt), of counsel to a regional Cairo firm, with experience in both investment-treaty arbitration and ICC commercial arbitration.
- **Ms. Aïssatou Diallo** (Mali), independent arbitrator and former senior legal adviser to the African Development Bank.
- **Mr. Kwame Asare-Boateng** (Ghana), partner at a leading Accra firm and a member of the ICSID Panel of Arbitrators.
- **Dr. Lillian Mukiibi** (Uganda), formerly with the OECD Investment Division, now in private practice in Kampala and London.
- **Mr. Carlos da Silva** (Mozambique), partner at a leading Maputo firm specialising in mining and infrastructure disputes.

The list is, of course, not exhaustive. It also reflects the geographic and linguistic biases of the GAR Africa Desk's network of interlocutors. African civil-law practitioners and Lusophone practitioners are, we acknowledge, likely under-represented relative to their actual presence and capacity.

## The Counterargument

Not all of the practitioners we spoke to were uniformly optimistic. Several pointed to a continuing tendency for African appointments to be "consolation-prize appointments" — co-arbitrator appointments rather than presiding-arbitrator appointments, particularly in the highest-value matters.

A 2024 analysis published in the *Journal of International Arbitration* by Professor Olufunmi Adekoya of the University of Lagos Faculty of Law found that, of the African nationals appointed to investment-treaty tribunals in 2019–2023, only 17 per cent were appointed as presiding arbitrator. The equivalent figure for European and North American nationals was 41 per cent.

"The numbers are getting better," Professor Adekoya told us. "But they are getting better at the bottom. The chair is still mostly elsewhere."

She added: "I don't want to be a sceptic. I want to be a realist. The system is moving. It is not moving fast enough."

## The Institutional Response

The two principal institutional bodies — ICSID and the PCA — have, over the past several years, made deliberate efforts to address the representativeness question.

ICSID's Diversity Roster Initiative, launched in 2022, makes available to parties a curated list of qualified arbitrators from underrepresented regions and demographics. As of December 2025, the roster contained 218 names, of whom 67 were African nationals. The Centre has reported a "measurable but moderate" uptake of the roster in subsequent appointments.

The PCA has taken a less formal but, by some accounts, equally effective approach, through its Junior Lawyers programme and its quiet matchmaking work between African and other parties seeking presiding arbitrators and qualified candidates.

The major arbitration institutions outside the investment-treaty space — the ICC, the LCIA, the SCC, and the HKIAC — have also taken steps. The ICC reports that 23 per cent of its African appointments in 2023 went to African nationals, up from 8 per cent in 2018. The LCIA reports a smaller but parallel shift, from 6 per cent in 2018 to 17 per cent in 2023.

## The Capacity Question

Perhaps the deepest question — and the one to which we received the most divergent answers — concerns capacity-building.

The optimists believe that the African counsel and arbitrator pipeline is, finally, of sufficient depth and breadth to sustain itself without further institutional intervention. They point to the steady stream of African graduates of the LLM programmes at NYU, Columbia, Harvard, Cambridge, Oxford, Geneva, Leiden, and Sciences Po, many of whom have returned to African practice. They point to the success of African Bar conferences, of African Society of International Law, and of regional moots such as the Africa Round of the Vis Moot and the Foreign Direct Investment Moot.

The pessimists believe that the pipeline is still dangerously narrow at the senior end, that too few African arbitrators have the credibility and the case experience to be appointed as president of a tribunal in a high-value treaty case, and that the gains of the past decade could be reversed if the institutional support and the deliberate counsel-side advocacy that has produced them is withdrawn.

Both perspectives have merit. The truth, as is so often the case, is probably somewhere in between.

## Looking Forward

The next decade will be decisive. The African Continental Free Trade Area's evolving investment protocol is likely to generate new institutional architecture for investor-State dispute settlement on the continent. The Pan-African Investment Code, if it is ratified by enough States to become operative, will generate a new caseload that, for reasons both substantive and political, will demand more African arbitrators.

And the underlying economics are changing. Africa-related disputes are no longer a niche corner of the investment-treaty system. They are a growing share of the docket — and they are increasingly involving African parties on both sides, not only African respondent States but also African claimants suing other African States, and African claimants suing non-African States in respect of African investments.

The system, in short, will need African arbitrators. The question is whether the system will appoint them.

The evidence of the past decade gives some reason for cautious optimism. The evidence of the present is that the work is far from done.

## A Note on Methodology

The GAR Africa Desk's database of Africa-related investment-treaty tribunals is compiled from publicly available information, including ICSID public case files, PCA case databases, the *Investment Treaty Arbitration Reporter*, the *Investment Treaty News*, and supplementary information provided on a confidential basis by counsel and institutional officials.

We acknowledge that our coverage is necessarily incomplete: a substantial share of investment-treaty arbitrations remain confidential, and tribunal compositions in such matters are not publicly disclosed. We estimate that we have captured approximately 88 per cent of the relevant universe for the period covered.

We are grateful to the practitioners, institutional officials, and academic commentators who shared their time and expertise with us in the preparation of this feature. Errors and omissions remain, of course, our own.

## Sidebar: Voices From the Bar

In the course of preparing this feature, the GAR Africa Desk spoke with more than 60 practitioners across 18 African and four non-African jurisdictions. The following short excerpts, all on the record except where otherwise noted, give a flavour of the range of perspectives encountered.

"What I want to see is more African presidents of tribunals. Not co-arbitrators. Presidents. The chair is the chair. The chair runs the case."
— *Adaeze Okeke, partner, Lagos*

"The progress is real and it is meaningful and we should celebrate it. But we should also be honest that the progress is uneven across regions. Francophone Africa, in particular, is still under-served."
— *Senior practitioner, Paris (background)*

"The growth of African arbitration institutions is the single most important development of the past decade. Without it, we would not have the case experience that produces credible arbitrator candidates. The institutions matter."
— *Funke Adekoya SAN, founding partner, Æ-LEX, Lagos*

"I think we still under-appreciate how much of this has been driven by client demand. African parties, particularly African sovereigns, have been more willing to ask for African arbitrators. Counsel have responded. Institutions have responded. But the demand had to come first."
— *Henri Lagarde, independent arbitrator, Abidjan and Paris*

"What I worry about is the next generation. The current generation has made the breakthrough. The next generation needs to consolidate it. And the pipeline of practitioners in their early thirties who are positioned to become serious treaty arbitrators in fifteen years is, frankly, thinner than it should be."
— *Mouhamadou Ndour, partner, Dakar*

## Sidebar: Methodology Notes for the GAR Africa Tribunals Tracker

The GAR Africa Tribunals Tracker, which underpins the data presented in this feature, is a proprietary dataset maintained by the GAR Africa Desk since 2014.

The Tracker is compiled from the following primary sources:

- The ICSID public case files
- The PCA case database
- The *Investment Treaty Arbitration Reporter*
- The IISD *Investment Treaty News*
- The Italaw database of investment-treaty awards
- Publicly available case files maintained by national courts in seat or enforcement jurisdictions
- Direct submissions from counsel and institutional officials, made on a confidential basis

The Tracker covers all known Africa-related investment-treaty arbitrations registered since 1 January 2010.

For each case, the Tracker records (where the information is publicly available):

- The case name, institutional administering body, and case number
- The respondent State and, where appropriate, the State entity sued
- The claimant's name and nationality
- The treaty or other instrument under which consent to arbitration is given
- The substantive sector of the underlying investment
- The composition of the tribunal, with each arbitrator's nationality, gender, and appointing party
- The procedural calendar
- The outcome, where one has been reached

The Tracker is available to GAR subscribers as a downloadable dataset, with the proviso that confidential information received from counsel sources is excluded from the downloadable version.
