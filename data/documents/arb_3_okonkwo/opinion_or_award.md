## I. Introduction

1. This arbitration arises out of a 30-year build-operate-transfer concession (the "Concession") granted to Claimant's local project company in 2014 for the design, financing, construction, and operation of a deepwater container terminal on the Atlantic coast of the Respondent State. The dispute concerns the lawfulness of two ministerial orders issued in March and July 2021 (the "Disputed Orders"), which together suspended Claimant's exclusive berthing rights and re-allocated approximately 38 per cent of projected throughput to a competing state-controlled operator.

2. Claimant commenced these proceedings under Article 41 of the Concession on 9 February 2022, claiming damages in the principal amount of EUR 612,400,000, together with pre- and post-award interest, costs, and a declaration that the Disputed Orders constituted a breach of the Concession's stabilisation clause (Article 27).

## II. Procedural Posture

3. The Tribunal was constituted on 22 June 2022. Procedural Order No. 1 was issued on 19 July 2022 following a case management conference held by videoconference. A hearing on jurisdiction and merits took place in Paris from 6–15 May 2023. Post-hearing briefs were filed on 4 August 2023. The Tribunal declared the proceedings closed under Article 27 of the 2021 ICC Rules on 22 September 2023.

## III. Jurisdiction

4. Respondent's jurisdictional objections — principally that the Disputed Orders are acts *jure imperii* outside the contractual arbitration clause — were addressed and rejected in the Tribunal's Decision on Jurisdiction of 12 December 2022 and are not reopened here. The Tribunal reaffirms that the Concession's broad arbitration clause ("any dispute *arising out of or in connection with* this Agreement") captures regulatory acts that directly impair the Claimant's contractual entitlements, irrespective of their formal characterisation under domestic law.

## IV. Applicable Law on the Merits

5. The Concession is governed by its own terms and, on a subsidiary basis, by the laws of the Respondent State. The Tribunal has further had regard to general principles of public international law to the extent that Article 27 of the Concession expressly incorporates such principles in respect of the stabilisation undertaking.

## V. The Tribunal's Findings on Breach

6. The Tribunal finds, by majority (Mr. Dlamini SC dissenting in part), that the Disputed Orders, taken together, constitute a breach of Articles 14 (exclusivity), 19 (regulatory due process), and 27 (stabilisation) of the Concession. The Tribunal does not accept Respondent's contention that the Orders were a *bona fide* exercise of police power necessary to address congestion at the port; the contemporaneous record, and in particular the inter-ministerial memorandum of 11 February 2021 (Exhibit R-117), indicates that the decisive motivation was the protection of a competing state-owned operator.

## VI. Quantum

8. For the reasons set out above, the Tribunal awards Claimant the sum of **EUR 418,900,000** in damages, together with compound interest at six-month EURIBOR plus 2 per cent from 1 August 2021 until payment.
