# Dr. Amara Okonkwo on Childhood Pneumonia and Malaria Co-Infection: A Profile

*West African Journal of Medicine — Research Profile Series, Vol. 47, Issue 3 (March 2026)*

---

## A Clinician-Scientist at the Bedside and the Bench

In the paediatric high-dependency ward at University College Hospital, Ibadan, the day begins early. By 06:45 most mornings, Dr. Amara Okonkwo — consultant paediatric pulmonologist and senior research fellow at the Institute for Advanced Medical Research and Training (IAMRAT) — has already reviewed the overnight admissions, signed off on two oxygen-weaning protocols, and is on her way to the genomics laboratory on the second floor of the College of Medicine building.

"You cannot separate the wheezing infant from the *Plasmodium* genome," she says, by way of explaining why her career sits awkwardly across two specialties that, in Nigeria's hospital hierarchy, rarely speak to one another. "If we want to bring down under-five mortality, we have to stop pretending that the lungs and the parasite are in different conversations."

Dr. Okonkwo, 44, completed her MBBS at the University of Ibadan in 2004, her residency in paediatrics at UCH in 2010, and a fellowship in paediatric pulmonology at the Red Cross War Memorial Children's Hospital in Cape Town in 2013. Her PhD, awarded by the University of Oxford in 2018 under the supervision of Professor Kathryn Maitland, examined the role of host single-nucleotide polymorphisms in modulating respiratory complications of severe falciparum malaria in West African children.

## The Ibadan Malaria-Lung Cohort

The work for which she is now best known began in late 2019, when she and her colleague Dr. Tunde Adebanjo enrolled the first 50 children in what would become the Ibadan Malaria-Lung Cohort (IMLC). The cohort, now 1,847 children strong across four sites in Oyo, Osun, and Kwara States, follows infants and toddlers admitted with severe malaria and tracks pulmonary outcomes at 30 days, 6 months, 12 months, and 24 months post-discharge.

The early findings, published in *The Lancet Global Health* in February 2024 (Okonkwo AN, Adebanjo TO, Maitland K, et al., "Pulmonary sequelae of severe falciparum malaria in West African children: a prospective cohort study"), suggested that approximately 22.4 per cent of children who survived severe malaria with respiratory distress went on to develop clinically significant restrictive lung disease by 24 months — a figure substantially higher than previous retrospective estimates had indicated.

"That number changed the conversation in our wards," recalls Professor Olusegun Oyedeji, head of paediatrics at UCH. "We had been discharging children, recording survival, and counting it as a win. Amara forced us to count again."

## Genomics: The HBB and G6PD Story

A second strand of her work, conducted in collaboration with the Wellcome Sanger Institute and the African Centre of Excellence for Genomics of Infectious Diseases at Redeemer's University, has focused on the interaction between haemoglobinopathy alleles (particularly HbAS and HbAC) and acute lung injury during severe malaria.

In a paper published in *Nature Communications* in November 2025 ("Heterozygous HbC modifies acute respiratory distress phenotype in paediatric severe malaria: a multi-ancestry genome-wide association study"), Dr. Okonkwo and 31 co-authors reported a previously uncharacterised modifier locus on chromosome 11p15.4 that appeared to attenuate the development of pulmonary oedema in HbAC heterozygotes. The finding has been cautiously welcomed by the field, with editorials in *PLOS Medicine* and the *British Medical Journal* describing it as "hypothesis-generating but not yet translational."

She is quick to caution against over-reading the result.

"We have a signal," she says. "We do not have a drug. We do not have a screening test. What we have is a reason to keep looking."

## A Clinician First

Despite her growing international profile — she gave the keynote at the American Thoracic Society International Conference in San Diego in May 2025, and was named to the WHO Strategic Advisory Group on Severe Malaria in early 2026 — Dr. Okonkwo still spends three full clinical days each week on the paediatric wards at UCH.

"The bench tells you what is possible," she says. "The bedside tells you what is necessary. I cannot do one without the other."

Her clinical reputation in Ibadan is formidable. Junior residents speak of "Okonkwo rounds" with a mix of dread and reverence — long, slow, methodical ward rounds at which every wheeze must be characterised, every chest film re-read at the workstation, and every parental consent re-explained in Yoruba or Igbo by the consultant herself if the resident's translation is judged inadequate.

"She has no patience for shortcuts," says Dr. Fatima Abubakar, a fourth-year resident. "She also has no patience for hierarchy. If you say something wrong, she will tell you. If your senior says something wrong, she will tell them too."

## The IAMRAT Laboratory

The genomics laboratory at IAMRAT, which Dr. Okonkwo has co-directed with Professor Catherine Falade since 2021, occupies a renovated wing of the Institute's main building. The wet-lab capacity — two Illumina NextSeq 2000 instruments, an Oxford Nanopore PromethION, and a recently commissioned 10x Genomics Chromium X — is modest by Western standards but represents one of the most capable paediatric infectious-disease genomics platforms in West Africa.

The laboratory's workflow is tightly coupled to the clinical wards: residual peripheral blood samples from consented IMLC participants are processed within 48 hours of collection, with DNA extracted, quality-controlled, and either sequenced locally or batched for shipment to the Sanger Institute under the cohort's data-sharing agreement.

Dr. Okonkwo is particularly proud of the laboratory's training mission. Since 2022, IAMRAT has hosted 14 Nigerian and 6 visiting African graduate students for periods of 6–18 months, with funding from the Wellcome Trust's DELTAS Africa initiative and the African Academy of Sciences.

## Public Engagement and Policy

Beyond the laboratory, Dr. Okonkwo has emerged as one of Nigeria's most visible advocates for paediatric respiratory health. She serves on the Federal Ministry of Health's Technical Working Group on Childhood Pneumonia, where she has been credited with pushing through revised oxygen-therapy guidelines in 2024 that mandated pulse oximetry screening at all primary-health-care facilities receiving federal subsidies.

She is also a regular contributor to *The Guardian (Nigeria)* and *Premium Times*, where her op-eds on vaccine equity, antimicrobial stewardship, and the chronic underfunding of paediatric critical care have drawn both praise and criticism.

In an October 2025 column titled "Our children deserve oxygen, not slogans," she wrote: "We have spent two decades building dashboards. We have not spent two decades building oxygen plants. The dashboards will not breathe for the child."

## On Being Asked About the Arbitrator

A persistent confusion — particularly in international media coverage of African public-health figures — has been the conflation of Dr. Okonkwo with a London-based international arbitrator of the same name. The two have, on at least three occasions, been confused in conference programmes, news indices, and academic citation databases.

Dr. Okonkwo, the paediatric pulmonologist, treats the confusion with weary humour.

"I have never set foot in a chambers in my life," she said in a brief aside during our interview. "I would not know an investment treaty if it walked into my clinic. My sister-in-law is the lawyer in our family, and she is a tax barrister in Lagos. So no — the arbitrator is not me, and she is not my relative either. There are many Okonkwos. It is a common name in eastern Nigeria."

She has, however, written privately to ORCID and to the editors of two indexing services to request clearer disambiguation of her publication record. Whether the request has been honoured is, as of this writing, unclear.

## Recognition

In 2023, Dr. Okonkwo was awarded the Nigerian National Order of Merit Prize for Medicine, the country's highest scientific honour. In 2024, she became the youngest fellow ever elected to the Nigerian Academy of Science in the medical sciences class. In 2025, the African Academy of Sciences named her among its inaugural cohort of "African Science Leaders." Her election to the Royal College of Physicians (London) as an honorary fellow was announced in January 2026.

She has declined to comment on persistent rumours that she has been approached to lead the WHO Regional Office for Africa's child-health programme.

"I am a doctor," she says. "I will go where I can do the most good. I do not have a five-year plan. I have patients to see this afternoon."

## A Note on Reproducibility

The IMLC cohort's de-identified clinical phenotype data and the GWAS summary statistics from the HbC paper are available through the African Genome Variation Project's controlled-access repository, with applications administered by the Sanger Institute Data Access Committee. Sequencing reads are deposited in the European Nucleotide Archive (study accession PRJEB78421).

Dr. Okonkwo has been a vocal advocate for African-led data stewardship of African biological samples. She sits on the advisory board of the Human Heredity and Health in Africa (H3Africa) initiative and was one of the lead authors of the 2023 H3Africa position paper on equitable data sharing in African genomics research.

"The data must serve our children," she said at the H3Africa annual meeting in Kigali in April 2025. "If our data only serves journals, we have failed."

## Sidebar: The Ibadan Malaria-Lung Cohort (IMLC) — Key Facts

The Ibadan Malaria-Lung Cohort is a prospective observational study of children admitted to participating hospitals with WHO-defined severe malaria.

Site distribution:

- University College Hospital, Ibadan (lead site, Oyo State)
- Wesley Guild Hospital, Ilesa (Osun State)
- Ladoke Akintola University Teaching Hospital, Ogbomoso (Oyo State)
- Federal Medical Centre, Ido-Ekiti (Ekiti State)
- General Hospital, Offa (Kwara State)

Inclusion criteria:

- Aged between 6 months and 9 years inclusive at the time of admission
- WHO-defined severe malaria with positive blood-film or rapid-diagnostic-test confirmation of *Plasmodium falciparum*
- Admission to the participating site within 48 hours of symptom onset
- Parental or guardian consent for cohort participation and biobank sample collection

Exclusion criteria:

- Pre-existing chronic respiratory disease (cystic fibrosis, structural lung disease, prior tuberculosis)
- HIV-positive status with CD4 count under 200 cells per cubic millimetre
- Severe acute malnutrition with weight-for-height z-score under minus 3
- Concomitant systemic bacterial infection requiring intensive-care admission

Cohort enrolment, by year:

- 2019: 87 children
- 2020: 142 children
- 2021: 318 children
- 2022: 401 children
- 2023: 412 children
- 2024: 387 children
- 2025 (through 30 November): 100 children

Total enrolment, project to date: 1,847 children.

Total reported deaths (at any point during follow-up): 121 children, of whom 84 died during the index admission, 19 died within 90 days of discharge, and 18 died beyond 90 days.

Total reported respiratory follow-up at 24 months: 1,082 children with complete spirometry data; 412 children with restrictive-pattern lung function (defined as forced vital capacity below the lower limit of normal for age and height).

Funding: Wellcome Trust Senior Fellowship in Public Health (Okonkwo, 2019–2024); MRC African Research Leaders Award (Okonkwo and Adebanjo, 2022–2027); National Institutes of Health R01 grant (Okonkwo, Maitland, and Falade, 2024–2029); supplementary support from the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation and the African Academy of Sciences.

## Sidebar: A Day in the Ward

The following is an abbreviated and de-identified account of one Tuesday morning in the paediatric high-dependency ward at UCH, as observed by the present author in October 2025.

06:45. Dr. Okonkwo arrives. She reviews the overnight admissions log with the on-call paediatric registrar, Dr. Akinleye. There are eleven new admissions overnight: four with severe malaria, two with bronchiolitis, one with a probable urinary tract infection, one with status epilepticus, one with a snakebite, and two awaiting further diagnostic workup.

07:00. Hand-over round begins. Dr. Okonkwo asks Dr. Akinleye to walk her through each child. She corrects, gently but firmly, his characterisation of one child's wheeze as "audible without stethoscope" — she has just walked past the bed and considers the wheeze to be only faintly audible, and she wants the chart updated to reflect that.

07:35. The team moves to bed three. A six-year-old boy from Ido-Ekiti, transferred late the previous night with severe malaria and respiratory distress. He is on oxygen by nasal cannula at three litres per minute, with saturation in the low nineties. Dr. Okonkwo examines him systematically — auscultates the chest, palpates the spleen, checks capillary refill — and then sits down on the edge of the bed to explain to the mother, in Yoruba, what she is seeing and what she expects over the next twelve hours.

08:10. A more difficult conversation, at bed seven. A two-year-old girl with severe malaria, transferred the previous afternoon from a peripheral hospital. The clinical picture is deteriorating. Dr. Okonkwo speaks to the parents for nearly fifteen minutes. The team waits at a respectful distance.

09:50. Rounds conclude. The team adjourns to the ward office. There is a brief discussion of one of the new admissions whose presentation is, in Dr. Okonkwo's view, not classical for severe malaria. She suggests bacterial-co-infection workup and a chest X-ray.

10:30. Dr. Okonkwo leaves the ward for the laboratory. The morning's blood samples will be processed within the day.

The author is grateful to the patients and families of the UCH paediatric high-dependency ward for permission to observe the morning's rounds, and to Dr. Okonkwo and her team for their generous explanations throughout.
