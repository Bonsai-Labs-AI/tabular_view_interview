# Summary Case Report

## Atlantic Gateway Infrastructure Holdings B.V. v. The Republic of [Redacted]

**Investment Arbitration Reporter — Case Summary Series**
**Issue No. 412, January 2026**
**Reporter: Magdalena Reyes-Holm**

---

## Bibliographic Note

This summary case report covers the final award rendered by a tribunal of the International Chamber of Commerce in ICC Case No. 26,4xx/AGF, *Atlantic Gateway Infrastructure Holdings B.V. v. The Republic of [Redacted]*, dated 14 November 2023.

The summary is prepared from the redacted text of the final award (excerpt) released by the parties in mid-2024 and from supplementary procedural documents made available through the Reporter's standard sources. The Reporter has not had access to the full text of the award, and the summary should be read with that caveat.

The case is, in the view of the editors of the Investment Arbitration Reporter, of substantial doctrinal interest in three respects: (i) the tribunal's reasoning on the relationship between regulatory acts *jure imperii* and contractually delimited dispute-resolution clauses; (ii) the tribunal's treatment of stabilisation clauses in concession agreements as incorporating public-international-law principles; and (iii) the tribunal's quantum methodology, which awarded a substantial but reduced damages figure relative to the principal claim.

---

## I. Background

The dispute arose out of a 30-year build-operate-transfer concession (the "Concession") granted in 2014 to the local project company of Atlantic Gateway Infrastructure Holdings B.V., a Netherlands-incorporated entity. The Concession concerned the design, financing, construction, and operation of a deepwater container terminal on the Atlantic coast of the Respondent State.

The substantive dispute concerned the lawfulness of two ministerial orders — issued in March 2021 and July 2021 (together, the "Disputed Orders") — which between them suspended the Claimant's exclusive berthing rights at the terminal and re-allocated approximately 38 per cent of projected throughput to a competing state-controlled port operator.

Atlantic Gateway commenced ICC arbitration on 9 February 2022 under Article 41 of the Concession, claiming damages in the principal sum of EUR 612.4 million together with pre- and post-award interest, costs, and a declaration that the Disputed Orders breached the stabilisation clause at Article 27 of the Concession.

---

## II. The Tribunal

The tribunal was composed of:

- **Dr. Amara N. Okonkwo** (presiding arbitrator) — Nigerian national, London- and Lagos-based independent arbitrator.
- **Professor Henri Lagarde** (co-arbitrator appointed by the Claimant) — French national, professor of international economic law.
- **Mr. Sipho Dlamini SC** (co-arbitrator appointed by the Respondent) — South African senior counsel.

The seat of arbitration was Paris, France. The governing law was the Concession Agreement of 2014; subsidiarily, the laws of the Respondent State; and, where applicable, public international law principles incorporated by reference into Article 27 of the Concession.

---

## III. Procedural History

The tribunal was constituted on 22 June 2022. Procedural Order No. 1 was issued on 19 July 2022 following a case-management conference held by videoconference. A combined hearing on jurisdiction and merits was held in Paris from 6 to 15 May 2023. Post-hearing briefs were filed on 4 August 2023. The proceedings were declared closed under Article 27 of the 2021 ICC Rules on 22 September 2023. The award was issued in Paris on 14 November 2023.

The Respondent's principal jurisdictional objection was rejected in the tribunal's Decision on Jurisdiction of 12 December 2022. That decision, which was incorporated into the final award by reference, held that the Concession's broad arbitration clause — "any dispute *arising out of or in connection with* this Agreement" — captured regulatory acts that directly impaired the Claimant's contractual entitlements, regardless of their formal characterisation under the domestic law of the Respondent State as acts *jure imperii*.

The Reporter notes that the jurisdictional decision is itself of interest as a reasoned application of the principle that the formal categorisation of an act as sovereign or commercial under domestic law is not dispositive of its characterisation for the purposes of a contractual arbitration clause. The tribunal's reasoning on this point is consistent with the line of ICC awards beginning with the well-known *SPP v. Egypt* and *Pyramids* line of cases.

---

## IV. Findings on Breach

The tribunal found, by majority (Mr. Dlamini SC dissenting in part), that the Disputed Orders — taken together — constituted breaches of three provisions of the Concession:

- **Article 14 (exclusivity)** — through the diversion of throughput to the competing state-controlled operator;
- **Article 19 (regulatory due process)** — through the issuance of the Orders without the consultations and impact assessments required by the Concession;
- **Article 27 (stabilisation)** — through the unilateral alteration of the regulatory framework in a manner adverse to the Claimant's contractual rights.

The tribunal rejected the Respondent's principal substantive defence — that the Disputed Orders constituted a *bona fide* exercise of police power necessary to address port congestion. The contemporaneous documentary record, and in particular an inter-ministerial memorandum dated 11 February 2021 (admitted as Exhibit R-117), demonstrated to the tribunal's satisfaction that the decisive motivation behind the Orders was the protection of a competing state-owned port operator, rather than any genuine concern with congestion management.

The Reporter highlights the tribunal's discussion of Exhibit R-117 as an instructive example of how contemporaneous internal government documents, when produced in arbitration, can determine the outcome on the question of regulatory motive. The memorandum, on the tribunal's reading, simply could not be reconciled with the Respondent's *bona fide* police-power defence.

The tribunal's analysis of the stabilisation clause, contained at paragraphs 142 through 311 of the award (which are not included in the publicly released excerpt), is reported by counsel sources to have been notably substantive. The clause, according to those sources, was read by the tribunal as incorporating general principles of public international law concerning the protection of legitimate expectations and the obligation of the State to act in good faith in respect of contractual undertakings. We hope to publish a fuller analytical note on the tribunal's stabilisation-clause reasoning when the full text of the award becomes available.

---

## V. Quantum

The tribunal awarded the Claimant the sum of **EUR 418.9 million** in damages, together with compound interest at six-month EURIBOR plus 2 per cent from 1 August 2021 until payment in full.

The amount awarded represented approximately 68 per cent of the principal sum claimed. The Reporter understands that the discount reflects the tribunal's modifications to the Claimant's discounted-cash-flow model, including in particular: (i) a higher discount rate than that proposed by the Claimant's quantum experts; (ii) a more conservative throughput assumption for the diverted volume; and (iii) a temporal limitation on certain elements of the lost-profits claim. The full reasoning of the tribunal on quantum is contained in the omitted paragraphs of the award.

The award also ordered the Respondent to bear 70 per cent of the costs of the arbitration (including the tribunal's fees and expenses, the ICC administrative charge, and the Claimant's reasonable legal costs).

---

## VI. The Dissent

Mr. Dlamini SC's partial dissent — which has not been publicly released — is understood from sources close to the proceedings to have focused on the tribunal's quantum analysis and, in particular, on the treatment of the Claimant's lost-profits claim for the post-2030 period.

The Reporter has not seen the dissent and cannot summarise it further. We hope to publish a separate note on the dissent if and when it becomes available.

## VII. Significance

In the Reporter's assessment, the award is of significance for the following reasons:

1. **Stabilisation clause as a vehicle for public international law principles.** The tribunal's treatment of Article 27 of the Concession as incorporating general principles of public international law (rather than relying solely on the domestic law of the Respondent State or on the four corners of the contract) is a relatively unusual approach in ICC commercial arbitration, and may prove influential in future cases involving concession agreements with stabilisation provisions.

2. **Regulatory act / contractual breach.** The tribunal's finding that an act formally characterised as a sovereign regulatory act under domestic law may nonetheless constitute a contractual breach for the purposes of an arbitration clause has implications for the broader debate about the boundary between investment-treaty and commercial arbitration.

3. **Quantum methodology.** The tribunal's substantial but reduced damages award reflects what appears to be a careful and well-reasoned quantum analysis, and may serve as a useful reference point for tribunals in future infrastructure-concession cases.

4. **The contemporaneous documentary record.** The decisive role of the inter-ministerial memorandum (Exhibit R-117) underscores the importance of document-production practice in cases involving disputed regulatory motives.

## VIII. Post-Award Procedure

The Reporter understands that the Respondent State has applied to the French courts for annulment of the award under Article 1520 of the French Code of Civil Procedure. The annulment application is currently pending before the Paris Court of Appeal. We will report further once a decision is rendered.

There has, as of the date of this report, been no public announcement of voluntary compliance with the award.

## Appendix D — Glossary of Key Terms

For the benefit of readers less familiar with the terminology of investment arbitration, the following short glossary is provided.

- **Concession agreement.** A long-term contract between a state (or state entity) and a private investor under which the investor is granted the right to construct, operate, and (often) eventually transfer back to the state, a piece of infrastructure or a productive asset.

- **Stabilisation clause.** A contractual provision in a long-term concession agreement under which the state undertakes that adverse subsequent changes in the legal or regulatory framework will not be applied to the investor, or that the investor will be compensated for any such adverse changes. Stabilisation clauses come in many forms; the form considered in this case incorporated public international law principles in respect of the stabilisation undertaking.

- ***Jure imperii*.** Latin phrase meaning "by sovereign right." Refers to acts performed by a state in its sovereign capacity, as distinguished from acts performed in a commercial capacity (*jure gestionis*). The distinction is important in a number of areas of international law, including state immunity from suit.

- ***Bona fide*.** Latin phrase meaning "in good faith." Often used to characterise a regulatory act undertaken for genuine public-interest reasons, as distinguished from one undertaken for protectionist or other illegitimate reasons.

- **Article 27 of the ICC Rules (2021).** The provision of the 2021 ICC Arbitration Rules governing the closing of proceedings.

- **Annulment.** A post-award procedure under which the validity of an arbitration award may be challenged before a national court (in the case of commercial awards) or, in the case of ICSID Convention awards, before an ad hoc Committee. The grounds for annulment are typically limited and procedural rather than substantive.

- **EURIBOR.** Euro Interbank Offered Rate. A benchmark interest rate at which Eurozone banks lend to one another, frequently used as a reference rate for interest on damages awards in Eurozone-denominated arbitrations.
