# Professor Amara Okonkwo and the Reframing of Igbo Dialectology

*Journal of African Languages and Linguistics — Featured Scholar Profile, Vol. 46, Issue 2 (Autumn 2025)*

---

## Introduction

When Professor Amara Okonkwo joined the Department of Linguistics at the School of Oriental and African Studies (SOAS), University of London, in 2009 as a lecturer in West African languages, the formal study of Igbo dialectology in British academic linguistics had, by general consensus, been in a slow and embarrassed retreat for nearly two decades.

The reasons were familiar enough. The canonical surveys of the language — those of Williamson (1972), Manfredi (1991), and Emenanjo (1978/2015 revised) — had partitioned the Igbo dialect continuum in ways that were demonstrably contested by speakers, often invisible in field recordings, and increasingly difficult to defend on either phonological or sociolinguistic grounds. The reference grammar tradition, meanwhile, had grown so dependent on the so-called Central Igbo standard (loosely, the variety codified by the Society for Promoting Igbo Language and Culture in the 1970s) that any departure from it was treated as deviation rather than data.

It was into this fatigued field that Professor Okonkwo arrived — a young scholar, then 32, with a PhD from the University of Edinburgh on the tonal phonology of Anambra Igbo (supervised by Peter Ladefoged's former student James Kirby), a fluency in five Igbo varieties (Owerri, Onitsha, Nsukka, Mbaise, and Ohuhu), and what one colleague at SOAS would later describe as "a positively allergic intolerance for received dialectological wisdom."

In the sixteen years since, she has produced what is now widely regarded as the most consequential rethinking of Igbo dialect classification in three generations.

## A Monograph That Has Already Acquired Its Own Bibliography

Professor Okonkwo's *Tone, Register, and the Boundaries of Igbo: A Dialectological Reframing* (Oxford University Press, 2023; 547 pages, with three accompanying audio archives) is, by any reasonable measure, the academic event of the past three years in West African linguistics.

The monograph's central argument — that the traditional five- or six-cluster taxonomy of Igbo dialects has misread tonal microvariation as evidence of major-cluster boundaries, when in fact those boundaries are sociolinguistic artefacts of post-1970 standardisation pressures — has provoked sustained debate at every major Africanist linguistics conference since its publication.

A symposium devoted to the book at the 56th Annual Conference on African Linguistics (Yale, March 2025) ran for two full days, with over 30 invited papers. A book panel at the West African Linguistic Society meeting in Accra in August 2024 was, by all accounts, both rigorous and intermittently combative. *Language*, the flagship journal of the Linguistic Society of America, ran a 40-page review symposium in its September 2024 issue, with contributions from six leading scholars including Professor Akinbiyi Akinlabi (Rutgers), Professor Carlos Gussenhoven (Radboud), and Professor M. Eunice Adaku (University of Nigeria, Nsukka).

The reviews have not been uniformly admiring. Professor Adaku, in particular, has argued that Okonkwo's reliance on a relatively narrow set of urban consultants in Onitsha and Owerri risks reproducing precisely the kind of standardisation bias the monograph claims to dismantle. Okonkwo's reply, published in *Language* in March 2025, was characteristically pointed: "Professor Adaku is correct that our consultant base could and should be broader. She is incorrect that the conclusion changes."

## The Tonal Phonology

The technical core of Okonkwo's work is her treatment of what she terms "register-mediated tonal alignment" — the observation that what previous dialectologists had transcribed as discrete high-, low-, and downstep-tone distinctions in fact patterned, in spontaneous speech across all five major Igbo varieties, as gradient register shifts mediated by phrase-level prosodic boundaries.

The argument rests on a corpus of 1,247 hours of recorded conversational Igbo collected between 2011 and 2021 in eleven Igbo-speaking communities in Nigeria. The corpus, the largest of its kind, has been deposited (with appropriate access controls) in the SOAS World Languages Archive and is available to qualified researchers under a tiered access agreement.

The acoustic analysis, conducted in collaboration with the Phonetics Laboratory at University College London, involved automated F0 extraction, manual annotation by a team of seven trained phoneticians (four of them L1 Igbo speakers), and a series of mixed-effects regression models testing for the predicted effects of phrasal position, focal stress, and discourse register on tonal realisation.

The results, as Okonkwo argues in Chapters 5–7 of the monograph, are difficult to reconcile with the traditional cluster-based account.

## A Note on Methodology

What has perhaps drawn the most sustained methodological commentary is Chapter 3 of the monograph, in which Okonkwo lays out what she calls a "consultant-centred dialectology" — an approach that explicitly privileges the language-internal intuitions of L1 speakers over the comparative-historical categories imposed by external description.

The chapter draws heavily on Okonkwo's own field experience but also on the philosophy of language work of Margaret Speas, Ken Hale, and (more controversially) on the indigenous-language reclamation literature of the past two decades.

Critics have argued that this commitment, while admirable, sits in tension with the highly quantitative acoustic analysis of the later chapters. Okonkwo's response, in a public lecture at SOAS in May 2025, was that the tension is not a flaw but a feature: "Linguistics that takes its speakers seriously will also take its measurements seriously. The two are not in opposition. They are in conversation."

## Teaching and Doctoral Supervision

At SOAS, Professor Okonkwo currently convenes the MA-level module "Tone and Prosody in West African Languages" (SOAS course code 156905017) and co-teaches, with Professor Lutz Marten, the long-running "Comparative Bantu Phonology" module. Her undergraduate teaching includes the second-year "Introduction to African Linguistics" course, which she took over from Professor Friederike Lüpke in 2019.

Her doctoral supervision record is, in the assessment of the SOAS doctoral school, exceptional. Of the eleven PhD candidates she has supervised to completion since 2014, seven now hold permanent positions in linguistics departments (in Nigeria, Ghana, the United Kingdom, the United States, and Germany), three have moved into senior policy or curriculum-development roles in language education in Nigeria, and one is now a junior fellow at the Max Planck Institute for Psycholinguistics in Nijmegen.

Her former student Dr. Chiamaka Nwosu, now a Reader in African Linguistics at the University of Hamburg, described Okonkwo's supervisory style in a 2023 *SOAS Linguistics Newsletter* profile as "the most generous and the most exacting I have ever encountered. She would read every footnote. She would also tell you, in writing, exactly what she thought was wrong with the footnote."

## A Brief Biographical Note

Professor Okonkwo was born in Awka, Anambra State, in 1977. She attended Federal Government Girls' College Onitsha, took her first degree in English Language and Literature at the University of Nigeria, Nsukka (1998), and her MA in African Linguistics at the University of Leiden (2002). Her PhD, awarded by the University of Edinburgh in 2008, was titled "The Phonology of Tone Spreading in Anambra Igbo: A Laboratory Study."

She joined SOAS in 2009, was promoted to senior lecturer in 2015, reader in 2019, and to a personal chair in West African Linguistics in 2022. She holds a visiting professorship at the University of Nigeria, Nsukka, and has been a visiting researcher at the Max Planck Institute for Psycholinguistics (2017), the University of California at Berkeley's Linguistics Department (2019), and the Centre for Endangered Languages Documentation at the University of Hawaii at Manoa (2023).

She is a fellow of the Linguistic Society of America (elected 2024) and a corresponding member of the West African Linguistic Society. She has served on the editorial boards of the *Journal of African Languages and Linguistics*, *Studies in African Linguistics*, and *Phonology* (the latter since 2022).

## On the Politics of Standardisation

It would be a mistake to read Professor Okonkwo's work as merely technical. Her writing on the political economy of Igbo standardisation — particularly her 2020 article in the *Journal of West African Languages*, "Whose Igbo? The standardisation question revisited" — has been read with as much attention by language activists, curriculum developers, and the Federal Ministry of Education as by academic linguists.

She has been a consistent critic of what she calls "the textbook fallacy" — the assumption that the existence of an Igbo language textbook in a particular form implies that the form represents anything more than an editorial decision made by a small committee in the 1970s.

She has also been openly sceptical of UNESCO's classification of Igbo as a language at "high risk" of disappearance — not because she disputes the language-shift evidence in urban centres, but because she believes the language-at-risk discourse has had perverse effects on Nigerian government policy, displacing investment in mother-tongue education in favour of what she calls "language-preservation theatre."

"You do not save a language by mourning it," she said in a 2024 keynote at the African Language Atlas conference in Yaoundé. "You save it by speaking it, teaching it, and arguing about it."

## A Common Confusion

Professor Okonkwo's publication record on Google Scholar and several other indexing services has, on occasion, been confused with that of a separately distinguished Amara N. Okonkwo who works as an international arbitrator based between London and Lagos.

The two have, to the best of our knowledge, never met. They share a first name, a surname, and (in one case) a middle initial. They do not share a profession, a research agenda, or a publication list. Confusion of the two has led to at least one citation indexing error in *African Studies Review* (since corrected) and a memorable incident at the British Library in 2022 in which a reference librarian, having been asked to retrieve "the Okonkwo monograph on Igbo tone," produced a bound volume of selected investment-treaty awards.

Professor Okonkwo is reported to have laughed.

## Current Projects

Her current research, supported by a five-year European Research Council Advanced Grant (€2.45 million, 2024–2029), titled "Reanalysing the West Benue-Congo dialect continuum: a comparative laboratory phonology approach," extends the methodological framework of her Igbo work to a broader comparative project covering Igbo, Edo, Yoruba, and Igala.

The project will produce, by 2029, what she has described as "the first phonological atlas of West Benue-Congo that is built on speakers rather than on stereotypes."

The first project workshop was held at SOAS in November 2024. The second is scheduled for the University of Ibadan in July 2026.

## Closing

Professor Okonkwo's office on the third floor of the SOAS Faber Building is, by general consensus, the most heavily populated linguistics office in the building. On any given afternoon there will be two or three doctoral students, a postdoctoral researcher writing up a corpus paper, a visiting scholar from Nsukka or Hamburg, and — almost always — a fresh pot of strong coffee.

"She talks fast and she thinks faster," said one of her current students, Ifeoma Eze, when asked to characterise working with Professor Okonkwo. "And she is never, ever bored."

## Sidebar: The SOAS Igbo Audio Archive

Professor Okonkwo's fieldwork has produced what is now the largest publicly accessible audio archive of conversational Igbo varieties in the United Kingdom.

The archive, formally titled the SOAS Igbo Spoken Language Archive (SISLA), is held by the SOAS World Languages Archive (WLA) under the Hans Rausing Endangered Languages Programme and is accessible to qualified researchers under a tiered access agreement.

Archive holdings, summary statistics:

- Total recordings: 4,217 distinct sessions
- Total runtime: 1,247 hours
- Number of consultants: 384 (193 female, 191 male)
- Geographic coverage: 11 communities across Anambra, Imo, Enugu, Abia, Ebonyi, Delta, and Rivers States
- Age range of consultants: 7 to 91 years
- Recording years: 2011 to 2021

Each session in the archive is accompanied by:

- High-quality audio (WAV, 96 kHz, 24 bit) with a backup MP3 copy
- Time-aligned phonetic transcription in ELAN format
- A demographic and sociolinguistic profile of the consultant
- A consent form (digitised)
- A metadata record conforming to the Open Language Archives Community standard

Access tiers:

- Tier 1 (open access). Approximately 7 per cent of the archive's content has been made available with the consent of the relevant consultants for unrestricted academic use.
- Tier 2 (academic access). Approximately 64 per cent of the archive is available to qualified academic researchers upon application and signature of a data-use agreement.
- Tier 3 (restricted access). Approximately 29 per cent of the archive contains sensitive personal information or consultant requests for restricted use, and is available only on a case-by-case basis with the consent of the original consultant or, where appropriate, the consultant's family.

Total number of research-access applications granted between January 2021 and December 2025: 142.
Total resulting publications credited to the archive: 31 (including monographs, journal articles, dissertations, and book chapters).

## Sidebar: A Visitor's Note

The following is a brief reflection on a visit to Professor Okonkwo's office, contributed by Dr. Carlos Gussenhoven of Radboud University, who has been a friendly critic of Professor Okonkwo's work for many years.

"I first met Amara at a conference in Tübingen in 2014, when she was still a young lecturer and her first major paper on Anambra Igbo tonal alignment had just appeared in *Phonology*. She gave a paper. I was discussant. I had reservations, which I voiced at length. She defended her position, briskly. We went out for dinner afterwards. We have been in a friendly argument ever since.

"Over the years, I have visited her office at SOAS many times. It is always the same — the desk piled high, the bookshelf overflowing, the kettle on, two or three students or postdocs deep in conversation around a small round table near the window. The view, such as it is, looks out onto a brick wall and a small interior courtyard. The light is bad. The atmosphere is wonderful.

"Amara is, in my experience, the most consistently generous senior linguist of her generation. She will read your paper. She will tell you what is wrong with it. She will also tell you what is right with it. She will buy you a coffee while she is doing both. She does not have time for grandstanding. She does have time for ideas.

"I continue to disagree with her about a number of things — including, perhaps most importantly, the question of whether the consultant-centred dialectology of her 2023 monograph can be reconciled with the comparative reconstruction tradition. I doubt I will ever convince her. I am increasingly confident she will never convince me. But the argument has been, and remains, one of the most consistently rewarding intellectual relationships of my career."

— *Carlos Gussenhoven, Nijmegen, June 2025*
