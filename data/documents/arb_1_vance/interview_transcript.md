# "The Tribunal's Job Is to Decide" — A Conversation with Prof. Eleanor Vance

*The following is an edited transcript of a conversation conducted in February 2024 for the* Global Arbitration Review *podcast,* The Hearing. *The interviewer is GAR senior reporter Lila Chen.*

---

**Chen:** Professor Vance, thank you for joining us. You've had an unusual career path — full-time academic for nearly two decades, and now one of the busier sole arbitrators on the New York scene. Why the pivot?

**Vance:** Thank you for having me. I'd push back gently on the word "pivot" — I started taking arbitral appointments while I was still teaching, probably around 2008 or so. By the mid-2010s I was sitting on maybe six or seven matters a year alongside teaching, and at some point the calendar just had to give. I took emerita status at Columbia in 2022 and now I'm full-time in chambers.

**Chen:** You write notoriously long awards. Is that the academic in you?

**Vance:** Partly. But it's also a deliberate choice. If a party is going to challenge an award at the seat — and in some of the jurisdictions I sit in, that's a near-certainty — the tribunal owes the parties and the supervisory court a reasoned record. I'd rather write 180 pages than have a sloppy 40-page award vacated on a *Mitsubishi* or manifest-disregard theory.

**Chen:** Speaking of which — your 2018 *Columbia Law Review* piece on manifest disregard. Has the doctrine moved at all since then?

**Vance:** Not in any direction I find coherent. The Second and Ninth Circuits still treat it as alive in some attenuated form; the Fifth has flatly rejected it post-*Hall Street*. I think we're a decade away from the Supreme Court taking it up again, if ever.

**Chen:** Let's talk practice mechanics. You're known for being expensive. Is that fair?

**Vance:** *(laughs)* I charge what the market charges for a chair with my profile. My current rate is twelve hundred dollars an hour, with the usual ICC- and AAA-style caps when those apply. I'm told that's at the lower end for U.S.-based arbitrators with international caseloads. I don't take retainers, and I don't take cancellation fees for hearings that move by more than thirty days' notice.

**Chen:** Any recent matters you can talk about in general terms?

**Vance:** Only what's already on the public docket. I chaired an ICC matter — Meridian Capital — that involved a post-acquisition indemnity dispute over an oil and gas SPA. I issued a partial award on liability in late 2021. The case is illustrative of a trend I've been seeing: private equity buyers using arbitration as a forum for what are essentially accounting disputes. I'm not sure that's the best use of the institution, but it's where the work is.

**Chen:** What do you say to younger lawyers who want to build an arbitrator practice?

**Vance:** Read widely. Learn at least one other language well enough to read pleadings — I do my own reading in French and Italian when matters allow it, and it changes how you understand a record. And be patient. Most arbitrators don't get their first chair appointment until they're well into their forties.

**Chen:** Prof. Vance, thank you.

**Vance:** My pleasure.
