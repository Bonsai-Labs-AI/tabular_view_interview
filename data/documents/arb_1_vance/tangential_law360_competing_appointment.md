## Argentine Mining Giant Taps Veteran Swiss Arbitrator To Chair $3.4B ICC Treaty Dispute

**By Sasha Wendelin** | June 28, 2023, 4:12 PM EDT

The ICC International Court of Arbitration has confirmed the constitution of a three-member tribunal to hear a $3.4 billion treaty-based dispute between Argentine state-owned mining company Yacimientos Mineros Federales SA ("YMF") and a Toronto-listed claimant, North Andes Lithium Corp., over the 2022 cancellation of two lithium-brine concessions in Salta Province, three sources with direct knowledge of the proceeding told Law360 on Wednesday.

The tribunal will be chaired by **Dr. Annika Beilenberg-Roth**, a Zurich-based independent arbitrator and former partner at Lalive, whose appointment was made by the ICC Court after the party-appointed co-arbitrators failed to agree on a chair candidate within the ninety-day window set by the procedural calendar. The party-appointed arbitrators are **Professor Hernán Saldaña-Cifuentes** (nominated by the claimant) of the Universidad Torcuato Di Tella in Buenos Aires, and **Dr. Patrick O'Halloran SC** (nominated by the respondent), a Dublin-based barrister and former Irish Attorney General.

### Background

The dispute arises from the November 2022 cancellation by the Salta provincial government, allegedly at the direction of the Argentine federal government, of two mining concessions held by North Andes Lithium Corp.'s wholly owned Argentine subsidiary, Salar Norte Litio SA. The concessions covered approximately 47,000 hectares in the Salar de Olaroz–Cauchari basin and were originally granted in 2017 under the Argentina–Canada bilateral investment treaty.

North Andes alleges that the cancellation was effected without due process, in breach of the BIT's fair-and-equitable-treatment standard, and in furtherance of an effective expropriation of approximately $2.8 billion in committed development capital. The company also alleges that the cancellation was preceded by a sustained pattern of harassment, including the cancellation of customs clearances, the obstruction of equipment imports, and the targeted application of provincial tax assessments later struck down by the Argentine federal courts.

The Argentine state denies the substantive allegations and raises a jurisdictional objection grounded in the BIT's denial-of-benefits clause, asserting that North Andes Lithium Corp. is a shell entity controlled by Chinese principals through a Toronto holding-company structure and is therefore not entitled to BIT protection.

### Counsel

The claimant is represented by a team from Three Crowns LLP led by partners **Camille Forrester-DuBois** and **Jeremiah Olabisi-Penrose**, with associate counsel **Sayed-Reza Mostafavi-Hardcastle**. The respondent is represented by Foley Hoag LLP, led by partners **Karoline Brüning-Whetstone** and **Mauricio Sandoval-Eriksson**, with counsel from the Argentine Procuración del Tesoro de la Nación.

Counsel for both parties declined to comment when contacted by Law360.

### The Tribunal

Dr. Beilenberg-Roth, who chairs the tribunal, is widely regarded as one of the most active continental arbitrators of her generation in Latin American investment-treaty matters. She sat as co-arbitrator in *Compañía Energética del Cono Sur v. Bolivia* (ICSID Case No. ARB/19/14, award rendered 2022) and is currently presiding over a $1.1 billion gas-transport dispute administered by the ICC and seated in Geneva. She was admitted in Zurich in 1995 and holds a doctorate in international law from the University of Geneva.

Professor Saldaña-Cifuentes, the claimant's nominee, is a professor of international economic law at the Universidad Torcuato Di Tella and a former Argentine Minister of Justice and Human Rights (briefly, in 2015). His scholarship is concerned principally with the jurisdictional architecture of investment treaty arbitration and with the relationship between international investment law and domestic public-law institutions. He has sat in approximately fifteen prior investment-treaty matters, most often as a party-appointed arbitrator for claimants.

Dr. O'Halloran SC, the respondent's nominee, was Attorney General of Ireland from 2014 to 2016 and has served as counsel and arbitrator in a range of commercial and public-international-law matters since returning to private practice. He is a member of Brick Court Chambers' international arbitration panel and is widely regarded as one of the leading public-international-law practitioners in the Irish bar.

According to two of the sources, the ICC Court considered a number of candidates for chair before settling on Dr. Beilenberg-Roth. Arbitrators understood to have been previously considered but not selected include Sir Quentin Marlborough-Stoddart KC of Essex Court Chambers, Maître Cécile Vannier-Lafont of Vannier Lafont Avocats in Paris, Prof. Eleanor Vance of Columbia Law School (now in independent practice), Justice Kgomotso Setlhare (Ret.) of the South African Supreme Court of Appeal, and Prof. Yoshiro Tatsumi-Aoki of Keio University.

### Seat, Procedural Calendar, and Substantive Law

The tribunal will sit in Paris, with the ICC's hearing facilities at the Court of Arbitration's headquarters at 33-43 Avenue du Président Wilson available for procedural conferences and an evidentiary hearing scheduled provisionally for the autumn of 2024. The substantive law of the dispute is principally the Argentina–Canada BIT, together with applicable rules of customary international law.

The procedural calendar set out in Procedural Order No. 1, issued on June 19, contemplates the following principal milestones:

- Statement of Claim: October 31, 2023
- Statement of Defense (with any jurisdictional objections): March 28, 2024
- Reply on Merits / Counter-Memorial on Jurisdiction: July 30, 2024
- Rejoinder on Merits / Reply on Jurisdiction: October 15, 2024
- Evidentiary Hearing: November 18–29, 2024
- Post-Hearing Briefs: February 14, 2025

A final award is not expected before the third quarter of 2025.

### Argentina's Recent Investment Treaty Caseload

The new YMF / North Andes matter is the eleventh investment-treaty proceeding pending against the Republic of Argentina or against an Argentine sub-sovereign entity as of mid-2023, and the third arising from a mining-or-mineral-rights dispute in northern Argentina. It follows the December 2022 commencement of *Cariboo Northern Resources v. Argentina* (ICSID Case No. ARB/22/47, also concerning lithium-brine concessions) and the February 2023 commencement of *Andinor Copper SA v. Argentina* (ICSID Case No. ARB/23/4, concerning copper concessions in Catamarca Province).

The Procuración del Tesoro estimates the total amount in dispute across pending investment-treaty proceedings against Argentina at approximately $11.8 billion as of May 2023, against approximately $7.4 billion as of the same date in 2022. The substantial year-over-year increase is attributable principally to the lithium and copper claims.

### Funding

North Andes Lithium Corp. confirmed in a February 2023 disclosure to Canadian securities regulators that the claimant's costs in connection with the proceeding are being financed under a non-recourse arbitration finance facility provided by a major third-party funder, the identity of which has not been publicly disclosed. The terms of the facility, including the funder's expected return on a successful outcome, are likewise undisclosed; market participants told Law360 that the facility is understood to be in the range of $14 million to $18 million.

### Outlook

Practitioners told Law360 that the proceeding is likely to be closely watched for several reasons. First, the jurisdictional objection — grounded in alleged Chinese beneficial ownership of a Canadian claimant — squarely presents a denial-of-benefits question that has been the subject of considerable doctrinal interest but limited reasoned authority. Second, the case is among the first major mining-sector investment-treaty proceedings to arise from the post-2020 expansion of lithium-brine concessioning across the Andean triangle (Argentina, Bolivia, Chile). And third, the substantial third-party funding involvement is likely to occasion procedural skirmishing over disclosure obligations under the ICC's 2021 rules on funder identification.

"This is a case that is going to make law in three or four different directions," one Buenos Aires-based observer told Law360. "There's a real possibility that the award, when it issues, will be cited in casebooks for a generation."

A spokesperson for North Andes Lithium Corp. confirmed the constitution of the tribunal in a statement issued late Wednesday but otherwise declined to comment on the substance of the proceeding. The Argentine Procuración del Tesoro did not respond to a request for comment.

### Sidebar: The Argentine Procuración del Tesoro's Mining-Sector Defense Posture

The Argentine Procuración del Tesoro de la Nación — the office of the federal solicitor general — has, since approximately 2019, adopted a distinctively aggressive jurisdictional defense posture in mining-sector investment-treaty proceedings. Procurador del Tesoro **Dr. Hipólito Casarrubias-Mendizábal**, in office since the change of government in December 2023, has publicly stated that the denial-of-benefits jurisprudence developed by Argentine federal prosecutors in *Cariboo Northern Resources* and earlier matters represents what he called, in a January 2024 address at the Buenos Aires Bar Association, "the most important strategic asset in the defense of the Argentine fisc against speculative mining claims."

The denial-of-benefits jurisdictional theory — under which a respondent state argues that a claimant nominally incorporated in a BIT-protected jurisdiction is in substance owned or controlled by nationals of a non-protected third state — has been a feature of investment-treaty practice for more than two decades. Recent Argentine practice has, however, pressed the theory in directions that the broader investment-treaty bar regards as novel. In particular, the Procuración has, in the *Cariboo Northern* matter, advanced an argument that beneficial ownership for denial-of-benefits purposes should be assessed by reference to the underlying funding source rather than to the holder of the equity interest. This argument, which has not yet been adjudicated, would, if accepted, substantially expand the practical reach of the denial-of-benefits clause.

The cancellation of the Salar Norte Litio SA concessions in November 2022 was the first in a series of provincial-level mining concession cancellations across the Andean lithium triangle (Argentina, Bolivia, Chile) over the last eighteen months. ### Sidebar: The "Strategic Minerals" Provincial Legislation

A significant background development for the YMF / North Andes proceeding is the November 2021 passage by the Salta provincial legislature of Provincial Law No. 8624, the "Ley de Minerales Estratégicos," which designates lithium and several other minerals as "strategic" for provincial-development purposes and authorizes the provincial government to renegotiate or cancel existing mining concessions in order to "align them with provincial-development priorities."

The constitutionality of the Strategic Minerals Law has been the subject of substantial legal commentary, and at least three federal-court actions have been filed against the law in Argentine federal court. The Procuración del Tesoro takes the position that the Strategic Minerals Law represents a lawful exercise of provincial regulatory authority, consistent with both the Argentine constitutional architecture and with applicable international-law standards.

### Sidebar: Third-Party Funding Disclosure Issues

The substantial third-party funding presence in the YMF / North Andes matter — and in mining-sector investment-treaty proceedings generally — is expected to give rise to procedural skirmishing over the scope of disclosure obligations under the ICC's 2021 rules on funder identification. The ICC's 2021 rules require disclosure of the existence of third-party funding and of the identity of the funder, but the precise scope of the disclosure obligation — including in particular whether it extends to the funder's commercial terms, to the funder's beneficial owners, and to the existence of any conflicts between the funder and the arbitrators — remains the subject of considerable practical uncertainty.

A separate but related question concerns the use of third-party funding to finance respondent defense costs in investment-treaty proceedings. The Argentine Procuración del Tesoro has, to the author's knowledge, not disclosed any third-party funding of its defense costs in the YMF / North Andes matter or in any other pending investment-treaty proceeding.

### Sidebar: A Note on the Chair Selection Process

ICC practice in chair selection — when the party-appointed co-arbitrators fail to agree — calls for the ICC Court to consult its internal databases and external consultations to identify a list of candidates with the relevant subject-matter expertise, linguistic capacity, and availability. The list of candidates publicly understood to have been considered for the YMF / North Andes chairmanship reflects the ICC Secretariat's typical practice of working with a candidate pool of five to eight senior arbitrators with substantial Latin American investment-treaty experience.

The final selection of Dr. Beilenberg-Roth is consistent with what practitioners describe as an ICC trend toward selecting continental European arbitrators with civil-law backgrounds for chair roles in Latin American treaty matters, on the theory that civil-law-trained arbitrators are better positioned to navigate the substantive and procedural issues that arise in proceedings governed by treaty obligations and customary international law.

### Sidebar: A Note on the Argentine BIT Network

The Argentina–Canada BIT, on which North Andes's claim is grounded, is one of approximately fifty bilateral investment treaties to which Argentina is a party. The treaty was signed on November 5, 1991, and entered into force on April 29, 1993. Article 12 of the treaty contains a denial-of-benefits clause that is broadly typical of the Canadian model BIT of the early 1990s.

The Argentine BIT network has historically generated more investment-treaty arbitration than the network of any other state. As of December 2022, Argentina had been a respondent in 64 publicly known ICSID proceedings and an additional 19 non-ICSID investment-treaty proceedings (under UNCITRAL, ICC, or SCC rules), the vast majority of which arose under bilateral investment treaties. The cumulative claim value of pending proceedings against Argentina is estimated, as noted above, at approximately $11.8 billion as of May 2023.

Argentine practice in the defense of investment-treaty claims has evolved substantially over the past two decades. Following the 2001–2002 economic crisis and the wave of claims that followed it, Argentina developed substantial in-house and contracted-counsel capacity to defend major investment-treaty proceedings. The current configuration — under which the Procuración del Tesoro retains overall responsibility for defense strategy and delegates particular matters to specialised external counsel — was established in approximately 2015.

### Sidebar: The Brazilian Energy Sector and Investment-Treaty Considerations

Although the present matter concerns Argentina, it is worth noting that Brazil — the home jurisdiction of the respondent in the other ICC matter discussed above involving Professor Vance — is not a party to any ICSID Convention investment-treaty network. Brazil has, however, concluded a number of "Cooperation and Facilitation Investment Agreements" (CFIAs) over the past several years that include dispute-resolution mechanisms, though these mechanisms are state-to-state rather than investor-state.

The absence of investor-state arbitration in the Brazilian BIT network means that investment-treaty disputes involving Brazilian state-owned enterprises are typically resolved through commercial arbitration under the institutional rules of bodies such as the ICC, rather than through investment-treaty arbitration under the ICSID or UNCITRAL rules.

### Sidebar: Reflections on the Mining-Sector Arbitration Wave

Practitioners on both the claimant and respondent sides told Law360 that the current wave of mining-sector investment-treaty arbitration is likely to continue for at least three to five years. The post-2020 expansion of provincial-level concession granting in Latin America, combined with the post-2022 political shifts in several of the major mining jurisdictions, has produced a substantial pipeline of claims that are now beginning to materialise as formal proceedings.

"What we are seeing is a generational shift in the political economy of mining in the Andes," one Buenos Aires-based mining-law specialist told Law360. "The contracts that were written in the 2010s were written in a market in which lithium was a niche industrial mineral. Lithium is now a geopolitically strategic resource, and the political economy of the contracts has caught up with that reality."

The result, the specialist continued, is a wave of disputes that will likely keep the ICC, ICSID, and UNCITRAL caseloads in Latin American investment treaty matters elevated for the foreseeable future, with significant implications for the broader investment-treaty bar and for the major institutional arbitration providers.

### Sidebar: A Note on Provincial-Level Treaty Implementation in Argentina

A recurring legal-technical question in Argentine investment-treaty proceedings concerns the relationship between federal-level treaty obligations and provincial-level regulatory actions. Argentina is a federal state, and the country's twenty-three provinces (together with the Autonomous City of Buenos Aires) retain substantial regulatory authority over natural resources within their respective territories. The Argentine federal constitution explicitly recognises provincial ownership of natural resources within provincial territory (Article 124), and provincial governments routinely exercise concession-granting, taxation, and regulatory authority in the mining and hydrocarbons sectors.

The interaction between this federal-provincial division of authority and Argentina's international-law obligations under its BIT network has been the subject of considerable arbitral and academic analysis. The settled position — repeatedly affirmed in ICSID arbitral practice — is that the Argentine Republic remains internationally responsible for provincial-level conduct that breaches the country's treaty obligations, regardless of the domestic constitutional allocation of authority between the federal and provincial levels.

The Argentine Procuración del Tesoro has, however, advanced in a number of recent matters the position that the practical reality of federal-provincial relations should inform the analysis of treaty-compatibility questions, particularly where the underlying claim concerns alleged regulatory conduct by a provincial government rather than by the federal government itself. This position has not been broadly adopted by arbitral tribunals, but it remains a feature of Argentine defense practice and is expected to be advanced in the YMF / North Andes proceeding.

### Sidebar: A Note on Chinese Investment in Argentine Mining

The respondent's denial-of-benefits jurisdictional objection — grounded in the allegation that North Andes Lithium Corp. is in substance owned or controlled by Chinese principals through a Toronto holding-company structure — raises a question of broader interest concerning the role of Chinese investment in the Argentine mining sector.

Chinese state-owned and state-affiliated investors have, over the past decade, become significant participants in the Argentine lithium and copper sectors. The China-Argentina bilateral relationship in the mining sector is, however, governed by a different legal architecture than the Argentina-Canada relationship that grounds the present proceeding. There is no investment-treaty arbitration mechanism between China and Argentina; Chinese investors who wish to access investment-treaty arbitration in respect of Argentine investments must therefore structure their holdings through a third jurisdiction, typically Canada or a Western European state, that is a party to a BIT with Argentina.

This structuring practice — sometimes referred to as "treaty shopping," though the term is not always precisely apt — is widely recognised as a feature of contemporary investment-treaty practice. The denial-of-benefits clause, present in many modern BITs and BIT-equivalent instruments, is the principal jurisdictional mechanism through which respondent states may challenge the entitlement of structured claimants to treaty protection.

The application of the denial-of-benefits clause to structures involving Chinese underlying ownership has been a subject of particular arbitral attention in recent years, with several recent awards reaching nuanced — and not always consistent — conclusions about the scope of the clause's application. The YMF / North Andes tribunal will therefore have the opportunity to contribute to the developing body of arbitral law on this question, and practitioners told Law360 that they will be watching the tribunal's eventual analysis closely.

### Sidebar: A Brief Note on the Salar de Olaroz–Cauchari Basin

The disputed concessions cover approximately 47,000 hectares in the Salar de Olaroz–Cauchari basin, one of the principal lithium-brine deposits in the Andean lithium triangle. The basin straddles the border between Salta and Jujuy provinces in northern Argentina and is part of the broader Puna de Atacama plateau, which extends across portions of northern Argentina, southwestern Bolivia, and northern Chile.

The basin's lithium-bearing brines are extracted through a process of solar evaporation, in which brine is pumped from underground aquifers into a series of evaporation ponds and concentrated over a period of approximately twelve to eighteen months to produce lithium-rich concentrates suitable for downstream processing. The process is comparatively low-cost relative to hard-rock lithium extraction (the principal alternative production method, used primarily in Australia and in parts of Africa), and lithium produced from Andean brines accordingly enjoys a substantial cost advantage in the global lithium market.

The Salar de Olaroz–Cauchari basin has, since the late 2010s, been the subject of substantial international investment, principally from Australian, Canadian, Chinese, and Japanese-Korean operators. The basin currently hosts six operating or near-operating lithium-brine projects, with combined annual production capacity in the range of 90,000–110,000 tonnes lithium carbonate equivalent (LCE) as of mid-2023.
