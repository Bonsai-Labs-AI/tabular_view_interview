# Society of Architectural Historians — New England Chapter

## Eleanor Vance and the Quiet Modernism of Postwar Boston Commercial Architecture

*A Biographical Essay*

**By Henrik Lobmeyer, FAIA** | SAH/NEC Bulletin, Vol. 41, No. 2 (Summer 2017), pp. 14–37

---

The career of Eleanor Vance (1922–2004) occupies an unusual and somewhat unfairly neglected place in the history of postwar New England commercial architecture. Trained at Yale during the years when Eugene Nalle and Louis Kahn were reshaping the school's pedagogy, she returned to Boston in 1951 and, over the next four decades, produced a body of work — chiefly office buildings, banks, and corporate headquarters between Route 128 and the Merrimack Valley — that synthesized Bauhaus structural rationalism with a distinctively understated New England material sensibility. She was, in the words of the late Robert Campbell, "the architect who built half of Boston's middle floors and got none of Boston's credit."

This essay attempts to redress that imbalance, drawing on the Vance papers recently donated to the Frances Loeb Library at Harvard's Graduate School of Design and on a series of oral histories conducted by the author and Margaret Henderson Floyd between 1998 and 2003.

### I. Origins, 1922–1944

Eleanor Margaret Vance was born on April 17, 1922, in Brookline, Mass., the only child of Dr. Phillip Vance, a Harvard-trained internist, and the former Anna Lindstrom, a violinist with the Boston Pops. Her childhood was marked by frequent travel: her mother's family had business interests in Stockholm, and the Vances spent summers and the occasional academic year in Sweden between 1928 and 1937. It was during these visits — particularly a 1934 visit to the Stockholm Exhibition pavilions designed by Gunnar Asplund — that Vance later said she "first understood that a building could be a piece of moral argument."

She enrolled at Smith College in 1939, intending to study art history, but transferred to the Cambridge School of Architecture and Landscape Architecture after two semesters. When the Cambridge School closed in 1942 — its student body absorbed by the Harvard Graduate School of Design under Walter Gropius — Vance opted instead to enroll at the Yale School of Architecture, then in a period of considerable instability under the deanship of Everett Meeks.

### II. Yale, 1942–1947

The Yale of Vance's student years was a place of unresolved tension between Beaux-Arts orthodoxy and an incipient European modernism. Her surviving studio drawings from 1943 and 1944 — a hypothetical post office for Litchfield, Conn., and a community library for a Vermont mill town — already show a preference for clear structural expression and an aversion to applied ornament. Her thesis project, a regional administrative building for the Tennessee Valley Authority, won the Charles O. Matcham Prize in 1947.

She completed her professional degree in 1947 and spent the following three years in the office of Eero Saarinen and Associates in Bloomfield Hills, Mich., where she worked on the General Motors Technical Center in Warren and on early studies for the John Deere headquarters in Moline, Ill. Her name appears on the drawing sheets for the Technical Center's research administration building (sheets A-204 through A-211) and on several presentation perspectives for which she was, in Saarinen's office shorthand, "the woman who could draw a curtain wall."

### III. Boston Years: Partnership with Heath and Forbes, 1951–1962

Vance returned to Boston in 1951 and joined the firm of Heath and Forbes, then a midsize commercial practice with offices in the Park Square Building. She was made an associate in 1954 and a full partner — only the third woman to hold full partnership in a Boston architectural firm — in 1958. The firm reorganized as Heath, Forbes & Vance in 1959.

The work of this period was almost entirely commercial: branch banks for Shawmut and the Industrial National Bank of Rhode Island, small office buildings along Route 128, and the headquarters of the New England Tool and Die Company in Lawrence (1957), generally considered her first mature work. The Tool and Die building — a three-story concrete-frame structure with brick infill panels and a deeply recessed entry — was published in *Progressive Architecture* in February 1958 and won an AIA New England Honor Award the following year.

A characteristic Vance project of this period combined:

- A clearly expressed structural frame, usually in cast-in-place concrete or, after about 1960, in welded steel
- Brick infill panels in a single warm earth tone — she was partial to the Stiles & Hart "Plymouth red" — laid in a Flemish bond with deeply raked joints
- Generous, but not gratuitous, glazing, almost always set deep within the structural bay and shaded by a projecting concrete or steel hood
- Site planning that subordinated the building to the existing topography, particularly when the site sloped

She was famously hostile to curtain-wall construction, which she regarded as a misapplication of the Miesian vocabulary to the wrong climate. "Glass is a wonderful material for Chicago," she told *Architectural Forum* in 1962. "It is a foolish material for Worcester."

### IV. The Independent Practice, 1962–1989

Vance left Heath, Forbes & Vance in 1962 over what her partners described as "irreconcilable differences regarding the proper response to speculative office work." She opened a small independent practice at 73 Newbury Street the following year, taking with her three associates and the firm's longtime structural consultant, Lawrence Diestel.

The work of the independent practice was more varied and, in some respects, more interesting than the work she had produced at Heath, Forbes & Vance. Major commissions of the period included:

- **Boston Five Cents Savings Bank, Watertown Branch (1964).** A small, almost domestically scaled banking hall with a hipped roof of standing-seam copper and load-bearing brick walls. Demolished 2008.
- **Wellesley Office Park, Buildings 2 and 3 (1968).** Two three-story speculative office buildings in pre-cast concrete with a continuous projecting eave at each floor line. Building 2 was substantially remodeled in 2003; Building 3 remains largely intact.
- **State Mutual Life Assurance Company Headquarters, Worcester (1972, with Donald Reay).** Vance's largest single commission, a ten-story tower with a structurally expressed concrete frame and bronze-anodized aluminum spandrels.
- **Northeastern Federal Savings, Quincy Center (1976).** A widely published example of urban-infill commercial work; received the Boston Society of Architects Harleston Parker Medal in 1978.
- **Cornell University, Sage Hall Renovation, Phase II (1981).** Her only significant commission outside New England.

She was elected a Fellow of the American Institute of Architects in 1974 and served on the Yale School of Architecture's visiting committee from 1976 to 1984.

### V. Teaching

Although primarily a practitioner, Vance taught intermittently throughout her career. She was a critic in the Yale design studios in the spring terms of 1965, 1968, and 1971, and she held the Pietro Belluschi visiting chair at MIT in the academic year 1979–1980. Her studio that year — which assigned each student a corner site in a small New England mill town and required the design of a three-story commercial building — became something of a Cambridge legend, in part because of her insistence that students dimension their drawings in feet and inches rather than meters.

Her papers contain an extensive correspondence with the historian Vincent Scully, whom she had known since her student years at Yale. The correspondence — collegial but frequently contentious — turns repeatedly on the proper relation of structure to expression in commercial work, and on what Vance called Scully's "regrettable fondness for the gestural."

### VI. Late Work and Retirement

By the early 1980s the speculative office market that had sustained Vance's practice was shifting decisively toward developer-driven design-build delivery and toward an aesthetic — variously called postmodern, contextual, or simply "Pei-after-Pei" — that Vance found uncongenial. Her last significant commission, the Five Mile River Office Park in Darien, Conn., was completed in 1986 and represented, by her own account, a deliberate concession to the new market. She closed the office at the end of 1989 and retired to a house she had designed for herself in Cornish, N.H.

In retirement she wrote prolifically. A book on the Saarinen office, *Drawing for Eero*, was published by Yale University Press in 1996. A second manuscript, *On the Discipline of the Middle Floor*, was unfinished at her death and remains in the Loeb Library archive.

### VII. Death and Legacy

Eleanor Vance died at her home in Cornish on August 21, 2004, at the age of 82. She was survived by a niece, Karen Vance Borkowski of Newton, and by two grandnephews. Her papers and architectural drawings were donated to the Frances Loeb Library in 2006.

A retrospective exhibition, *Quiet Frames: The Architecture of Eleanor Vance, 1951–1989*, was mounted at the Boston Society of Architects' BSA Space in the spring of 2015. The exhibition catalogue, edited by the author of this essay, is available from the SAH/NEC bookstore.

It remains an open question whether Vance's body of work will, over time, receive the critical reassessment that the author believes it deserves. The architecture she produced was rarely spectacular and almost never photogenic. It was, however, almost always intelligent, durable, and humane — virtues that the discipline has, at various points in the last three decades, found it convenient to forget.

### Appendix C — Studio Teaching Records, MIT 1979–1980

The Pietro Belluschi visiting studio assigned each student a corner site in one of seven New England mill towns: Lewiston, Maine; Manchester, N.H.; Lawrence, Mass.; Lowell, Mass.; Pawtucket, R.I.; Willimantic, Conn.; and North Adams, Mass. The studio brief required each student to design a three-story commercial building of approximately 18,000 gross square feet, with a ground-floor retail tenancy, two floors of office space, and a clearly resolved relationship to the existing urban fabric.

Vance famously refused to permit the use of architectural metric units in the studio, insisting that students dimension all drawings in feet and inches "because that is what the buildings are going to be built in." She also famously refused to permit the use of presentation typography in any face other than Univers, on the grounds that "Univers is the only typeface that does not lie about its width."

The final review, which was conducted on May 14, 1980, was attended by, among others, Vincent Scully, Tim Vreeland, Eleanor Munro, and the structural engineer William LeMessurier. A photographic record of the final review is preserved in the MIT Museum.

### Appendix E — A Note on Source Material

The Vance papers at the Frances Loeb Library at the Harvard Graduate School of Design were donated to the library in 2006 by the architect's niece, Ms. Karen Vance Borkowski, on behalf of the Vance estate. The papers comprise approximately 47 linear feet of material, including:

- 1,200+ original drawings (pencil, ink, and graphite on tracing paper), arranged by project
- 14 boxes of correspondence (1947–2003), arranged chronologically
- 6 boxes of office records of Heath, Forbes & Vance (1955–1962)
- 3 boxes of office records of the independent practice (1962–1989)
- Approximately 800 photographic prints and negatives documenting completed projects
- Personal teaching files from the MIT Belluschi studio (1979–1980) and the Yale visiting critic appointments (1965, 1968, 1971)
- A complete run of project models from the independent practice (kept in the Loeb's model storage)

The papers are open to researchers under standard Loeb Library access protocols. A complete finding aid was prepared in 2008 by archivist Mr. Sergei Galperin-Holcomb and is available on the Loeb Library website.

The Heath, Forbes & Vance records held at the Boston Public Library (BPL/MS/Vance/01–04) comprise an additional 22 linear feet of material drawn from the firm's office at the Park Square Building. The records were transferred to the BPL in 1991 following the firm's dissolution and are arranged by project number.

A smaller and more personal collection of material — including the architect's personal library, her drafting tools, and approximately 200 photographic prints documenting her travels in Sweden and northern Italy — was retained by Ms. Borkowski and is held privately at the Borkowski residence in Newton.

### Appendix G — A Note on Photography

Photographic documentation of Vance's built work was, by her own design, comparatively sparse during the period of the work's completion. The architect resisted invitations from architectural photographers throughout her career, on the explicit ground that "the photograph is not the building." Such professional photographs as exist of her completed work were taken either (a) shortly before the building's demolition, by the local architectural-history community, or (b) by her own office for marketing purposes, generally in a documentary rather than a fine-art style.

The most substantial existing photographic documentation of the State Mutual Worcester building is a 1974 portfolio by Lawrence S. Williams of Wayne, Pa., commissioned by State Mutual for its 1975 annual report. The portfolio includes 47 images and is held at the Worcester Historical Museum.

For Vance's domestic-scaled commercial work — the Watertown branch of the Boston Five and the Quincy Center branch of Northeastern Federal — the principal extant photographic record is the documentary photography by Robert Damora (1912–2009) and his wife, Sirkka Moss Damora (1924–2010), commissioned by the firms for their own marketing purposes. The Damora photographs are held in the Damora Archive at the Frances Loeb Library and are accessible to researchers under standard Loeb access protocols.

A modest number of architectural photographs of Vance's work were also taken by the photographer Nicholas Nixon over a series of visits to Vance's completed projects in 1988 and 1989. These photographs were exhibited as part of the 2015 *Quiet Frames* retrospective and are reproduced in the exhibition catalogue.

### Appendix H — Posthumous Designations and Memorials

The Boston Society of Architects established the Eleanor Vance Award for Excellence in Commercial Architecture in 2007, three years after the architect's death. The award is given annually to a New England-based architectural project of commercial program that, in the words of the BSA award citation, "exhibits the discipline of structural expression, the integrity of material selection, and the modesty of public manner that characterised the work of Eleanor Vance throughout her career." Past recipients of the Vance Award include:

- 2007: Maryann Thompson Architects, for the Cambridge Friends Meeting House addition (Cambridge, Mass.)
- 2008: Leers Weinzapfel Associates, for the University of Massachusetts Amherst Studio Arts Building
- 2009: William Rawn Associates, for the Cambridge Public Library
- 2010: Bruner/Cott & Associates, for the Mass MoCA Building 6 renovation
- 2011: deferred
- 2012: Touloukian Touloukian, for the Boston Wharf Road garage retrofit
- 2013: Anmahian Winton Architects, for the Manchester Athletic Club expansion
- 2014: Höweler + Yoon Architecture, for the Princeton University Eno Hall renovation
- 2015: Studio Luz Architects, for the Goodwin Procter office adaptive-reuse project, Boston
- 2016: Höweler + Yoon Architecture, for the MIT Hayden Library renovation, Phase I
- 2017: Schwartz/Silver Architects, for the Boston Athenæum interior renovation

The award is administered by the BSA in cooperation with the Vance family trust, which provides a modest financial component (currently $7,500) for the award recipient.

In addition to the BSA award, the Yale School of Architecture in 2011 established the Eleanor Vance Visiting Critic chair, which brings a senior practising architect with a substantial commercial-architecture practice to the school for a one-semester teaching residency every two years. The chair has been held by, among others, James Polshek (2011–2012), Marion Weiss (2013–2014), David Brody (2015–2016), and Maryann Thompson (2017–2018).

### Appendix I — Reflections from Former Colleagues

The author conducted brief telephone interviews with a number of Vance's former colleagues and students in the preparation of this essay. Selected reflections follow.

**Helena Forbes-Lindquist** (former associate at Heath, Forbes & Vance; daughter of Heath, Forbes partner Albert Forbes): "Eleanor was the smartest person in the office. Everyone knew it. The work she did at Heath, Forbes was, frankly, work that my father and his partners could not have done themselves. They knew it too, and they made her a partner, which for that time and place was an extraordinary thing. The reason she eventually left was not that the partnership did not work. The reason she left was that the projects had started to bore her, and she was not a person who could pretend to be interested in something that did not interest her."

**Donald Reay** (associate architect on the State Mutual Worcester project, 1925–2008, interview conducted in 2002): "Eleanor did not want to work in a hierarchical office and she did not want to be the kind of architect who had a hierarchical office. So she ran the practice the way she ran it, which meant that she worked on every project herself, every day, and there was no project that left the office that she had not personally drawn at the working-drawing scale. I do not know how she did it. I can tell you that no one else in the profession does it that way now, and the profession is poorer for that."

**Sebastian Whitcombe-Burdette** (former student at MIT, 1979–1980; now Professor of Architecture at the University of Virginia): "Eleanor's studio was the hardest studio I ever took. She would come into the studio at 8:30 in the morning, look at my drawings, and tell me — without any cruelty in it, just as a matter of fact — that I had wasted three weeks of my life on a section that did not resolve the problem the brief had set. I would then go back and produce a section that did resolve the problem. The studio was, in retrospect, the single best educational experience of my professional formation."

**Karen Vance Borkowski** (niece): "Aunt Eleanor was the only person in our extended family who never spoke about what she did at work. She spoke about her travels in Sweden, and she spoke about the donkey, and she spoke about her garden, but she never spoke about her buildings. We grew up thinking she was a kind of office worker — we did not know, until I went to college and started taking architecture-history courses, that she was actually a relatively prominent architect. When I asked her about this in the late 1990s, she told me that 'the buildings either spoke for themselves or they did not, and either way there was nothing for me to say about them.' That was the entire conversation."

### Appendix J — A Final Note

The author of this essay learned of Vance's work in 1996, as a young associate at Goody, Clancy & Associates in Boston, when an older colleague — the late Patrick O'Donohue-Caldwell — pointed out to me, during a site visit to a project on Newbury Street, that the small office building across the street had been designed by "a woman named Vance, who is more or less forgotten now but who built half of Boston's office space in the 1960s and never received the recognition she deserved." That casual observation, delivered between site-visit punch-list items, was the beginning of my own twenty-year interest in Vance's work. I am grateful to Patrick, who died in 2018, for the observation, and grateful to the Vance estate and to Karen Vance Borkowski for permitting me the privilege of producing this essay.
