### Overview

Eleanor M. Vance, MD, is a Professor of Pediatrics at Harvard Medical School and a senior attending physician in the Division of Pediatric Pulmonology at MassGeneral Hospital for Children, where she has practiced since completing her clinical fellowship in 1998. Dr. Vance directs the hospital's Severe Asthma Program and is the principal investigator of the NHLBI-funded Boston Pediatric Airway Cohort study (R01 HL142718-05).

She is best known for her clinical and translational work on pediatric severe asthma phenotypes, with particular emphasis on the role of mucin hypersecretion in steroid-resistant disease. Her 2017 first-author paper in *The New England Journal of Medicine* (377:2421–2433), reporting results of the CHILDHOOD-MUC5AC trial, has been cited more than 1,800 times and is widely credited with reshaping how academic pediatric pulmonologists conceptualize the airway-remodeling pathway in school-age severe asthma.

### Clinical Practice

Dr. Vance's clinical practice is based in the Yawkey Center for Outpatient Care, where she sees approximately 35–40 patients per week in the Pediatric Pulmonology clinic. Her referral practice draws heavily from the New England region and includes a substantial number of out-of-region tertiary referrals for management of severe, treatment-refractory asthma in school-age children and adolescents. She is the lead biologic-therapy prescriber for the Severe Asthma Program and was one of the first U.S. pediatric pulmonologists to prescribe dupilumab off-label for pediatric severe asthma in 2018, prior to the medication's pediatric label expansion.

She attends on the inpatient pediatric pulmonology service for approximately twelve weeks per year and serves as the consulting attending for the pediatric ICU step-down respiratory service.

### Research Program

Dr. Vance's research program is organized around three principal lines of inquiry:

#### 1. Mucin Biology in Pediatric Severe Asthma

Dr. Vance's laboratory, in collaboration with the Fahy laboratory at UCSF, has demonstrated that elevated MUC5AC concentrations in induced sputum are strongly predictive of poor inhaled-corticosteroid response in school-age children with severe asthma. The CHILDHOOD-MUC5AC trial (NEJM 2017; ClinicalTrials.gov NCT02141823) established sputum MUC5AC as a candidate biomarker for steroid-refractory pediatric asthma, and the follow-up CHILDHOOD-MUC5AC-2 longitudinal cohort (currently in year four of follow-up) is examining the relationship between baseline sputum MUC5AC and longitudinal lung-function trajectories through adolescence.

#### 2. The Boston Pediatric Airway Cohort

Dr. Vance is the principal investigator of the Boston Pediatric Airway Cohort, a prospective longitudinal study that has enrolled 1,247 children with physician-diagnosed asthma between the ages of 6 and 11 from across the greater Boston metropolitan area. The cohort, now in its eleventh year of follow-up, has produced more than 40 peer-reviewed publications and has served as the platform for three NIH-funded ancillary studies, including a 2021 sub-study of nasal microbiome composition led by co-investigator Dr. Pranav Subramanian.

#### 3. Health Disparities in Pediatric Asthma Outcomes

Beginning in approximately 2014, Dr. Vance has devoted an increasing fraction of her research effort to questions of pediatric asthma disparities. In collaboration with Dr. Inez Calderón of the Boston Children's Hospital Community Asthma Initiative, she has published a series of papers documenting differential biologic-therapy access among Medicaid-insured pediatric asthma patients in Massachusetts and a 2019 paper in *Pediatrics* reporting marked geographic variation in pediatric asthma ED utilization within the city of Boston (*Pediatrics* 2019;143(4):e20183114).

### Teaching

Dr. Vance is a former director of the Pediatric Pulmonology Fellowship Training Program (2014–2021) and continues to direct the program's clinical research curriculum. She lectures annually in the Harvard Medical School second-year integrated respiratory pathophysiology course (HMS HST.040) and serves as a small-group tutor in the first-year doctoring course. She has mentored 17 pediatric pulmonology fellows to independent academic careers, eight of whom currently hold tenure-track or tenured positions at U.S. academic medical centers.

In 2019 she received the Charles A. Janeway Mentorship Award from the Harvard Department of Pediatrics, which was given that year to recognize sustained mentorship of women and underrepresented-minority trainees.

### Selected Quotations

"What we are trying to do, fundamentally, is to bring the same kind of biomarker-driven precision to pediatric severe asthma that the oncology community has brought to childhood cancers over the last twenty-five years. We are not where they are. But we are getting closer." — Dr. Vance, in an interview with the *Boston Globe* for a feature on pediatric asthma research, March 14, 2022.

"There is no version of this story where the children of the South End and Roxbury should have asthma outcomes that look as different as they do from the outcomes of children five miles away.
