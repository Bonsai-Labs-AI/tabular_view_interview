## Vance to Chair Brazil–Europe Gas Infrastructure Arbitration

*ICC tribunal to hear $2 billion-plus dispute over offshore capacity allocation*

**By Imogen Hartwell-Beauclerc** | Reporter, GAR | 6 March 2024

---

The Columbia Law professor-emerita and full-time arbitrator **Eleanor Vance** has been confirmed as chair of a three-member ICC tribunal that will hear a multi-billion-dollar dispute between a Brazilian state-controlled energy company and a consortium of European pipeline operators over offshore gas-infrastructure capacity, GAR has learned.

Two persons with direct knowledge of the constitution of the tribunal said the appointment had been made in late February and formally confirmed by the ICC Court last week. The proceeding, which is seated in Paris and governed by Swiss substantive law as a matter of the underlying contract, arises out of a 2014 joint operating agreement covering offshore gas infrastructure off the south-eastern Brazilian coast. The amount in dispute is understood to exceed US $2 billion.

The claimants, identified by the same sources as the European consortium members, allege that capacity-allocation decisions taken by the Brazilian operator between 2020 and 2022 contravened the preferential-access provisions of the JOA. The respondent denies the allegations and is expected to advance a number of counterclaims of its own.

### Counsel

Counsel for the claimant consortium are understood to be a team from **Three Crowns LLP**, while the respondent is understood to be represented by **Freshfields Bruckhaus Deringer**. Neither firm responded to GAR's request for comment, citing the confidentiality of the proceeding.

### The Chair

Professor Vance, 59, is among a relatively small group of senior U.S. legal academics to have made the full transition from academic life to a substantial independent arbitration practice. She took emerita status at Columbia Law School in 2022 having joined that school's faculty in 2001 (after a five-year period at Georgetown Law), and she was appointed to the Charles Evans Hughes Professorship of Law at Columbia in 2009. She practises from a chambers on Park Avenue in Midtown Manhattan.

A Rhodes Scholar who took her first degree in law at Oxford in 1988, Professor Vance went on to earn her JD from Yale in 1991. She thereafter clerked for the late Judge Patricia Wald of the United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit before entering the academy. Her co-authored treatise *The Law and Practice of Commercial Arbitration* — written with Professor Robert Kaminski of NYU Law — is in its third edition and is widely assigned in both American and English LL.M. arbitration courses.

Speaking to GAR, Professor Kaminski described his co-author and longtime collaborator as "exceptionally well suited" to a complex, high-value commercial chair. "Eleanor combines doctrinal seriousness with procedural discipline in a way that is genuinely rare," he said. "She is willing to write long, fully reasoned awards — which is increasingly what parties want when they know that an annulment challenge is likely to follow."

### Recent Appointments

Professor Vance has chaired or sat as sole arbitrator in upwards of eighty commercial matters over the last decade. Among her recent publicly reported chair appointments are:

- A US $1.4 billion ICC-administered post-acquisition indemnification dispute, in which she issued a partial final award on liability against the seller-respondents in 2020;
- An LCIA-administered reinsurance dispute seated in London, in which she sat as chair.

She has also been active in institutional governance. She served on the **ICC Commission on Arbitration and ADR** between 2015 and 2021 and presently sits on the board of the **New York International Arbitration Center**.

### The Procedural Calendar

Persons close to the proceeding told GAR that the tribunal is expected to convene a first procedural conference later in the spring, with an evidentiary hearing now provisionally scheduled for early 2025. A final award is unlikely to issue before the end of 2025 at the very earliest, and persons familiar with similar matters in the ICC's energy-sector caseload suggested that an award before mid-2026 would be regarded as comparatively quick.

### Context: Brazilian Offshore Gas Disputes

The new proceeding adds to a growing portfolio of high-value commercial arbitrations arising from the post-2010 expansion of Brazilian offshore gas infrastructure. Practitioners contacted by GAR observed that capacity-allocation provisions in pre-2015 JOAs covering Brazilian offshore assets were drafted at a time when the market structure and the regulatory environment looked quite different from how they look now, and that disputes of the type now before Professor Vance's tribunal were broadly foreseeable.

"You had a generation of contracts written for a world that was about to change very substantially," one London-based partner told GAR. "It is genuinely unsurprising that we are now seeing the disputes that those contracts were always going to produce."

### Why It Matters

The matter is the latest in a series of high-value commercial arbitrations in which a U.S. academic-turned-arbitrator has been appointed as chair of an ICC tribunal seated outside the United States. Some practitioners regard this as reflective of a broader internationalisation of the chair-arbitrator market, in which institutional appointments are increasingly being made on the basis of subject-matter expertise and procedural reputation rather than national affiliation.

"Five or ten years ago, you might have expected this kind of chair appointment to go to a continental European arbitrator with an energy-sector background," one Paris-based partner told GAR. "What you are seeing now is a market in which the best chairs are appointed on substantive grounds, and Eleanor Vance is a very strong substantive choice for this kind of matter."

Professor Vance was reached by email but, as is her usual practice in respect of pending matters, declined to comment.

The ICC International Court of Arbitration did not respond to a request for comment, in line with its practice of declining to confirm or deny the existence or particulars of pending proceedings.

### Sidebar: Vance's Recent ICC Practice

Professor Vance's recent reported chair and co-arbitrator appointments at the ICC include the following matters, each of which has been publicly reported in at least one of the major arbitration trade publications:

- A 2020 partial final award in a $1.4 billion ICC-administered post-acquisition indemnification dispute, in which Professor Vance — sitting as chair — issued a partial final award on liability against the seller-respondents. The award is understood by GAR's sources to have been the subject of a subsequent annulment challenge, which was unsuccessful.
- An LCIA-administered reinsurance dispute seated in London, in which Professor Vance sat as chair. The proceeding was settled in 2022 following a partial final award on a coverage issue.
- A 2019 ICC-administered shareholder dispute concerning a Spanish renewables-developer joint venture, in which Professor Vance sat as a party-appointed co-arbitrator.
- A 2018 ICC-administered commodity-supply dispute concerning a long-term LNG supply agreement, in which Professor Vance sat as chair.

GAR understands that Professor Vance is also currently sitting as co-arbitrator in a confidential SCC matter and as chair of a confidential UNCITRAL ad hoc proceeding administered by the PCA. These matters have not been publicly reported and are mentioned here only at a high level of generality.

### Sidebar: The Three Crowns / Freshfields Match-Up

The two principal counsel teams in the new ICC proceeding — Three Crowns LLP for the European consortium and Freshfields Bruckhaus Deringer for the Brazilian state-owned operator — represent a familiar pairing in major ICC energy-sector matters. The two firms have appeared opposite one another in at least seven publicly reported major ICC energy matters since 2018, with broadly comparable outcomes across the series. The new proceeding adds a further significant matter to that series and is expected to feature, on Three Crowns's side, a team led by partners Camille Forrester-DuBois (Paris) and Hendrik van Doorslaer-Reinhart (London), and on Freshfields's side, a team led by partner Constance Bellechasse-Maillard (Paris) and counsel Edouard Vasseur-Beaumont (London). None of the firms has confirmed the composition of the respective teams, however, and GAR cautions readers that the composition may shift before the proceeding's first procedural conference.

### Sidebar: Annulment Risk and the Reasoned Award

A theme that recurs in practitioner discussion of major ICC energy-sector chair appointments is the question of annulment risk, and in particular the question of whether the chair is prepared to write a long, fully reasoned award that will survive a Paris-seat annulment challenge under the French Code of Civil Procedure or, where relevant, an enforcement challenge under the New York Convention.

Professor Vance's reputation in this respect is that of an arbitrator who is willing to write — and indeed insists on writing — substantially longer and more fully reasoned awards than the institutional median. Her best-known award, the 2020 partial final award in the post-acquisition indemnification matter referred to above, is understood to have run to 412 pages in length, including detailed treatments of each of the principal contractual interpretation issues, of the parties' factual and expert evidence, and of the governing law. GAR's sources for the present brief told us that this approach is one of the qualities that the parties' counsel were understood to value when they were in a position to confirm or to oppose her appointment as chair.

It is worth noting in this connection that the underlying contract in the present proceeding — the 2014 joint operating agreement — is governed by Swiss substantive law as a matter of contract, while the seat of arbitration is Paris. Annulment of any final award would therefore be sought, if at all, in the Paris Court of Appeal under French annulment law; enforcement of the award (likely in Brazil, in France, and in one or more European jurisdictions where the consortium members are domiciled) would be governed by the New York Convention. The interaction between the Swiss substantive law applicable to the merits and the French procedural law applicable to the annulment proceeding is a matter to which both Professor Vance and her co-arbitrators (when their identities are confirmed) will need to attend with some care.

### Sidebar: Professor Vance's Park Avenue Chambers

Professor Vance practises from chambers at a Park Avenue address in Midtown Manhattan. The chambers, which she opened in 2022 following her transition to emerita status at Columbia Law School, are understood to comprise three full-time professional staff — a chief of chambers (Ms Pilar Echevarría-Hutchings), a junior research associate, and an administrative assistant — and to accept appointments principally in commercial, energy, M&A, and reinsurance disputes. Professor Vance is understood to maintain an active practice of approximately fifteen to twenty active matters at any one time, with a chair-to-co-arbitrator ratio of approximately three to one. Her standard arbitrator's hourly rate is, on information and belief, in the upper band of the U.S. independent-arbitrator market.

The chambers' website (which is minimal, as is typical of independent arbitrators' practice in the U.S. market) lists the institutional appointments at which Professor Vance is available to sit; these include the ICC, the LCIA, the SCC, the ICDR, the SIAC, the PCA (in UNCITRAL ad hoc matters), and the AAA. She is also available to sit in ICSID and ICSID Additional Facility matters.

### Sidebar: The Wider Context of Brazilian State-Owned Energy Disputes

Brazilian state-owned energy companies have, since approximately 2018, become substantially more active in international commercial arbitration than they were in the preceding decade. The reasons for this shift are multiple and include: (a) the maturation of a generation of long-term contracts entered into during the 2010s commodity boom; (b) the substantial regulatory reforms to the Brazilian oil-and-gas sector that followed the conclusion of the so-called Lava Jato investigations; (c) the recalibration of capacity-allocation and offtake arrangements following the entry of new private-sector participants into the Brazilian upstream and midstream markets; and (d) the broader global shift in energy markets following the 2022 Russian invasion of Ukraine and the associated disruption to European gas supply.

The combined effect of these developments has been to put substantial pressure on the commercial arrangements that govern the relationship between Brazilian state-owned operators and their European, North American, and Asian counterparties. Many of those arrangements — including the 2014 JOA that underlies the new proceeding — were entered into in a market environment that no longer exists, and disputes over the interpretation and application of those arrangements have, perhaps unsurprisingly, multiplied.

GAR's *Brazilian Arbitration Watch* tracker, which is maintained by the GAR research team and updated quarterly, identified twenty-three publicly reported commercial-arbitration proceedings involving Brazilian state-owned energy companies as of the end of 2023, compared with eleven as of the end of 2020 and four as of the end of 2017. The new proceeding involving Professor Vance is the largest of those twenty-three matters by reported amount in dispute.

### Sidebar: The Park Avenue Independent Arbitrator Market

The expansion of the U.S. independent-arbitrator market — and in particular the emergence of a substantial cohort of senior academic-turned-arbitrator practitioners practising from Manhattan and Washington chambers — has been one of the more interesting structural developments in the U.S. arbitration profession over the past decade.

The trend reflects, in part, the maturation of the U.S. arbitration bar, the growth of full-service institutional arbitration in New York (centered on the New York International Arbitration Center and the ICDR), and the recognition by parties and counsel that the chair appointment market is increasingly internationalised. It also reflects, more specifically, the emergence of a "second career" model for distinguished legal academics who have completed their primary academic careers and who wish to bring their substantive expertise to bear on commercial dispute resolution.

Professor Vance is among the most prominent of this cohort. Other senior U.S. academics with substantial full-time arbitration practices include — without limitation — Professor Robert Kaminski of NYU Law (Professor Vance's longtime treatise co-author), Professor Catherine M. Whitmer of Harvard Law (now of counsel at her own chambers in Cambridge), and Professor Theodore Bockstein of the University of Chicago (who maintains chambers on Wacker Drive). The cohort as a whole is characterised by a willingness to write substantially longer awards than the institutional median, a strong subject-matter focus, and a comparatively high acceptance rate of chair (as opposed to party-appointed co-arbitrator) appointments.

The structure of the Park Avenue chambers model — small staff, high billing rate, intensive matter-by-matter engagement — is comparatively unfamiliar to the European arbitration market, which is dominated by partners in major commercial law firms who maintain arbitration practices as one element of a broader transactional and dispute-resolution practice. The two models are, in many respects, in productive competition, and the new ICC proceeding chaired by Professor Vance — in which the parties are represented by major European-headquartered law firms but the chair sits independently from her own chambers — is a characteristic illustration of how the two models can interact in a single major matter.

### Sidebar: The 2014 JOA Generation of Disputes

The JOA that underlies the new ICC proceeding was signed in 2014, a year that marked, in retrospect, an inflection point in the global energy market. The substantial expansion of U.S. shale production over the preceding several years had begun to put downward pressure on global oil and gas prices, and 2014 closed with the first major commodity-price correction of the post-2008 cycle.

Many of the joint operating agreements signed in 2014 — including the agreement that underlies the new proceeding — were therefore drafted in a market environment that was about to undergo substantial change. The capacity-allocation provisions of those agreements, in particular, were drafted on assumptions about future production volumes and future capacity utilisation that the subsequent decade did not validate.

The result has been a substantial wave of disputes — now beginning to materialise as formal proceedings — concerning the interpretation and application of those provisions. Practitioners on both the claimant and respondent sides told GAR that the new ICC proceeding is likely to be one of perhaps five or six "generational" matters in this category over the next several years, each likely to involve amounts in dispute in the range of US $1 billion to US $3 billion and each likely to be of substantial precedential significance for the interpretation of similar provisions in the broader JOA universe.
