# The Boston Globe — Obituaries

## Eleanor Vance, Folk Singer Who Anchored the Cambridge Coffeehouse Scene, Dies at 78

**By Harriet Donnelly** | Globe Staff | November 14, 2019

Eleanor "Ellie" Vance, the plainspoken contralto whose finger-picked ballads and acerbic between-song patter made her a fixture of the Cambridge and Western Massachusetts folk circuit for more than four decades, died Tuesday at her home in Shelburne Falls. She was 78.

The cause was complications from pancreatic cancer, said her longtime partner, the painter Margery Holst.

Though Ms. Vance never crossed over to mainstream commercial success, she was, in the words of the folklorist Bertram Lasswell, "the conscience of the Pioneer Valley circuit — the one the younger singers all wanted to please." She recorded six albums for Folkways and its successor labels between 1968 and 1994, and a seventh, the live retrospective *Iron Bridge Sessions*, was released in 2011 by Smithsonian Folkways.

### Early Life

Eleanor Margaret Vance was born on March 3, 1941, in Lowell, Mass., to Thomas Vance, a stationary engineer at the Boott Cotton Mills, and the former Catherine Doolan, a seamstress. The youngest of five children in a working-class Irish-American household, she grew up singing in the choir at the Immaculate Conception parish and, by her own account, "stealing every Carter Family record I could find" from a cousin who worked at a record store on Merrimack Street.

She enrolled at the University of Massachusetts in Amherst in 1959, intending to study elementary education, but dropped out after two years to follow the folk revival to Cambridge. By 1962 she was a regular at the Club 47 on Mt. Auburn Street, where she shared bills with the young Tom Rush, Eric von Schmidt, and a then-unknown Joan Baez. She supported herself by waiting tables at the Pamplona on Bow Street.

### The Folkways Years

Ms. Vance's first record, *Songs of the Merrimack* (Folkways FA 2419, 1968), was produced by Moses Asch on a budget of $340 and recorded over a single weekend at a church basement in Watertown. It collected traditional New England mill ballads, several of which she had transcribed from her great-aunt Bridget. The album sold poorly on release but became an underground reference for the early-1970s folk-revival second wave; tracks from it were later sampled by the indie band The Decemberists on their 2006 album *The Crane Wife*.

Her second album, *Hard Coal and Cold Coffee* (Folkways FA 2627, 1971), is generally considered her masterpiece. It contains her best-known original composition, "The Lawrence Strike," a seven-minute narrative ballad about the 1912 Bread and Roses strike that has been covered by Pete Seeger, Si Kahn, and most recently Rhiannon Giddens.

She recorded four more studio albums for Folkways and its successors:

- *Iron Bridge* (1976)
- *Granite and Spruce* (1981)
- *The Long Counties* (1987)
- *Late Songs* (1994)

A planned 1998 album titled *The Quabbin Sessions*, recorded with the fiddler Bruce Molsky, was shelved after a dispute with the label and remains unreleased.

### Coffeehouse Circuit

Ms. Vance was, by temperament and by economic necessity, a touring musician. From the early 1970s through the late 2000s she played a steady rotation of small rooms across New England: the Iron Horse in Northampton, Passim in Cambridge, the Vanilla Bean in Pomfret, Conn., the Towne Crier in Pawling, N.Y., and the Linda Norris Auditorium in Albany. She estimated, in a 2003 interview with *Sing Out!* magazine, that she had played the Iron Horse "something like two hundred and forty nights" over the course of her career.

She was famously suspicious of festival bookings and turned down Newport three times — in 1979, 1982, and 1991 — saying she did not like to perform "for people eating sandwiches on a hillside." She did, however, headline the Falcon Ridge Folk Festival's Most Wanted stage in 1994 and 2001.

Her between-song banter — dry, political, and sometimes withering toward the audience — was the subject of a 1985 *Boston Phoenix* profile titled "The Last Honest Woman in the Folk Room." She once stopped a song mid-verse at the Club Passim to inform a chatting couple at a front table that "the cover charge does not include a license to narrate."

### Teaching and Activism

Ms. Vance was a longtime artist-in-residence at the Ashfield Community Music School, where she taught Carter-style guitar to two generations of Pioneer Valley children. She also led an annual workshop on traditional ballad interpretation at the Augusta Heritage Center in Elkins, W.Va., from 1988 until 2014.

She was an early and vocal opponent of the Northfield Mountain Pumped Storage Project and performed at numerous benefits for the Connecticut River Watershed Council. In 1989 she was arrested at a Seabrook Station protest along with Pete Seeger and Arlo Guthrie, charges that were later dismissed.

### Personal Life

Ms. Vance lived for the last thirty-two years of her life in a converted dairy barn outside Shelburne Falls with Ms. Holst, whom she married in a private ceremony in 2004 and again, legally, in 2014. She kept goats, an irascible donkey named Brendan, and a vegetable garden that she described as "more philosophy than agriculture."

In addition to Ms. Holst, she is survived by a sister, Mary Vance Kovalsky of Dracut; two nephews; and four grandnephews. A brother, Patrick, died in 1998.

### Legacy

A tribute concert featuring Tom Rush, Patty Larkin, and Susan Werner is being planned for the spring at the Shea Theater in Turners Falls. In lieu of flowers, the family has requested donations to the Smithsonian Folkways Recordings preservation fund or to the Hilltown Land Trust.

"She was not interested in fame and she was not interested in money," Mr. Rush said by telephone Wednesday. "She was interested in the songs. That is a rarer thing than people realize."

A private burial will be held at the Hillside Cemetery in Shelburne. A public memorial gathering will be announced at a later date.

### Critical Reception, Selected

Critical attention to Ms. Vance's work came in waves. The first wave, in the late 1960s and early 1970s, was driven by the Folkways imprint's connection to the Cambridge revival scene; *Sing Out!* and *Broadside* both ran appreciative reviews of *Songs of the Merrimack* in 1968 and 1969, and a young Robert Christgau gave *Hard Coal and Cold Coffee* a B-plus in the *Village Voice* in 1972, calling it "a record by a singer who has clearly read more than she has performed, and is the better for it."

The second wave of attention came in the late 1980s, when a generation of younger Boston-area songwriters — among them Patty Larkin, Cliff Eberhardt, and Greg Brown — publicly cited Ms. Vance as an influence and brought her name to a national singer-songwriter audience that had largely missed her in the first instance. This period saw her booked for the first time at the Philadelphia Folk Festival (1989), the Vancouver Folk Music Festival (1991), and the Winnipeg Folk Festival (1993).

A third, smaller wave of attention attended the 2011 release of *Iron Bridge Sessions*, which collected previously unreleased live recordings from a 1979 residency at the Iron Horse in Northampton. The album received a four-star review in *MOJO* (vol. 217, December 2011) and a notable rediscovery essay in *The Oxford American* (Issue 76, Spring 2012) by the music writer Amanda Petrusich.

### A Note on the Family

The Vance family of Lowell should not be confused with the unrelated Vance family of Marblehead (descendants of the merchant captain Josiah Vance), nor with the better-known Boston-Brahmin Vance line, of which the late Secretary of State Cyrus Vance was the most prominent twentieth-century member. The Lowell Vances were comparatively modest mill-town Catholics; Thomas Vance, the singer's father, never earned more than $4,200 in a single year, and the family lived at 134 Aiken Avenue for the entirety of Ms. Vance's childhood.

The singer often spoke, on stage and off, of her great-aunt Bridget Doolan (1879–1963), an immigrant from County Clare who worked at the Boott Mills from 1893 until her retirement in 1944. It was Bridget Doolan from whom Ms. Vance learned the bulk of her repertoire of traditional mill ballads, and it is Bridget Doolan to whom *Songs of the Merrimack* is dedicated.

### A Final Note from the Obituary Editor

Among the many tributes received by the *Globe* in the days following Ms. Vance's death was a handwritten note from the singer Suzanne Vega, who had played opening for Ms. Vance at Passim in 1983 and who wrote, in part: "She told me, after the second set, that the only thing that mattered was the song. She did not mean it as advice. She meant it as a description of how the world was. I have thought of that every working day since."

A second note, from the political-folk singer Si Kahn, observed that "Ellie Vance was the last serious topical songwriter of her generation who had actually been on the picket lines she sang about. She earned every word of every verse, and there will not be another one of her in any of our lifetimes."

### Memorial Service Details

A public memorial gathering will be held at the Shea Theater in Turners Falls on Saturday, March 14, 2020, at 2:00 p.m. The gathering will be open to the public; no tickets are required. The Vance family has asked that those wishing to perform at the gathering contact the Shea's artistic director, Mr. Gerard O'Sullivan-Boyle, in advance.

A separate, smaller gathering of family and close friends will be held at the Mohawk Trail Concerts grounds in Charlemont on Sunday, March 15. This gathering is by invitation only.

The Smithsonian Folkways Recordings is in the process of preparing a memorial reissue of *Songs of the Merrimack* and *Hard Coal and Cold Coffee* on a single double-LP, with extensive new liner notes by the folklorist Bertram Lasswell. The reissue is expected in the fall of 2020.

The Vance family has also indicated that a posthumous album, drawn from previously unreleased home recordings made by Ms. Vance between 2010 and 2018, is in preparation. The album, tentatively titled *The Late Sessions*, is expected to be released by Smithsonian Folkways in 2021 under the supervision of the producer Jeff Place.

### A Final Personal Note

In her last public performance — at the Iron Horse on October 12, 2019, six weeks before her death — Ms. Vance closed the evening with a song she had written in the months following her diagnosis. The song, titled "Letter to the Next Person Who Sings This," has not been commercially released and is not believed to have been recorded except informally by audience members. The chorus, as transcribed by an audience member who was present and as confirmed by Ms. Holst, runs:

> *Tell them I sang for as long as the songs let me sing.*
> *Tell them I sang for the others, and then for myself, and then for the others again.*
> *Tell them the river is wider than they were ever told.*
> *Tell them the river is wider than they will ever know.*

The audience that night, by all accounts, sat in silence for a full thirty seconds after the last chord before applauding. Several were in tears. The Iron Horse's owner, Mr. Eric Suher, told the *Daily Hampshire Gazette* afterwards that "in twenty-five years of running rooms in this valley, I have never been in an audience that knew it was watching something end."

This obituary is the longer of two prepared by *The Boston Globe* for publication in the event of Ms. Vance's death; the shorter, prepared in 1999 by the late Margo Howe-Donovan, is preserved in the *Globe* obituary morgue and was not used.

Ms. Vance was, by her own request, buried in a simple pine coffin built by her neighbor, the cabinetmaker Asa Pelham-Crick, in a plot at Hillside Cemetery purchased by Ms. Holst in 2017. Her grave is marked by a single fieldstone, on which is incised the title of her best-known composition: "The Lawrence Strike."

### Personal Correspondence Bequest

Ms. Vance's personal correspondence — comprising approximately 18 linear feet of letters, postcards, and handwritten notes received over the course of her career — was bequeathed to the Folk Music Archive at the John Edwards Memorial Foundation at the University of California, Los Angeles. The correspondence includes substantial exchanges with, among others, the late Pete Seeger, Anne Hills, Si Kahn, Greg Brown, Patty Larkin, Suzanne Vega, and the playwright Tony Kushner.

The Foundation has indicated that the Vance correspondence will be open to researchers under standard access protocols beginning in 2025, subject to the usual restrictions on personal correspondence involving living individuals.

### A Note from the Folk Music Community

The folk-music journal *Sing Out!* will dedicate the entirety of its Winter 2020 issue to a tribute to Ms. Vance, including memorial essays by, among others, the songwriter and historian John McCutcheon, the singer Susan Werner, the radio producer Nick Spitzer, and the folklorist Bertram Lasswell. The issue will be guest-edited by the writer and broadcaster Scott Alarik and is expected to include previously unpublished photographs from the Vance family archive.

The Folk Alliance International organization has announced that the 2020 Folk Alliance International annual conference, to be held in New Orleans in January 2020, will dedicate its memorial set to Ms. Vance. The memorial set is expected to feature performances by Patty Larkin, Tom Rush, Susan Werner, Greg Brown, Mary Chapin Carpenter, and Cliff Eberhardt, among others, and will be broadcast live on the WWOZ-FM and on the Folk Alliance International streaming service.

The Smithsonian Folkways imprint has also announced that the proceeds of all sales of Ms. Vance's catalog through the Smithsonian Folkways online store during the calendar year 2020 will be donated to the Hilltown Land Trust in Cummington, Mass., per Ms. Vance's testamentary wishes.

### A Final Tribute, from Tom Rush

Tom Rush, who first met Ms. Vance at the Club 47 in early 1963 and who shared the bill with her on countless evenings over the subsequent five and a half decades, offered the following remembrance to the *Globe* in a brief telephone conversation last evening:

"I have been thinking, since I learned the news on Wednesday morning, about the first time I heard Ellie sing. I cannot recall the exact date — it would have been some Tuesday or Wednesday at the Club 47, sometime in the spring of 1963 — but I remember that she sang 'The Lawrence Strike,' which was at that time only about eight months old, and I remember that the room went absolutely silent during the third verse, the verse about the children at the mill gate, and I remember thinking to myself that I was hearing a songwriter who was going to outlast all of us.

"That turned out to be both true and not true. She outlasted most of the songwriters of our generation in a certain sense — she kept writing right up to the end, and the late songs are some of the best songs she ever wrote — but she did not outlast us in the way the world keeps score, because she did not become famous, and she did not become wealthy, and she did not get the Kennedy Center Honors or the Library of Congress concert series or any of the other markers of cultural significance that go to the songwriters the rest of the country remembers.

"But she will outlast us in the only way that finally matters, which is that the songs are still going to be sung. The songs are good enough that the songs are going to be sung. And that is the only kind of immortality that I think Ellie ever cared about, or ever wanted."
